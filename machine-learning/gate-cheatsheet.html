1 Data notation

Suppose a dataset contains $m$ observations and $n$ features. Then:

$$X\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\qquad y\in\mathbb{R}^m$$

Rows $=$ observations, columns $=$ features. For observation $i$: $x_i=$ input, $y_i=$ actual target, and the model's prediction is $\hat y_i$ (read "y-hat").

TermMeaning
FeatureAn input used for prediction
Label / targetThe correct answer associated with an input
ImputationFilling missing values via mean, median, mode, or a model-based prediction
CorrelationTwo variables move together
CausationA change in one variable actually causes a change in another
⚠️ Trap

Correlation does not imply causation.

4 Linear regression

4.1 Simple linear regression — predicts a numerical value from one feature:

$$\hat y=\beta_0+\beta_1x$$

$\beta_0=$ intercept (predicted value when $x=0$); $\beta_1=$ slope (change in predicted $y$ when $x$ increases by 1); $x=$ input; $\hat y=$ prediction. Example: $\text{marks}=20+8(\text{study hours})$ — one extra study hour increases predicted marks by 8.

4.2 Multiple linear regression:

$$\hat y=\beta_0+\beta_1x_1+\beta_2x_2+\cdots+\beta_px_p\qquad\text{matrix form: }\hat y=X\beta$$

If the model contains an intercept, the first column of $X$ is all ones. For $n$ observations and $p$ predictors, $X:n\times(p+1)$ — the "$+1$" is the intercept column.

5 Regression with intercept versus without intercept

A frequent GATE trap.

5.1 With an intercept — model $y=b+wx$:

$$w=\frac{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2}\qquad b=\bar y-w\bar x$$

The least-squares line passes through $(\bar x,\bar y)$.

5.2 Without an intercept — model $y=wx$, the line is forced through the origin:

$$w=\frac{\sum_ix_iy_i}{\sum_ix_i^2}$$
Model written in questionFormula
$y=wx$$\sum xy/\sum x^2$
$y=b+wx$Centred slope plus intercept
⚠️ Trap

Never use the two formulas interchangeably.

6 Residuals
$$e_i=y_i-\hat y_i$$

If $e_i\gt0$, the model predicted too low. If $e_i\lt0$, the model predicted too high.

7 Error measures

7.1 Mean Absolute Error — use when the question says "absolute error," "MAE," or "less sensitivity to large errors":

$$MAE=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n|y_i-\hat y_i|$$

7.2 Sum of Squared Errors (also called RSS):

$$SSE=RSS=\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i-\hat y_i)^2$$

7.3 Mean & Root Mean Squared Error — RMSE has the same unit as the target:

$$MSE=\frac{SSE}n\qquad RMSE=\sqrt{MSE}$$
🧠 Error-calculation pipeline
  • $y-\hat y\to$ square $\to$ sum $\to$ divide by $n$ $\to$ square root if RMSE.
🎯 Why square errors?

Errors $+5,-5$ add directly to zero — hiding both mistakes. Squaring gives $5^2+(-5)^2=50$: it prevents cancellation and penalizes large errors heavily. A residual of 20 contributes $20^2=400$, while a residual of 2 contributes only $2^2=4$ — therefore OLS is sensitive to outliers.

8 SST, SSR, SSE and $R^2$

Total target variation splits as:

$$SST=SSR+SSE$$

$SST=\sum_i(y_i-\bar y)^2$ (total variation); $SSR=\sum_i(\hat y_i-\bar y)^2$ (variation explained by the model); $SSE=\sum_i(y_i-\hat y_i)^2$ (variation not explained).

$$R^2=\frac{SSR}{SST}=1-\frac{SSE}{SST}\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad SSE=SST(1-R^2)$$

$R^2=0.80$ means the model explains 80% of the variation in the target.

GivenUse
SST and SSR given$R^2=SSR/SST$
SST and $R^2$ given$SSE=SST(1-R^2)$
RMSE requested from $R^2$$R^2\to SSE\to MSE\to RMSE$
9 Ordinary Least Squares

OLS chooses coefficients that minimize squared residuals:

$$\min_\beta\sum_i(y_i-\hat y_i)^2\qquad\text{matrix form: }\min_\beta\|y-X\beta\|_2^2$$

The normal equation:

$$X^TX\hat\beta=X^Ty\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad\hat\beta=(X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty\ \ \text{(if invertible)}$$
Closed form $(X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty$Gradient descent $w_{\text{new}}=w_{\text{old}}-\eta\nabla J(w)$
Small/medium feature spacesLarge datasets
Direct exact computationLarge feature spaces
—Iterative optimization
⚠️ Trap

A statement saying matrix inversion is always faster is false.

10 Multicollinearity

Perfect multicollinearity occurs when one feature is an exact linear combination of others, e.g. $x_3=2x_1-x_2$. Then $X^TX$ is singular.

ConsequenceDetail
Ordinary inverseDoes not exist
CoefficientsNot unique
PredictionsCan still be unique
$$\hat\beta=X^+y\quad\text{(pseudoinverse, when }X^TX\text{ is singular)}$$
⚠️ Trap

Non-unique coefficients $\ne$ impossible predictions.

Important assumptions: linear relationship; independent observations/errors; errors have mean approximately zero; constant error variance; no perfect multicollinearity.

12 Correlation
$$r_{XY}=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y}$$

Properties: $-1\le r\le1$; $r_{XY}=r_{YX}$. If $y=ax+b$ with no noise and $x$ not constant: $a\gt0\Rightarrow r=1$, $a\lt0\Rightarrow r=-1$.

⚠️ Trap

A correlation of zero means no linear relationship — a nonlinear relationship may still exist.

13 Gradient descent
$$w_{\text{new}}=w_{\text{old}}-\eta\nabla J(w)$$

$\eta=$ learning rate; $\nabla J=$ gradient. For squared-error regression: $\nabla J(w)=\dfrac2nX^T(Xw-y)$.

🧠 Memory
  • Gradient points uphill; subtract it to move downhill.
14 Regularization

Regularization discourages unnecessarily complex models.

$$\text{Total loss}=\text{Prediction loss}+\lambda(\text{Regularization penalty})$$
🧠 Solve in this order
  • Calculate prediction → calculate prediction loss → calculate penalty → add them.
15 Ridge regression
$$J(w)=L(w)+\lambda\|w\|_2^2\qquad\|w\|_2^2=w_1^2+w_2^2+\cdots+w_p^2$$

Ridge: shrinks coefficients; reduces variance; may increase bias; helps control overfitting; helps with multicollinearity; usually does not zero coefficients out; does not care about the sign of coefficients.

🧠 Memory
  • Ridge restrains weights.
⚠️ Convention warning

$L+\lambda\|w\|^2$ has penalty gradient $2\lambda w$; $L+\tfrac\lambda2\|w\|^2$ has penalty gradient $\lambda w$. Follow the exact formula in the question — if it gives you $w\leftarrow w-\eta(\nabla L+\lambda w)$, use that exact update; do not insert another factor of 2.

16 Lasso regression
$$J(w)=L(w)+\lambda\sum_j|w_j|$$

Lasso: can make coefficients exactly zero; performs feature selection; is convex; is not differentiable at zero.

$$\frac{d|w|}{dw}=\begin{cases}1,&w\gt0\\-1,&w\lt0\end{cases}\qquad\text{no unique ordinary derivative at }w=0$$

L2L1

🧠 Memory
  • Ridge $=$ L2, Lasso $=$ L1.
17 Bias and variance

Bias — error from a model being too restricted or simple; high bias usually means underfitting. Variance — sensitivity to changes in the training sample; high variance usually means overfitting.

UnderfittingOverfitting
High training error + high validation errorVery low training error + higher validation error
Model too simpleToo many parameters/features
Excessive regularizationToo little data, weak regularization
Very large $k$ in kNNVery small $k$ in kNN
🧠 Strong GATE signal
  • Training error decreases while validation error increases $\Rightarrow$ overfitting.
  • Regularization usually creates: bias $\uparrow$, variance $\downarrow$.
18 Logistic regression

Mainly used for classification. First calculate a linear score, then convert it to a probability:

$$z=\beta_0+\beta^Tx\qquad p=\sigma(z)=\frac1{1+e^{-z}}$$
19 Sigmoid facts
$$\sigma(0)=0.5\qquad z\to+\infty\Rightarrow p\to1\qquad z\to-\infty\Rightarrow p\to0$$

The sigmoid is strictly increasing, bounded between 0 and 1, and is the CDF of the standard logistic distribution — it is not itself the corresponding PDF.

20 Odds and log-odds
$$\text{Odds}=\frac{p}{1-p}\qquad\log\!\left(\frac{p}{1-p}\right)=\beta_0+\beta^Tx$$
🧠 Memory
  • Probability is curved; log-odds are linear.
21 Logistic decision boundary

Usually $p\ge0.5\Rightarrow$ class 1. Since $p=0.5\iff z=0$, the decision boundary is:

$$\beta_0+\beta^Tx=0\qquad\text{one feature: }x=-\frac{\beta_0}{\beta_1}$$
22 Sigmoid derivative
$$f(x)=\frac1{1+e^{-x}}\qquad f'(x)=f(x)[1-f(x)]$$

If the question directly gives $f(x)=p$, immediately use $f'(x)=p(1-p)$ — no need to calculate $x$. Example: $p=0.4\Rightarrow f'(x)=0.4(0.6)=0.24$.

22.1 With an input coefficient — if $p=\sigma(\beta_0+\beta_1x)$, then $\dfrac{dp}{dx}=\beta_1p(1-p)$.

22.2 Maximum sigmoid slope — $p(1-p)$ is maximum at $p=0.5$, i.e. $z=0$ (one feature: $x=-\beta_0/\beta_1$). The largest value of $p(1-p)$ is $0.25$.

23 Logistic likelihood

For binary labels $y_i\in\{0,1\}$ and $p_i=P(y_i=1)$:

$$L=\prod_ip_i^{y_i}(1-p_i)^{1-y_i}$$

True class 1 → contribute $p_i$. True class 0 → contribute $1-p_i$.

🧠 Golden rule
  • Multiply the probability assigned to each observation's true class.
24 Likelihood equivalences
$$\max L\iff\max\log L\iff\min(-\log L)$$

Binary negative log-likelihood is binary cross-entropy:

$$-\sum_i\big[y_i\log p_i+(1-y_i)\log(1-p_i)\big]$$
25 Softmax

For $K$ classes:

$$P(y=k)=\frac{e^{z_k}}{\sum_{j=1}^Ke^{z_j}}$$

Valid PMF because $P(y=k)\gt0$ and $\sum_{k=1}^KP(y=k)=1$.

🧠 Memory
  • Binary classification → sigmoid. Multiclass classification → softmax.
26 Confusion matrix
Predicted positivePredicted negative
Actual positiveTPFN
Actual negativeFPTN

TP: correctly detected positive. FN: missed positive. FP: false alarm. TN: correctly rejected negative.

🧠 Memory
  • The second letter tells you what the model predicted.
27 Classification metrics

27.1 Accuracy — of all observations, how many were classified correctly?

$$\text{Accuracy}=\frac{TP+TN}{TP+TN+FP+FN}$$

27.2 Precision — of everything predicted positive, how much was actually positive?

$$\text{Precision}=\frac{TP}{TP+FP}$$

27.3 Recall, sensitivity, TPR — of all actual positives, how many were found?

$$\text{Recall}=\text{Sensitivity}=\text{TPR}=\frac{TP}{TP+FN}$$

27.4 Specificity — of all actual negatives, how many were correctly rejected?

$$\text{Specificity}=\frac{TN}{TN+FP}$$

27.5 False Positive Rate:

$$FPR=\frac{FP}{FP+TN}=1-\text{Specificity}$$
🧠 Memory
  • Precision starts from predictions.
  • Recall starts from reality.
28 Class-wise precision and recall

When the question asks about "Class $C$," treat $C$ as positive:

$$\text{Precision}_C=\frac{\text{Correctly predicted as }C}{\text{Everything predicted as }C}\qquad\text{Recall}_C=\frac{\text{Correctly predicted as }C}{\text{Everything actually belonging to }C}$$
🧠 Memory
  • Precision → predicted-class column. Recall → actual-class row.
29 Balanced dataset and balanced accuracy

A balanced binary dataset has equal actual positives and negatives:

$$TP+FN=TN+FP$$
$$\text{Balanced Accuracy}=\frac{\text{Sensitivity}+\text{Specificity}}2$$

For a binary dataset with equal actual class counts: $\text{Accuracy}=\text{Balanced Accuracy}$ — but this equality is not guaranteed for an imbalanced dataset.

30 Prevalence and detection prevalence
$$\text{Prevalence}=\frac{TP+FN}N\qquad\text{Detection Prevalence}=\frac{TP+FP}N$$

They are not necessarily equal.

31 Cohen's Kappa

Kappa adjusts agreement for agreement expected by chance:

$$\kappa=\frac{p_o-p_e}{1-p_e}$$

$p_o=$ observed agreement; $p_e=$ expected chance agreement.

32 Perfect and always-positive classifiers

32.1 Perfect classifier — no mistakes: $FP=FN=0$. The confusion matrix is diagonal; all off-diagonal entries are zero; if each class appears at least once, all diagonal entries are non-zero.

32.2 Always predicts positive — then $FN=0$, $TN=0$, therefore:

$$\text{Sensitivity}=1\qquad\text{Specificity}=0$$
33 ROC curve
$$Y\text{-axis}=\text{TPR}=\text{Sensitivity}\qquad X\text{-axis}=\text{FPR}=1-\text{Specificity}$$

The decision threshold changes along the curve. Slope: $\dfrac{d(\text{TPR})}{d(\text{FPR})}$.

AUC — area under the ROC curve. $AUC=1$: perfect ranking. $AUC=0.5$: random ranking. Higher AUC is better.

34 Linear separability

Two classes are linearly separable if one straight line can put them on opposite sides:

$$w^Tx+b=0\qquad\text{classify by the sign of }w^Tx+b$$
Visual patternSeparable?
One class clearly above anotherUsually yes
One class surrounded by anotherNo
XOR pattern (diagonal alternation)No
Upper-left vs lower-right groupsUsually yes (diagonal line)
🎯 Convex-hull test

Imagine wrapping a rubber band around every point of one class — that enclosed shape is the class's convex hull. For strict linear separability, the class convex hulls must not intersect.

$$\text{Overlapping convex hulls}\Rightarrow\text{not linearly separable}$$
35 Nearest-centroid classification

A point is assigned to the closest class mean. With means $\mu_r,\mu_g$, define $f(x)=\|\mu_r-x\|^2-\|\mu_g-x\|^2$; if $f(x)\lt0$, the point is closer to $\mu_r$.

🎯 Difference-of-squared-distances trick

Expanding both squared distances, the $x^Tx$ terms cancel on subtraction:

$$f(x)=2(\mu_g-\mu_r)^Tx+\|\mu_r\|^2-\|\mu_g\|^2=w^Tx+b$$

where $w=2(\mu_g-\mu_r)$ and $b=\|\mu_r\|^2-\|\mu_g\|^2$. The decision boundary is the perpendicular bisector of the two means.

⚠️ Trap

Two quadratic-looking squared distances can produce a linear decision function because the $x^Tx$ terms cancel.

36 k-Nearest Neighbours
ClassificationRegression
Majority class among the $k$ nearest neighboursAverage target among the $k$ nearest neighbours

kNN is a lazy learner — it stores training data and performs most work during prediction.

37 Distance formulas

37.1 Euclidean distance:

$$d(a,b)=\sqrt{\sum_j(a_j-b_j)^2}$$

When only comparing distances, square roots can be skipped: $d_1\lt d_2\iff d_1^2\lt d_2^2$.

37.2 Manhattan distance:

$$d(a,b)=\sum_j|a_j-b_j|$$

Use whichever distance the question specifies.

38 kNN solving algorithm
$$\text{Calculate}\to\text{Sort}\to\text{Select}\to\text{Vote}$$

Identify the query point → calculate distance to every training point → sort nearest to farthest → choose the first $k$ → count class votes → select the majority class. For minimum odd $k$, test $k=1,3,5,7,\dots$

🧠 Memory
  • Small $k$ is nervous. Large $k$ forgets local detail.
40 Weighted kNN
$$w_i=\frac1{d_i^p}\qquad p=1\text{ or }2\text{ (common choices)}$$

Procedure: calculate neighbour weights → sum weights class-wise → choose the class with the greatest total weight. A class with fewer but closer neighbours may win.

41 kNN ties

Ties can occur from equal vote counts, equal distances, or rounded distances becoming equal. Odd $k$ reduces vote ties in binary classification, but cannot prevent all distance ties.

42 Why 1-NN is not always perfect

Even with a very large dataset, errors may remain: classes overlap; labels contain noise; the Bayes error is non-zero; similar inputs may belong to different classes.

$$\text{Large data does not guarantee perfect 1-NN accuracy}$$
43 Feature scaling

Distance-based methods are sensitive to feature scale. If one feature ranges 0–1 and another 0–100000, the second feature dominates distance. Scaling matters for kNN, k-means, and nearest-centroid classification. Common methods: standardization, min-max normalization.

44 k-Means clustering

k-means is unsupervised. Here $k=$ number of clusters — do not confuse it with kNN, where $k$ is the number of neighbours.

kNNk-means
SupervisedUnsupervised
Uses labelsNo labels
$k=$ neighbours$k=$ clusters
Predicts class/valueDiscovers groups
🧠 Memory
  • kNN asks neighbours. k-means moves means.
45 k-Means algorithm
$$\text{Assign}\to\text{Update}\to\text{Repeat}$$

Assignment step — assign each point to its nearest centroid. Update step — move each centroid to the mean of its assigned points.

46 Centroid
$$\mu_j=\frac1{|C_j|}\sum_{x\in C_j}x$$

For two-dimensional data: average all $x$-coordinates, average all $y$-coordinates.

47 Inertia or WCSS

k-means minimizes:

$$WCSS=\sum_{j=1}^k\sum_{x\in C_j}\|x-\mu_j\|^2$$

Also called inertia, within-cluster SS, within-cluster sum of squares.

⚠️ Trap

Use squared distance — do not take square roots. Good clustering has low within-cluster SS and high between-cluster SS.

48 Convexity of k-means regions

With fixed centroids, each cluster region is a Voronoi region — and Euclidean Voronoi regions are convex: if two points $a,b$ are inside, every point on their line segment $(1-t)a+tb,\ 0\le t\le1$ is also inside, including the midpoint $\dfrac{a+b}2$.

🎯 GATE signal

If two points are known to be in the same k-means cluster and an option contains their midpoint — check the midpoint first.

49 Other k-means properties

Uses squared Euclidean distance; sensitive to scaling; sensitive to initialization; may converge to a local optimum; different initializations may produce different results; works best with compact, roughly convex clusters; performs poorly on rings and moon-shaped clusters; increasing $k$ cannot increase the best possible WCSS; the elbow method can help choose $k$.

50 Cross-validation

Cross-validation estimates generalization and selects hyperparameters. $k$-fold CV splits data into $k$ folds; each fold becomes validation data once; scores are averaged:

$$\text{CV score}=\frac{s_1+s_2+\cdots+s_k}{k}$$

Advantages: uses data efficiently, less dependent on one lucky split, every observation is validated once. Disadvantage: more computation.

51 Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation

With $n$ model-selection observations, $k=n$; each split trains on $n-1$ and validates on 1; number of validation splits $=n$.

51.1 After holding out a test set — with $N=$ total observations, $T=$ held-out test observations, LOOCV uses $N-T$ observations:

$$\text{LOOCV splits}=N-T$$
⚠️ Trap

Numbers of classes and features given in the question are distractors.

52 Grid-search model count

With $g$ hyperparameter combinations and $k$ folds:

$$\text{Number of model fits}=g\times k$$

Example: $12$ combinations $\times5$ folds $=60$ fits. A final full-data refit is included only when explicitly asked.

53 Time-series cross-validation

Do not randomly shuffle time-series data.

$$\text{Train on past}\to\text{validate on future}$$

Example: Jan–Mar → Apr; then Jan–Apr → May. Ordinary shuffled $k$-fold can cause future information to leak into past predictions.

56 Five-minute final revision

Remember this chain: $\text{Regression}\to\hat y=\beta_0+\beta^Tx\to SSE=\sum(y-\hat y)^2\to MSE=SSE/n,\ RMSE=\sqrt{MSE}\to R^2=1-SSE/SST$

$$\hat\beta=(X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty\qquad y=wx\Rightarrow w=\sum xy/\sum x^2$$ $$\text{Ridge}=L2\qquad\text{Lasso}=L1\qquad\text{Total loss}=\text{Data loss}+\lambda\text{ penalty}$$ $$p=\frac1{1+e^{-z}}\qquad\log\frac{p}{1-p}=z\qquad p'=p(1-p)\qquad\frac{dp}{dx}=\beta_1p(1-p)$$ $$\text{Precision}=\frac{TP}{TP+FP}\quad\text{Recall}=\frac{TP}{TP+FN}\quad\text{Specificity}=\frac{TN}{TN+FP}\quad FPR=1-\text{Specificity}$$ $$\text{Overlapping convex hulls}\Rightarrow\text{not linearly separable}\qquad\|a-x\|^2-\|b-x\|^2\Rightarrow x^Tx\text{ cancels}$$ $$\text{kNN: Calculate}\to\text{Sort}\to\text{Select}\to\text{Vote}\qquad\text{k-means: Assign}\to\text{Update centroid}\to\text{Repeat}$$ $$WCSS=\sum\|x-\mu\|^2\qquad\text{Grid fits}=\text{combinations}\times\text{folds}\qquad\text{LOOCV splits}=\text{non-test observations}$$
✦ The final mental story

Remember the complete subject as one story: start with data arranged as rows and features. For numerical answers, fit a regression line. Measure its mistakes using residuals, MAE or squared errors. Use $R^2$ to see how much variation was explained. Least squares chooses coefficients by minimizing squared errors; regularization prevents those coefficients from becoming unnecessarily complex.

For classes, logistic regression turns a linear score into a probability through the sigmoid. Evaluate classifiers using the confusion matrix, precision, recall, specificity and ROC. Some classes can be separated by one line; convex hulls reveal when this is impossible.

kNN classifies by asking nearby labelled points. k-means discovers unlabelled groups by repeatedly assigning points and moving centroids. Finally, cross-validation chooses hyperparameters without touching the final test set.

That story links the entire GATE DA machine-learning syllabus into one continuous structure.
