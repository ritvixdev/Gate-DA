💡 The one habit that solves most GATE ML questions

Before touching your pen, ask three questions in order: what is being predicted — a number, a category, or nothing at all (just groups)? What does the formula need — raw data, or something already computed like a mean or a residual? What is the question really testing — a definition, a calculation, or a trap?

Almost every GATE ML question is a straightforward calculation wearing a wordy disguise. Undress the wording first, and the arithmetic underneath is usually short.

2 The three problem types — regression, classification, clustering
🌱 The idea in plain words

Every machine-learning problem starts with one question: what am I trying to predict, and do I already have correct answers to learn from? That single question sorts almost all of GATE ML into three boxes.

Regression predicts a number — a price, a salary, a temperature, marks, rainfall in millimetres. Think study hours → marks. Classification predicts a category — spam or not spam, disease or no disease, cat, dog or bird. Clustering discovers groups without being given correct labels — think customers → natural customer groups, where nobody told the algorithm which customer belongs where.

ProblemCorrect answers supplied?Output
RegressionYesNumber
ClassificationYesClass
ClusteringNoCluster
🔍 Spot it in the exam

If the question gives you labelled training examples and asks for a real-valued prediction, it's regression. If the labels are categories, it's classification. If there are no labels at all — just points that need to be grouped — it's clustering. This one sentence resolves a surprising number of "which method applies here" questions.

Q · Basic data concepts. Select the correct option(s).

A. Filling in missing values in a dataset is referred to as data imputation.
B. A dataset with $m$ instances and $n$ features has an $m\times n$ data matrix when columns represent features.
C. In a function-approximation problem such as regression, labels are real numbers.
D. Two features can be correlated.

Solution — step by step
  1. A is true: replacing missing values with an estimate (mean, median, mode, or a model's prediction) is exactly what "imputation" means.
  2. B is true: rows are instances, columns are features — that's the standard data-matrix convention.
  3. C is true: regression ordinarily predicts real-valued outputs.
  4. D is true: nothing stops two input features from moving together.

Answer: A, B, C, D.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Regression gives a quantity. Classification gives a category. Clustering discovers communities.
  • GATE frequently repeats the same concept with a different sentence order or slightly different wording — don't let rephrasing fool you into re-deriving something you already know.
🔗 Connect the dots

Everything from Part II onward is really just "how do we do regression well" (linear regression, least squares, regularization), "how do we do classification well" (logistic regression, kNN, metrics), or "how do we do clustering well" (k-means) — plus cross-validation, which keeps all three honest.

3 Dataset notation — the data matrix $X$
🌱 The idea in plain words

Suppose you have $m$ observations (people, houses, days — whatever you're studying) and $n$ features (age, size, temperature — whatever you measured about them). Machine learning collects all of it into one rectangular table:

$$X\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$$

Rows represent observations. Columns represent features. For example, three people described by age, income and years of experience give a $3\times3$ matrix:

$$X=\begin{bmatrix}25&40000&2\\30&60000&6\\35&75000&9\end{bmatrix}$$

The target vector collects the one number we're trying to predict for each row — say, salary:

$$y=\begin{bmatrix}50000\\70000\\90000\end{bmatrix}$$
🔍 Spot it in the exam

Whenever a question says "$m$ observations, $n$ features," your very first line of working should be "$X$ is $m\times n$." Half of these questions are just testing whether you know rows-are-observations, columns-are-features.

🧠 Trick to remember
  • Rows = records, columns = characteristics. "Record" and "row" both start with R.
4 Basic data terms — feature, target, prediction & imputation
🌱 The idea in plain words

A feature ($x_1,x_2,\dots,x_p$) is an input used for prediction. The target or label ($y$) is the answer you're trying to predict. The prediction ($\hat y$, read "y-hat") is the model's own guess at that answer. Imputation means replacing missing values with an estimate — the mean, median or mode of the column, most commonly.

⚠️ Correlation is not causation

Two variables can move together (correlation) without one actually producing a change in the other (causation). Ice-cream sales and drowning deaths rise together every summer — heat causes both, neither causes the other. GATE loves testing exactly this distinction.

🧠 Trick to remember
  • $\hat y$ carries a hat because it's "wearing a disguise" for the real $y$ — it's an estimate, not the truth.
5 Correlation vs causation, and Pearson's symmetry
🤔 Why is correlation symmetric?

Pearson correlation is $R_{xy}=\dfrac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y}$. Since $\text{Cov}(X,Y)=\text{Cov}(Y,X)$ and $\sigma_X\sigma_Y=\sigma_Y\sigma_X$, swapping the roles of $X$ and $Y$ changes nothing. So $R_{xy}=R_{yx}$ always — correlation never has a "direction," even though causation might.

$$-1\le r\le1$$

A perfect increasing straight-line relationship $y=ax+b$ with $a\gt0$ gives $r=1$; if $a\lt0$, then $r=-1$ (assuming $x$ is not constant). Nothing else can push $r$ outside this range.

Q · Symmetry of correlation. If $R_{xy}=0.8$, what is $R_{yx}$?

A. $0.8$    B. $-0.8$    C. $0$    D. Cannot be determined

Solution — step by step
  1. $\text{Cov}(X,Y)=\text{Cov}(Y,X)$, and $\sigma_X\sigma_Y=\sigma_Y\sigma_X$.
  2. Therefore $R_{xy}=R_{yx}$ exactly — no computation needed once you see the symmetry.

Answer: A.

Q · Permissible correlation values. Which values are not permissible for Pearson correlation?

A. $2$    B. $-1$    C. $-2$    D. $1$

Solution — step by step
  1. Pearson correlation always satisfies $-1\le r\le1$.
  2. $2$ and $-2$ fall outside that range; $-1$ and $1$ are the boundary values themselves, both allowed.

Answer: A, C.

🧠 Trick to remember
  • Correlation may be explained by a third variable, reverse causation, coincidence, or selection bias — never assume causation from correlation alone.
  • Perfect line, positive slope → $r=1$. Perfect line, negative slope → $r=-1$. Anything less tidy sits strictly in between.
6 Simple linear regression — fitting one line
🌱 The idea in plain words

Imagine points scattered on graph paper. Linear regression draws a single straight line through them so that the line sits, on average, as close as possible to every point. With one feature:

$$\hat y=\beta_0+\beta_1x$$

where $\beta_0$ is the intercept, $\beta_1$ is the slope, $x$ is the input, and $\hat y$ is the prediction. For example, if $\text{marks}=20+10(\text{hours})$, then at 5 hours: $\hat y=20+10(5)=70$. The slope means "one extra hour of study predicts 10 more marks"; the intercept means "at zero hours studied, the predicted mark is 20."

🔍 Spot it in the exam

Whenever you see a fitted equation like $\text{marks}=20+10(\text{hours})$, read $\beta_0$ as the "starting value" and $\beta_1$ as "the change per unit increase" — that reading answers most interpretation questions instantly.

7 Multiple linear regression & the design matrix
🌱 The idea in plain words

With several features, the same idea just adds more slopes:

$$\hat y=\beta_0+\beta_1x_1+\beta_2x_2+\cdots+\beta_px_p$$

For example, $\text{salary}=20000+3000(\text{experience})+500(\text{test score})$. In matrix form this is simply $\hat y=X\beta$. If an intercept is included, $X$'s first column is a column of ones:

$$X=\begin{bmatrix}1&x_{11}&x_{12}\\1&x_{21}&x_{22}\\1&x_{31}&x_{32}\end{bmatrix}$$

With $n$ observations and $p$ predictors, $X$ is $n\times(p+1)$ — the extra "$+1$" column is exactly that column of ones.

Q · The first column of the design matrix. In $y=X\beta+\epsilon$, there are $n$ observations and $p$ independent variables, so $X$ is $n\times(p+1)$. The first column of $X$ is a vector of:

A. Zeros    B. Cannot be determined    C. Minus ones    D. Ones

Solution — step by step
  1. The first column represents the intercept term $\beta_0(1)$.
  2. For that term to multiply out correctly for every row, every row needs a literal $1$ in that column.

Answer: D.

🔗 Connect the dots

This design matrix is exactly the $X$ from Section 3 — now with one extra column of ones bolted on for the intercept. Everything downstream (least squares, ridge, lasso) works directly with this $X$.

8 The intercept — when do you actually need it?
🌱 The idea in plain words

The intercept is the model's starting value when every predictor is zero. Suppose $\text{distance}=\text{initial position}+\text{speed}\times\text{time}$. If the initial position is known to be $10$, you need an intercept: $d=10+vt$. If the object is known to start exactly at the origin, the line can be forced through $(0,0)$: $d=vt$.

🔍 Spot it in the exam

"Known non-zero starting value" → include an intercept. "Known to start at exactly zero" → force the line through the origin. Never answer "always include one" or "never include one" — both extremes are usually wrong.

Q · Physical meaning of the intercept. Simple linear regression estimates a car's speed from distance-time data. Time is measured exactly and distance is noisy.

A. Include an intercept if the initial distance is known to be non-zero.
B. Include an intercept if the initial distance is known to be exactly zero.
C. Never include an intercept.
D. Always include an intercept.

Solution — step by step
  1. A known non-zero initial position requires an intercept to represent that starting value.
  2. A known-zero initial position lets the model be forced through the origin instead.
  3. "Always" and "never" are both absolute statements that ignore the actual physical setup, so they're wrong.

Answer: A.

9 Residuals — how far off was the prediction?
🌱 The idea in plain words

For observation $i$, the residual is simply what actually happened minus what the model guessed:

$$e_i=y_i-\hat y_i$$

If $y=80$ and $\hat y=75$, then $e=80-75=5$ — the prediction was 5 too low. If $e=-5$, the prediction was 5 too high. Every error metric in the rest of this page is built from these residuals.

10 Why square the errors? MAE vs squared error
🤔 Why does squaring work?

Suppose the residuals are $+5$ and $-5$. Adding them directly gives $5-5=0$, which incorrectly hides both errors — it looks like a perfect fit! Squaring gives $5^2+(-5)^2=25+25=50$: it prevents positive and negative errors from cancelling, and it penalizes large errors much more strongly than small ones.

$$\text{MAE}=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n|y_i-\hat y_i|$$

Mean Absolute Error grows linearly with the size of the mistake: $|2|=2$, but $|20|=20$ — ten times the error gives exactly ten times the penalty. Squared error grows much faster, which is exactly why OLS (which minimizes squared error) reacts so strongly to outliers — see Section 12.

11 SSE, MSE & RMSE — the total, the average, and the un-squared
$$SSE=RSS=\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i-\hat y_i)^2\qquad MSE=\frac{SSE}{n}\qquad RMSE=\sqrt{MSE}$$

SSE (also called RSS) is the total squared error across every observation. MSE is just SSE averaged over $n$. RMSE takes the square root of MSE, which returns the error to the original unit of the target — if marks are the target, RMSE is measured in marks too, unlike MSE which is in "marks squared."

Q · Calculate the MSE. Given $y=[4,6,7,10]$ and $\hat y=[5,5,8,9]$, calculate the MSE.

Solution — step by step
  1. Residuals: $y-\hat y=[-1,1,-1,1]$.
  2. Squared residuals: $[1,1,1,1]$.
  3. $\text{MSE}=\dfrac{1+1+1+1}{4}=1$.

Answer: MSE = 1.

🧠 Memory chain
  • $R^2\to SSE\to MSE\to RMSE$ — each one feeds into the next; if a question gives you any one of them plus $n$ and $SST$, you can walk the whole chain.
12 Why OLS is so sensitive to outliers
⚠️ One far-away point can pull the whole line

OLS minimizes squared error. A residual of $2$ contributes $2^2=4$ to the total loss; a residual of $20$ contributes $20^2=400$ — one hundred times more, even though the residual itself is only ten times bigger. One distant, noisy observation can therefore pull the fitted line substantially away from where it "should" sit for the rest of the data.

Q · OLS and outliers.
A. OLS slope and intercept estimates are not sensitive to outliers.
B. OLS slope and intercept estimates are sensitive to outliers.

Solution

The squared-error objective heavily amplifies large residuals, so a single extreme point can move the fitted line noticeably.

Answer: B.

13 SST, SSR, SSE and $R^2$ — slicing the variation cake
🌱 The idea in plain words

Think of all the variation in $y$ as a cake. One portion is explained by the model; the rest is error.

$$SST=SSR+SSE$$

where $SST=\sum_i(y_i-\bar y)^2$ is the total variation, $SSR=\sum_i(\hat y_i-\bar y)^2$ is the variation the model explains, and $SSE=\sum_i(y_i-\hat y_i)^2$ is what's left unexplained.

$$R^2=\frac{SSR}{SST}=1-\frac{SSE}{SST}\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad SSE=SST(1-R^2)$$

$R^2=0.80$ means the model explains 80% of the variation in $y$.

Q · Direct $R^2$. Suppose $SST=500$, $SSR=380$. Find $R^2$.

Solution

$R^2=\dfrac{380}{500}=0.76$

Q · Reduction in SSE. Model A has $R^2_A=0.86$; Model B has $R^2_B=0.90$. Both use the same dataset with $SST=500$. By how much did SSE decrease?

Solution — step by step
  1. $SSE_A=500(1-0.86)=70$.
  2. $SSE_B=500(1-0.90)=50$.
  3. Decrease: $70-50=20$.

Answer: 20.

Q · RMSE from $R^2$. A model is evaluated on 200 observations with $R^2=0.84$ and $SST=2500$. Calculate RMSE.

Solution — step by step
  1. $SSE=2500(1-0.84)=2500(0.16)=400$.
  2. $MSE=\dfrac{400}{200}=2$.
  3. $RMSE=\sqrt2\approx1.414$.

Answer: RMSE ≈ 1.414.

14 Least squares & the normal equations
🌱 The idea in plain words

Ordinary Least Squares simply chooses the coefficients that minimize total squared error:

$$\min_{\beta_0,\beta_1}\sum_i\big[y_i-(\beta_0+\beta_1x_i)\big]^2$$

For simple regression with an intercept, the slope and intercept have closed forms:

$$\beta_1=\frac{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2}=\frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\text{Var}(X)}\qquad\beta_0=\bar y-\beta_1\bar x$$

The fitted line always passes through $(\bar x,\bar y)$ — a useful sanity check on any worked answer.

In matrix form, $\hat y=X\beta$ and the objective becomes $\min_\beta\|y-X\beta\|_2^2$. Setting the gradient to zero gives the normal equations:

$$X^TX\hat\beta=X^Ty\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad\hat\beta=(X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty\quad\text{(when }X^TX\text{ is invertible)}$$
🔍 Spot it in the exam

If you see "fit by linear least squares" with an intercept implied, reach for the centred-slope formula. If the model is explicitly written as $y=wx$ with no intercept term at all, that formula is wrong — go straight to Section 15 instead.

15 Regression through the origin
⚠️ Do not use the centred slope formula here

If the model is explicitly $y=wx$, there is no intercept — the line is forced through $(0,0)$. The correct formula is:

$$w=\frac{\sum_ix_iy_i}{\sum_ix_i^2}$$

Q · Least squares without an intercept (real GATE). Given $\{(-1,1),(2,-5),(3,5)\}$, fit $y=wx$ by linear least squares. Find the optimal $w$ to three decimal places.

Solution — step by step
  1. Because the model is $y=wx$, use $w=\dfrac{\sum x_iy_i}{\sum x_i^2}$.
  2. Numerator: $(-1)(1)+(2)(-5)+(3)(5)=-1-10+15=4$.
  3. Denominator: $(-1)^2+2^2+3^2=1+4+9=14$.
  4. $w=\dfrac{4}{14}=\dfrac27\approx0.286$.

Answer: $w\approx0.286$ — this matches the official GATE 2025 answer range.

16 The pseudoinverse & multicollinearity
🌱 The idea in plain words

$\hat\beta=(X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty$ requires $X^TX$ to be invertible. But even when $X^TX$ is singular, a least-squares solution still exists — it can be written using the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse, $\hat\beta=X^+y$. What actually fails under perfect collinearity is the ordinary inverse and the uniqueness of the coefficient vector; the fitted values can still be unique.

Perfect multicollinearity occurs when one feature is an exact linear combination of others, e.g. $x_3=2x_1-x_2$. Then the columns of $X$ are linearly dependent, so $X^TX$ is singular, coefficients are not uniquely identifiable, and multiple coefficient vectors can produce the exact same predictions.

Q · Closed-form OLS. Which statements about $\hat\beta=(X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty$ are true?

A. The estimator is a weighted linear combination of observed target values.
B. The inverse expression requires $X^TX$ to be invertible, so predictors cannot be perfectly collinear.
C. Matrix inversion is always more efficient than gradient descent, regardless of data size.
D. When the inverse exists, the expression gives the unique coefficient vector minimizing RSS.

Solution — step by step
  1. A: true — $\hat\beta$ is a matrix multiplied by $y$, i.e. a linear combination of the targets.
  2. B: true, for this particular closed-form and for coefficient uniqueness.
  3. C: false — inversion can be very expensive in high dimensions; gradient descent often wins there.
  4. D: true under the invertibility condition.

Answer: A, B, D.

Q · Exact predictor dependence. One predictor is an exact linear combination of the remaining predictors. Which statement is correct?

A. Residuals become zero.   B. $R^2$ becomes negative.
C. OLS predictions remain unique, but coefficient estimates are not unique.   D. Predictions become impossible.

Solution

Different coefficient vectors can represent the exact same fitted point in the column space of $X$, so predictions stay unique even though the coefficients themselves are not.

Answer: C.

🧠 Trick to remember
  • Singular $X^TX$ breaks the ordinary inverse and coefficient uniqueness — it does not break the model's ability to predict.
17 Sample size & estimator variance

A simplified relationship for the variance of a slope estimate is:

$$\text{Var}(\hat\beta_1)\propto\frac{\sigma^2}{n\,\text{Var}(x)}$$

As $n$ increases, the variance shrinks, so the estimate ordinarily becomes more stable — but it does not become exactly zero, no matter how much data you add.

Q · Doubling sample size. The sample size doubles while the underlying data distribution remains unchanged. What is the most likely effect on the variance of OLS coefficient estimates?

A. Increases   B. Remains exactly unchanged   C. Decreases   D. Becomes zero

Solution

More observations normally reduce uncertainty in the estimate, but never make it exactly zero.

Answer: C.

18 Residual plots — homoscedasticity vs heteroscedasticity
🌱 The idea in plain words

A residual plot places fitted values on the horizontal axis and residuals on the vertical axis. If the spread of residuals stays roughly the same everywhere — a random cloud, no funnel shape — that's homoscedasticity. If the spread visibly changes with the fitted value — a funnel or cone, widening or narrowing — that's heteroscedasticity.

Q · Residual pattern. Standardized residuals are plotted against predicted values and appear randomly scattered.

A. The assumption of heteroscedasticity is met.   B. The assumption of homoscedasticity is met.

Solution

Random scatter with roughly constant spread supports homoscedasticity, not heteroscedasticity.

Answer: B.

19 Gradient descent
🌱 The idea in plain words

Instead of solving for the exact minimum in one shot, gradient descent repeatedly nudges the parameters a little downhill:

$$w_{\text{new}}=w_{\text{old}}-\eta\nabla J(w)$$

where $\eta$ is the learning rate and $\nabla J(w)$ is the gradient. The gradient points uphill (toward increasing loss), so subtracting it moves you downhill, toward lower loss. For squared-error regression, $\nabla J(w)=\dfrac2nX^T(Xw-y)$.

🔗 Connect the dots

Gradient descent is how you fit models when the closed-form normal equations are too expensive — and it's the exact mechanism used to update weights when a regularization penalty (next section) is added to the loss.

20 Regularization — the general recipe
🌱 The idea in plain words

Every regularized objective has the same shape — a prediction loss, plus a penalty on the size of the weights, scaled by how much you care about that penalty:

$$\text{Total loss}=\text{Prediction loss}+\lambda\cdot\text{Penalty}$$
🔍 Spot it in the exam

Whenever a question asks for an "overall regularized loss," calculate four things in order: the prediction, the prediction error, the regularization penalty, and finally their total. Skipping straight to a formula usually causes a sign or scaling mistake — do it step by step.

21 Ridge regression — the L2 penalty
$$J(w)=L(w)+\lambda\|w\|_2^2\qquad\text{where}\qquad\|w\|_2^2=w_1^2+\cdots+w_p^2$$

Ridge shrinks coefficients, reduces model variance, may increase bias, and helps with multicollinearity — but it usually does not push coefficients exactly to zero.

⚠️ Watch the convention — $\lambda\|w\|^2$ vs $\frac\lambda2\|w\|^2$

These two conventions have different gradients: $\nabla(\lambda\|w\|^2)=2\lambda w$, while $\nabla\!\left(\frac\lambda2\|w\|^2\right)=\lambda w$. Always follow the exact formula the question gives you — don't assume which convention is "standard."

Q · Ridge concept (real GATE). Which statement is true for Ridge Regression?

A. The regularizer protects against a model working well on test data but poorly on training data.
B. The regularizer uses the $L_1$ norm.
C. Ridge aims to reduce the number of negative-valued parameters.
D. The regularizer may increase bias but helps reduce variance in predictions.

Solution — step by step
  1. A reverses the usual overfitting pattern — overfitting means excellent training performance but weak test performance, not the reverse.
  2. B is false: Ridge uses $L_2$, not $L_1$.
  3. C is false: Ridge penalizes the magnitude of weights, not their sign.
  4. D is exactly the bias–variance trade-off Ridge makes.

Answer: D.

Q · Complete regularized loss (real GATE). Linear Ridge Regression uses $\hat y=w^Tx$ with $w=\begin{bmatrix}-3\\4\end{bmatrix}$, $x=\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}$. The prediction loss is MAE and the regularizer weight is $0.20$. The true value is $y_{\text{true}}=x_1+x_2$. Find the overall regularized loss.

Solution — step by step
  1. Prediction: $\hat y=w^Tx=(-3)(1)+(4)(2)=-3+8=5$.
  2. True value: $y_{\text{true}}=1+2=3$.
  3. MAE for one observation: $|5-3|=2$.
  4. Ridge penalty: $0.20\|w\|^2=0.20[(-3)^2+4^2]=0.20(9+16)=5$.
  5. Total: $2+5=7$.

Answer: 7.00 — matches the worked result on the GATE-question page.

Q · L2 update. Logistic regression with L2 regularization uses $\beta\leftarrow\beta-\eta(\nabla L+\lambda\beta)$. Given $\eta=0.1$, $\beta=3$, $\nabla L=2$, $\lambda=0.4$, find the updated coefficient.

Solution — step by step
  1. $\lambda\beta=0.4(3)=1.2$.
  2. Total gradient: $2+1.2=3.2$.
  3. Update: $\beta_{\text{new}}=3-0.1(3.2)=2.68$.

Answer: 2.68.

🧠 Trick to remember
  • Ridge restrains large weights — think of it as a rubber band pulling every coefficient gently toward zero, never letting any one of them run away.
22 Lasso regression — the L1 penalty
$$J(w)=L(w)+\lambda\sum_j|w_j|$$

Lasso can set coefficients exactly to zero — which means it can perform feature selection — and it is convex, but it is not differentiable at $w=0$: $\dfrac{d|w|}{dw}=1$ for $w\gt0$ and $-1$ for $w\lt0$, with no single ordinary derivative right at $w=0$.

Q · L1 differentiability. Logistic regression with L1 regularization is trained using gradient descent. Which statement is correct?

A. The L1 penalty has continuous second derivatives everywhere.
B. The optimization becomes non-convex.
C. Every update is differentiable.
D. Standard gradient descent cannot directly use a unique derivative exactly at $w=0$.

Solution

The absolute-value function has a sharp corner at zero, so it has no unique ordinary derivative there — subgradient or proximal methods handle this in practice.

Answer: D.

🧠 Trick to remember
  • Ridge → $L_2$ → shrinks but rarely zeroes. Lasso → $L_1$ → can zero coefficients out entirely, doubling as feature selection.
23 Overfitting & underfitting
🌱 The idea in plain words

Underfitting means the model is too simple: high training error and high validation error, together with high bias, low variance, excessive regularization, or a very large $k$ in kNN. Overfitting means the model has fit the training noise itself: very low training error but higher validation error, together with low bias, high variance, too many parameters or features, a very small $k$ in kNN, or insufficient regularization.

⚠️ Training performance alone tells you nothing about generalization

A model can look perfect on the data it was trained on and still fail badly on new data. Generalization can only be judged using data the model has not seen.

Q · Definitions. Select the correct option(s).

A. An overly simplistic model is underfitting.
B. Generalization efficiency can be judged reliably from training data alone.
C. Generalization can be gauged using unseen test data.
D. In overfitting, a model fits training data very well but generalizes poorly.

Solution

Training performance alone cannot reveal whether a model generalizes, so B is false; A, C and D are all true.

Answer: A, C, D.

Q · Opposite movement of errors. Adding one parameter decreases training MSE but increases validation MSE. The model is:

A. Overfitting   B. Underfitting

Solution

The extra parameter is helping the model memorize training data while harming its performance on unseen data.

Answer: A.

🧠 Trick to remember
  • Underfitting = "too lazy to learn." Overfitting = "memorized the homework instead of learning the subject."
24 The confusion matrix
🌱 The idea in plain words

Every binary classifier's mistakes and successes fit into a $2\times2$ table:

Predicted positivePredicted negative
Actual positiveTPFN
Actual negativeFPTN

TP: actual positive, predicted positive. FN: actual positive, predicted negative. FP: actual negative, predicted positive. TN: actual negative, predicted negative.

🧠 Trick to remember
  • The second letter (P or N) always tells you what the model predicted; the first letter (T or F) tells you whether that prediction was correct.
25 Accuracy, precision, recall & specificity
$$\text{Accuracy}=\frac{TP+TN}{TP+TN+FP+FN}$$

Out of everyone, how many predictions were correct?

$$\text{Precision}=\frac{TP}{TP+FP}$$

Of everything predicted positive, how much was truly positive? Precision starts from the predictions.

$$\text{Recall}=\text{Sensitivity}=\text{TPR}=\frac{TP}{TP+FN}$$

Of all actual positive cases, how many did the model detect? Recall starts from reality.

$$\text{Specificity}=\frac{TN}{TN+FP}\qquad\text{FPR}=\frac{FP}{FP+TN}=1-\text{Specificity}$$

Specificity measures the fraction of actual negatives correctly identified.

Q · FPR from specificity. A classifier has $\text{Sensitivity}=0.92$, $\text{Specificity}=0.84$. Find FPR.

Solution

$\text{FPR}=1-0.84=0.16$. Sensitivity was a distractor — it wasn't needed here.

Q · Precision. A confusion matrix has $TP=45$, $FP=15$, $TN=130$, $FN=10$. Calculate precision.

Solution

$\text{Precision}=\dfrac{45}{45+15}=\dfrac{45}{60}=0.75$

Q · Accuracy from class-wise rates. A classifier is evaluated on 1000 observations: 400 positive, 600 negative. It correctly classifies 90% of positives and 80% of negatives. Find accuracy.

Solution — step by step
  1. Correct positives: $TP=0.90(400)=360$.
  2. Correct negatives: $TN=0.80(600)=480$.
  3. Accuracy $=\dfrac{360+480}{1000}=0.84$.

Q · Equal confusion outcomes. A balanced binary dataset produces $TP=FP=TN=FN=\tfrac14$. What is the accuracy?

Solution

$\text{Accuracy}=TP+TN$ because these are proportions summing to one $=\tfrac14+\tfrac14=0.5$.

🧠 Trick to remember
  • Precision starts from predictions; recall starts from reality. Say it out loud once and you'll never swap them again.
26 Class-wise metrics for multiclass problems
🌱 The idea in plain words

For multiclass or named-class questions, temporarily treat the class of interest as "positive":

$$\text{Precision}_C=\frac{\text{correctly predicted as }C}{\text{everything predicted as }C}\qquad\text{Recall}_C=\frac{\text{correctly predicted as }C}{\text{everything actually belonging to }C}$$

Q · Author classification (real GATE). There are 20 stories by Author X and 10 by Author Y. 6 X stories are labelled Y, and 2 Y stories are labelled X. Which statements are true?

A. Accuracy is $11/15$.   B. Precision of X is higher than precision of Y.
C. Recall of X is higher than recall of Y.   D. Accuracy is $14/15$.

Solution — step by step
  1. Build the table: Actual X → 14 predicted X, 6 predicted Y (total 20). Actual Y → 2 predicted X, 8 predicted Y (total 10).
  2. Accuracy $=\dfrac{14+8}{30}=\dfrac{22}{30}=\dfrac{11}{15}$. So A is true, D is false.
  3. $\text{Precision}_X=\dfrac{14}{14+2}=\dfrac{14}{16}=0.875$. $\text{Precision}_Y=\dfrac{8}{8+6}=\dfrac{8}{14}\approx0.571$. So B is true.
  4. $\text{Recall}_X=\dfrac{14}{20}=0.70$. $\text{Recall}_Y=\dfrac{8}{10}=0.80$. So C is false.

Answer: A, B — the same computations appear on the GATE-question page.

27 Perfect & always-positive classifiers

A perfect classifier makes no mistakes: $FP=FN=0$. For multiple classes, every off-diagonal cell is zero — the confusion matrix is diagonal.

Q · Perfect confusion matrix. A classifier makes no errors, and every class has at least one observation. Which statements are true?

A. Diagonal entries are non-zero.   B. Diagonal entries may be zero or non-zero.
C. All off-diagonal entries are zero.   D. Off-diagonal entries may be zero or non-zero.

Solution

Every class has at least one correctly classified member, so diagonal entries are positive. All errors are absent, so off-diagonal entries are zero.

Answer: A, C.

If a model predicts positive for everyone: no positive case is missed ($FN=0$), but no negative case is ever correctly called negative ($TN=0$). Therefore $\text{Sensitivity}=1$ and $\text{Specificity}=0$.

Q · Always positive. A classifier predicts the positive class for every input.

A. Sensitivity $=0$, specificity $=1$   B. Sensitivity $=1$, specificity $=0$

Solution

Answer: B.

28 Balanced accuracy, prevalence & Cohen's Kappa

A balanced binary dataset has equal actual positives and negatives: $TP+FN=TN+FP$.

Q · Balance identity. For a perfectly balanced binary dataset, which equality is guaranteed?

A. $TP+FP=TN+FN$   B. None   C. $TP+FN=TN+FP$   D. $TP+TN=FP+FN$

Solution

Answer: C.

$$\text{Balanced accuracy}=\frac{\text{Sensitivity}+\text{Specificity}}{2}$$

It weights the two actual classes equally. Ordinary accuracy equals balanced accuracy whenever the dataset has equal actual class counts ($TP+FN=TN+FP$) — no additional assumption about equal error rates is needed.

$$\text{Prevalence}=\frac{TP+FN}{N}\qquad\text{Detection prevalence}=\frac{TP+FP}{N}$$

Prevalence is the actual positive fraction; detection prevalence is the predicted positive fraction. They need not be equal.

$$\kappa=\frac{p_o-p_e}{1-p_e}$$

where $p_o$ is observed agreement and $p_e$ is agreement expected purely by chance — Cohen's Kappa adjusts accuracy for the agreement you'd get from lucky guessing alone.

Q · Metric statements. Which statements are correct?

A. Cohen's Kappa adjusts for agreement expected by chance.
B. Precision increases when false positives decrease while TP stays fixed.
C. Detection prevalence equals prevalence for every classifier.
D. Balanced accuracy equals ordinary accuracy for every classifier and dataset.
E. Specificity increases when false positives decrease.

Solution — step by step
  1. A: true, directly from the Kappa formula.
  2. B: true — lowering FP shrinks precision's denominator, raising precision.
  3. C: false — actual-positive and predicted-positive counts can differ.
  4. D: false in general — only true when actual classes are balanced.
  5. E: true — lowering FP increases $\dfrac{TN}{TN+FP}$.

Answer: A, B, E.

29 ROC and AUC
🌱 The idea in plain words

The Receiver Operating Characteristic curve plots $\text{TPR}=\text{Sensitivity}=\text{Recall}$ on the $y$-axis against $\text{FPR}=1-\text{Specificity}$ on the $x$-axis. Changing the probability threshold that separates "predict positive" from "predict negative" changes both TPR and FPR, tracing out the curve.

Q · ROC variables. Which statements are correct?

A. Recall is plotted on the X-axis.   B. TPR or sensitivity is plotted on the Y-axis.
C. FPR or $1-\text{specificity}$ is plotted on the X-axis.   D. Precision is plotted on the Y-axis.

Solution

Answer: B, C.

The ROC curve's slope is $\dfrac{d(\text{TPR})}{d(\text{FPR})}$ — like any curve $y=f(x)$, its slope is $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.

Q · ROC slope statement. The slope of the ROC curve represents the rate of change of TPR with respect to FPR.

A. False   B. True

Solution

Answer: B.

AUC is the area under the ROC curve. $AUC=1$ means perfect ranking; $AUC=0.5$ means random ranking. Higher is always better.

Q · Best AUC. Which AUC is most desirable?

A. 0.8   B. 0.5   C. 0.75   D. 0.9

Solution

Answer: D.

🧠 Trick to remember
  • ROC axes spell "FTP" backwards in a sense — FPR on the floor (x-axis), TPR up top (y-axis).
30 Logistic regression — from a line to a probability
🌱 The idea in plain words

A straight line can output anything — $-2$, $1.7$, $10$ — none of which are valid probabilities. Logistic regression fixes this by squashing a linear score into the range $(0,1)$. First compute the score:

$$z=\beta_0+\beta_1x_1+\cdots+\beta_px_p$$

Then squash it through the sigmoid:

$$p=\sigma(z)=\frac1{1+e^{-z}}$$
🤔 Why the sigmoid, specifically?

$\sigma(0)=0.5$; as $z\to+\infty$, $\sigma(z)\to1$; as $z\to-\infty$, $\sigma(z)\to0$. It is strictly increasing everywhere, and it happens to be exactly the cumulative distribution function of the standard logistic distribution — so it's a genuine, well-behaved probability, not an arbitrary squashing trick.

Q · Sigmoid properties. The function $\dfrac{e^x}{1+e^x}$ is:

A. A probability density function   B. A cumulative distribution function
C. Strictly increasing for every finite $x$   D. None of the above

Solution

Its derivative is $\sigma(x)[1-\sigma(x)]\gt0$ for every finite $x$, confirming it is strictly increasing and is indeed a CDF.

Answer: B, C.

31 Odds, log-odds & the decision boundary
$$\text{odds}=\frac{p}{1-p}\qquad\log\!\left(\frac{p}{1-p}\right)=\beta_0+\beta^Tx$$

Probability itself is not linear in the predictors — the log-odds are. This is the single most important fact about logistic regression, and it's exactly what "logistic" refers to.

Q · What is linear? In logistic regression, a linear relationship is assumed between the independent variables and:

A. The sigmoid of the dependent variable   B. The log-odds
C. The sine of the dependent variable   D. None

Solution

The exact linear quantity is $\log\dfrac{p}{1-p}$ — the log-odds, not simply "the log of the dependent variable."

Answer: B.

Usually $p\ge0.5\Rightarrow$ class 1, and since $p=0.5\iff z=0$, the decision boundary is $\beta_0+\beta^Tx=0$ — a line in two dimensions, a hyperplane in $d$ dimensions.

🧠 Trick to remember
  • Probability curves; log-odds is the straight line hiding underneath it.
32 The sigmoid's derivative
$$f(x)=\frac1{1+e^{-x}}\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad f'(x)=f(x)[1-f(x)]$$
🔍 Spot it in the exam

If the question gives you $f(x)=p$ directly, immediately write $f'(x)=p(1-p)$ — there is no need to solve for $x$ at all.

Q · Derivative from function value (real GATE). Let $f(x)=\dfrac1{1+e^{-x}}$. Find $f'(x)$ at the point where $f(x)=0.4$.

Solution

$f'(x)=0.4(1-0.4)=0.4(0.6)=0.24$ — the official GATE 2024 key accepts approximately $0.24$.

By the chain rule, if $p=\sigma(\beta_0+\beta_1x)$, then $\dfrac{dp}{dx}=\beta_1\,p(1-p)$.

Q · Logistic derivative. A model is $\log\!\left(\dfrac{p}{1-p}\right)=-1.5+0.3x$. At $x=5$, find $\dfrac{dp}{dx}$.

Solution — step by step
  1. $z=-1.5+0.3(5)=0$.
  2. $p=\sigma(0)=0.5$.
  3. $\dfrac{dp}{dx}=0.3(0.5)(0.5)=0.075$.

Since $p(1-p)$ is maximized at $p=0.5$ (i.e. $z=0$), the sigmoid's slope is steepest exactly at the decision boundary, at $x=-\dfrac{\beta_0}{\beta_1}$.

Q · Maximum derivative location. A logistic model has $\beta_0=-4$, $\beta_1=0.5$. Find $x$ where the derivative is maximized.

Solution

Set $-4+0.5x=0\Rightarrow0.5x=4\Rightarrow x=8$.

33 Maximum likelihood & softmax
🌱 The idea in plain words

For binary labels $y_i\in\{0,1\}$ with model probability $p_i=P(y_i=1)$, the likelihood multiplies, for each observation, the probability the model assigns to that observation's true class:

$$L=\prod_ip_i^{y_i}(1-p_i)^{1-y_i}$$

For a point truly labelled 1, use $p_i$; for a point truly labelled 0, use $1-p_i$.

Q · Class-specific probability notation. There are $n$ observations. $p_i$ is the probability of class 0, $q_i$ is the probability of class 1. The first $k$ observations are truly class 0, the remaining are truly class 1. Which is the likelihood?

A. $(1-p_1)\cdots(1-p_k)q_{k+1}\cdots q_n$   B. $p_1\cdots p_kq_{k+1}\cdots q_n$
C. $(1-p_1)\cdots(1-p_k)(1-q_{k+1})\cdots(1-q_n)$   D. $p_1\cdots p_k(1-q_{k+1})\cdots(1-q_n)$

Solution

Each observation contributes the probability assigned to its own true class: class-0 points contribute $p_i$ (the probability of class 0), class-1 points contribute $q_i$ (the probability of class 1).

Answer: B.

Because $\log$ is strictly increasing, $\max L\iff\max\log L$, and negating turns maximization into minimization: $\max\log L\iff\min(-\log L)$. The binary negative log-likelihood is exactly binary cross-entropy:

$$-\sum_i\big[y_i\log p_i+(1-y_i)\log(1-p_i)\big]$$

Q · Logistic optimization. Optimal logistic-regression parameters can be found by:

A. Maximizing likelihood   B. Maximizing log-likelihood
C. Minimizing likelihood   D. Minimizing negative log-likelihood

Solution

Answer: A, B, D.

For $N$ classes, softmax generalizes the sigmoid: $P(y=k)=\dfrac{e^{z_k}}{\sum_{i=1}^Ne^{z_i}}$. It is a valid PMF because every term is positive and they all sum to 1.

Q · Softmax validity. For $P(y=k)=\dfrac{e^{x_k}}{\sum_{i=1}^Ne^{x_i}}$:

A. It is a valid probability mass function.   B. It is not a valid probability mass function.

Solution

Answer: A.

⚠️ Many predictors, small data

Logistic regression tends to overfit when it has a large number of independent variables relative to the amount of data available, especially without regularization — "large number of predictors" alone isn't the precise story; it's the ratio to available data that matters.

34 Linear separability
🌱 The idea in plain words

Two classes are linearly separable when one straight line (or, in higher dimensions, one hyperplane) can put every point of one class on one side and every point of the other class on the other side:

$$w^Tx+b=0\qquad\text{(boundary)}\qquad\text{predict by the sign of }w^Tx+b$$

Fast visual rules: clearly separated upper and lower groups → usually separable with a horizontal line. One class surrounded by the other → not separable. An XOR-like arrangement (diagonally alternating classes) → not separable. Upper-left vs lower-right groups → often separable with a diagonal line.

🤔 The convex-hull test

Imagine wrapping a rubber band around each class — that's its convex hull. If the two classes' convex hulls overlap, no separating line can exist. If the hulls are disjoint, a separating hyperplane is guaranteed to exist.

Q · Four datasets (real GATE). Four figures contain circles and squares. (i) circles sit above the squares. (ii) a lone circle is surrounded by squares on all sides. (iii) circles and squares occupy opposite corners in an XOR pattern. (iv) squares sit toward the upper-left, circles toward the lower-right. Which datasets are linearly separable?

A. Dataset (i)   B. Dataset (ii)   C. Dataset (iii)   D. Dataset (iv)

Solution — step by step
  1. (i): a horizontal boundary separates circles above from squares below — separable.
  2. (ii): the circle's point lies inside the squares' convex hull — not separable.
  3. (iii): the class convex hulls intersect in the XOR pattern — not separable.
  4. (iv): a diagonal line separates upper-left from lower-right — separable.

Answer: A, D — the official GATE 2024 key lists exactly A and D for this MSQ.

35 Nearest-centroid classification
🌱 The idea in plain words

Each class has a mean — $\mu_{\text{red}}$, $\mu_{\text{green}}$. Assign a new point $x$ to whichever mean it's closer to. Define $f(x)=\|\mu_{\text{red}}-x\|^2-\|\mu_{\text{green}}-x\|^2$; if $f(x)\lt0$, $x$ is closer to red.

🤔 The important GATE expansion trick

Expand each squared distance: $\|\mu_r-x\|^2=\|\mu_r\|^2-2\mu_r^Tx+x^Tx$, and similarly for green. Subtracting the two, the $x^Tx$ terms cancel, leaving:

$$f(x)=2(\mu_g-\mu_r)^Tx+\|\mu_r\|^2-\|\mu_g\|^2=w^Tx+b$$

where $w=2(\mu_g-\mu_r)$ and $b=\|\mu_r\|^2-\|\mu_g\|^2$. Although each individual distance is quadratic in $x$, their difference is affine (linear plus a constant) — that cancellation is the whole trick.

Q · Difference of squared distances (real GATE). A test point is labelled by $f(x)=\|\mu_{\text{red}}-x\|^2-\|\mu_{\text{green}}-x\|^2$, with red assigned when $f(x)\lt0$. Which statements are correct?

A. $x=0$ is labelled green if $\|\mu_{\text{red}}\|\lt\|\mu_{\text{green}}\|$.
B. $f$ is linear in $x$.
C. $f(x)=w^Tx+b$, with $w,b$ determined by the class means.
D. $f$ is a quadratic polynomial in $x$.

Solution — step by step
  1. At $x=0$: $f(0)=\|\mu_r\|^2-\|\mu_g\|^2$. If $\|\mu_r\|\lt\|\mu_g\|$, then $f(0)\lt0$, so the point is labelled red — making A false.
  2. The common $x^Tx$ terms cancel between the two expansions, so $f$ is affine (effectively linear), not quadratic — making D false and B true.
  3. C follows directly from the expansion above. The official GATE 2025 key confirms B and C.

Answer: B, C.

36 k-Nearest Neighbours
🌱 The idea in plain words

For classification: find the $k$ nearest labelled points and take a vote. For regression: find the $k$ nearest points and average their target values. kNN is a lazy learner — it does almost no work during training and most of its work at prediction time.

$$d(a,b)=\sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^d(a_j-b_j)^2}$$

When only comparing distances, squared distances may be used directly, since square root is an increasing function: $d_1\lt d_2\iff d_1^2\lt d_2^2$ — no need to actually take the root.

🔍 The procedure, every time

Calculate every distance → sort nearest to farthest → select the first $k$ → count class votes.

Q · Minimum odd $k$ (real GATE). The query diamond is at $(2,1)$. Circles at $(1,1),(1,2)$; squares at $(2,3),(3,2),(3,3)$. Find the smallest odd $k$ for which the diamond is classified as a square.

Solution — step by step
  1. Distances from $(2,1)$: $(1,1)$ Circle $=1$; $(1,2)$ Circle $=\sqrt2$; $(3,2)$ Square $=\sqrt2$; $(2,3)$ Square $=2$; $(3,3)$ Square $=\sqrt5$.
  2. $k=1$: nearest is $(1,1)$, Circle wins 1–0.
  3. $k=3$: the three nearest are two Circles and one Square — Circles win 2–1.
  4. $k=5$: all five points vote — Squares win 3–2.

Answer: $k=5$.

Q · Effects of $k$. A very large $k$ may include points from other classes. A very small $k$ makes kNN sensitive to noise.

A. Both false   B. First false, second true   C. Both true   D. First true, second false

Solution

Answer: C.

Weighted kNN gives closer neighbours more influence: $w_i=\dfrac1{d_i^p}$, commonly with $p=1$ or $p=2$, with weights summed separately per class.

Q · Weighted vote. Five neighbours: distance 1 (class A), distance 2 (class B), distance 3 (class A), distance 4 (class B), distance 5 (class B). Weights use $w=1/d$. Which class wins?

Solution — step by step
  1. Class A: $1+\tfrac13=1.333$.
  2. Class B: $\tfrac12+\tfrac14+\tfrac15=0.5+0.25+0.2=0.95$.

Answer: Class A — a class with fewer neighbours can still win if those neighbours are much closer.

⚠️ Ties, rounding, and the limits of 1-NN

Ties can occur from equal votes, equal-distance training points, or rounding making distances look equal. Odd $k$ reduces vote-count ties in binary classification but cannot remove all distance ties. And even with unlimited data, 1-NN is not guaranteed perfect: classes can overlap, labels can be noisy, and the Bayes error may simply be non-zero.

Q · Very large data and 1-NN. With binary classification, $k=1$, and a very large labelled dataset, will every new point always be classified correctly?

A. False   B. True

Solution

"Always" would require perfectly separable, noise-free classes — an assumption we can't make in general.

Answer: A.

🔗 Connect the dots

Because kNN and k-means (next section) are both distance-based, feature scaling matters enormously for both: if age ranges 20–60 but salary ranges 20,000–200,000, salary differences will silently dominate every distance calculation unless features are scaled first.

37 k-Means clustering
kNNk-means
SupervisedUnsupervised
Uses labelsDoes not use labels
$k=$ neighbours$k=$ clusters
Predicts a class/valueFinds groups
🌱 The idea in plain words

k-means repeats two steps until nothing changes: an assignment step (assign each point to its closest centroid) and an update step (move each centroid to the mean of its assigned points). Assign → update → repeat.

$$\mu_j=\frac1{|C_j|}\sum_{x\in C_j}x\qquad\text{WCSS}=\sum_{j=1}^k\sum_{x\in C_j}\|x-\mu_j\|^2$$

WCSS (inertia, within-cluster sum of squares) uses squared distances — never take a square root here. Good clustering means low within-cluster SS and high between-cluster SS at the same time.

Q · Inertia. Four points: $(1,2),(2,1),(8,9),(9,8)$, clustered as $C_1=\{(1,2),(2,1)\}$, $C_2=\{(8,9),(9,8)\}$. Find the inertia.

Solution — step by step
  1. $\mu_1=(1.5,1.5)$. Each point contributes $(0.5)^2+(0.5)^2=0.5$, so cluster 1 totals $1$.
  2. $\mu_2=(8.5,8.5)$. Each point again contributes $0.5$, so cluster 2 totals $1$.
  3. $\text{WCSS}=1+1=2$.

Q · Better clustering. A k-means clustering becomes better when:

A. Within-cluster SS decreases and between-cluster SS increases.   B. Both increase.
C. Within-cluster SS increases and between-cluster SS decreases.   D. None.

Solution

Answer: A.

🤔 Why are k-means regions convex?

With centroids fixed, the points assigned to one centroid form a Voronoi region, which is convex: for any two points $a,b$ in the region, the whole segment $(1-t)a+tb$ for $0\le t\le1$ also lies in the region — including the midpoint $t=\tfrac12$, at $\tfrac{a+b}2$.

Q · Midpoint in a k-means region (real GATE). Euclidean k-means with $k=3$ is run on 100 points. Two points $\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}$ and $\begin{bmatrix}-1\\1\end{bmatrix}$ belong to cluster 3. Which point must also belong to cluster 3?

A. $\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\end{bmatrix}$   B. $\begin{bmatrix}0\\2\end{bmatrix}$   C. $\begin{bmatrix}2\\0\end{bmatrix}$   D. $\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix}$

Solution

Midpoint $=\tfrac12\left(\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix}-1\\1\end{bmatrix}\right)=\tfrac12\begin{bmatrix}0\\2\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix}$. A convex region must contain this midpoint.

Answer: D — the official GATE 2024 key marks option D.

🧠 Other k-means facts
  • Sensitive to feature scaling and to initial centroid placement; different starts can give different answers, and it may converge to a local rather than global optimum.
  • Works best for compact, roughly convex clusters; struggles with rings and moon-shaped clusters.
  • Increasing $k$ can never increase the best-achievable WCSS — the elbow method exploits exactly this to help choose $k$.
38 Cross-validation

Training set — learns model parameters. Validation set — chooses hyperparameters and model complexity. Test set — used once, at the very end, for final evaluation. Using test data during model selection causes leakage.

🧠 Trick to remember
  • Train teaches. Validation chooses. Test judges.

Parameters are learned from training data (regression coefficients, logistic coefficients, k-means centroids). Hyperparameters are chosen externally ($k$ in kNN, number of clusters, Ridge $\lambda$, number of folds, learning rate).

$$\text{CV score}=\frac1k\sum_{j=1}^ks_j$$

$k$-fold cross-validation divides model-selection data into $k$ folds, uses each as validation exactly once, and averages the scores. Advantages: every point is validated once, limited data is used efficiently, and results depend less on any one random split. Disadvantage: it requires repeated model fitting.

Q · Why use $k$-fold CV? $k$-fold cross-validation is particularly useful when data is scarce and generally gives a more reliable performance estimate than one train-test split, because several subsets are evaluated.

A. True   B. False

Solution

Answer: A.

$$\text{Number of fits}=g\times k\qquad(g\text{ hyperparameter combinations},\ k\text{ folds})$$

Q · Grid search. A dataset has 1200 observations. Five-fold CV is used with 12 hyperparameter combinations, one model fitted per combination per fold. How many models are trained?

Solution

$12\times5=60$. The figure $1200$ is a distractor — it plays no role in this count.

Leave-One-Out CV (LOOCV) is the special case $k=n$: each split trains on $n-1$ points and validates on the one left out, giving $n$ splits total.

Q · LOOCV equivalence. With $n$ data points, LOOCV is equivalent to $k$-fold CV when:

A. $k\lt n$   B. $k=n$   C. $k\gt n$   D. None

Solution

Answer: B.

⚠️ Held-out test data doesn't count toward LOOCV

If $N$ total observations include $T$ held out for final testing, LOOCV runs only on the remaining $N-T$ model-selection observations — the held-out test points never participate in any split.

Q · LOOCV after test split (real GATE). A classifier has 10 classes, 512-dimensional inputs, and 1000 observations; the first 100 are held out for final testing. LOOCV is used for model selection. How many validation splits are generated?

A. 10   B. 512   C. 900   D. 1000

Solution — step by step
  1. Model-selection observations: $1000-100=900$.
  2. LOOCV creates exactly one split per model-selection observation.

Answer: C — the number of classes and input dimensions are distractors.

🔍 Time-series data breaks the shuffling assumption

Time-series observations must preserve chronological order: train on the past, validate on the future (e.g. Jan–Mar → predict Apr, then Jan–Apr → predict May). Ordinary shuffled $k$-fold CV is inappropriate here because it can let future observations influence a model that's being evaluated on the past.

39 Final formula revision sheet

Everything on this page, compressed onto one shelf. Read top to bottom the morning of the exam.

Linear regression

$\hat y=\beta_0+\beta_1x$  ·  $\hat y=X\beta$  ·  with intercept: $\beta_1=\dfrac{\sum(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2}$, $\beta_0=\bar y-\beta_1\bar x$  ·  without intercept: $w=\dfrac{\sum x_iy_i}{\sum x_i^2}$

Errors

$e_i=y_i-\hat y_i$  ·  $\text{MAE}=\dfrac1n\sum|e_i|$  ·  $SSE=\sum e_i^2$  ·  $\text{MSE}=\dfrac{SSE}n$  ·  $\text{RMSE}=\sqrt{\text{MSE}}$

Variation and fit

$SST=SSR+SSE$  ·  $R^2=\dfrac{SSR}{SST}=1-\dfrac{SSE}{SST}$  ·  $SSE=SST(1-R^2)$

OLS

$X^TX\hat\beta=X^Ty$  ·  $\hat\beta=(X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty$  ·  $\hat\beta=X^+y$ (pseudoinverse, when needed)

Regularization

Ridge: $L+\lambda\sum_jw_j^2$  ·  Lasso: $L+\lambda\sum_j|w_j|$  ·  Gradient descent: $w_{\text{new}}=w-\eta\nabla J(w)$

Logistic regression

$z=\beta_0+\beta^Tx$  ·  $p=\dfrac1{1+e^{-z}}$  ·  $\log\dfrac{p}{1-p}=z$  ·  $\dfrac{dp}{dz}=p(1-p)$  ·  $\dfrac{dp}{dx}=\beta_1\,p(1-p)$  ·  max slope at $p=0.5,z=0$  ·  $L=\prod_ip_i^{y_i}(1-p_i)^{1-y_i}$

Classification metrics

$\text{Accuracy}=\dfrac{TP+TN}N$  ·  $\text{Precision}=\dfrac{TP}{TP+FP}$  ·  $\text{Recall}=\dfrac{TP}{TP+FN}$  ·  $\text{Specificity}=\dfrac{TN}{TN+FP}$  ·  $\text{FPR}=1-\text{Specificity}$  ·  $\text{Balanced accuracy}=\dfrac{\text{Sens}+\text{Spec}}2$

kNN

$d(a,b)=\sqrt{\sum_j(a_j-b_j)^2}$  ·  $w_i=\dfrac1{d_i^p}$

Nearest-centroid classifier

$\|\mu_r-x\|^2-\|\mu_g-x\|^2=2(\mu_g-\mu_r)^Tx+\|\mu_r\|^2-\|\mu_g\|^2$

k-means

$\mu_j=\dfrac1{|C_j|}\sum_{x\in C_j}x$  ·  $\text{WCSS}=\sum_j\sum_{x\in C_j}\|x-\mu_j\|^2$  ·  midpoint $=\dfrac{a+b}2$

Cross-validation

Grid-search fits $=$ combinations $\times$ folds  ·  LOOCV splits $=$ number of model-selection observations  ·  LOOCV after holdout $=N_{\text{total}}-N_{\text{test}}$

40 The final fifteen memory rules

Fifteen one-liners that carry the entire subject. Read them the morning of the exam — each one should instantly replay its section's story in your head. If one doesn't, that section wants ten more minutes of your time.

🧠 The fifteen rules
  • Regression predicts numbers; classification predicts classes.
  • $X$ is observations by features.
  • $y=wx$ and $y=b+wx$ use different slope formulas — never mix them up.
  • Residual is $y-\hat y$.
  • $SSE\to MSE\to RMSE$.
  • $R^2=1-SSE/SST$.
  • Perfect collinearity makes coefficients non-unique, not predictions.
  • Ridge is L2; Lasso is L1.
  • Total regularized loss is data loss plus penalty.
  • Logistic regression is linear in the log-odds, not in probability.
  • Sigmoid derivative is $p(1-p)$.
  • Precision looks at predicted positives; recall looks at actual positives.
  • kNN means calculate distances, sort, select and vote.
  • k-means means assign, update centroids, and minimize inertia.
  • LOOCV uses only non-test observations.
