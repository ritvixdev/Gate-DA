Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

8 questions. Tap any option — you'll see why it's right or wrong, and the correct one lights up.

Q1. Eigenvalues are $2,3,5$. Trace and determinant?
Swapped — trace is the sum, det is the product.
det is the product $=30$, not $10$.
✅ Correct — $\operatorname{tr}=\sum\lambda=10$, $\det=\prod\lambda=30$.
trace is the sum $=10$, not $30$.
Q2. $A$ is triangular with diagonal $1,4,7$. Eigenvalues?
✅ Correct — for triangular/diagonal matrices the eigenvalues are the diagonal entries.
$12$ is the trace, not the set of eigenvalues.
Diagonal entries are non-zero, so eigenvalues aren't $0$.
No need — triangular gives them for free.
Q3. Eigenvalues of $A$ are $2,3$. Eigenvalues of $A^{-1}$?
$4,9$ are eigenvalues of $A^2$ ($\lambda^2$).
Inversion gives reciprocals, not negatives.
✅ Correct — $A^{-1}$ has eigenvalues $1/\lambda$.
$A^{\mathsf T}$ keeps them; $A^{-1}$ flips to reciprocals.
Q4. Find the eigenvalues of $\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\2&3\end{bmatrix}$.
Eigenvalues aren't the diagonal unless triangular.
✅ Correct — $\lambda^2-7\lambda+10=0\Rightarrow(\lambda-5)(\lambda-2)=0$.
$7$ and $10$ are the trace and det, not the eigenvalues.
Solve $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$ → $5,2$.
Q5. Can the zero vector be an eigenvector?
No — eigenvectors are required to be non-zero.
✅ Correct — eigenvectors must be non-zero (though $\lambda$ itself can be $0$).
Even with $\lambda=0$ the eigenvector must be non-zero.
Symmetry doesn't change the non-zero requirement.
Q6. A real symmetric matrix always has…
Opposite — symmetric ⇒ all eigenvalues real.
Eigenvalues can differ; they're just guaranteed real.
✅ Correct — symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors.
No constraint forces $\det=1$.
Q7. If $0$ is an eigenvalue of $A$, then $A$ is…
Opposite — a zero eigenvalue means $\det=0$.
✅ Correct — $\det=\prod\lambda$ includes $0$, so $\det=0$ ⇒ singular.
Unrelated to orthogonality.
Unrelated to symmetry.
Q8. Eigenvalues of $A$ are $1,2$. Eigenvalues of $A^3$?
That's $3\lambda$; powers use $\lambda^k$, not $k\lambda$.
$2^3=8$, not $6$.
✅ Correct — $A^k$ has eigenvalues $\lambda^k$: $1^3=1$, $2^3=8$.
$A^3$ cubes each eigenvalue.
