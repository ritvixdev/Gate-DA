SVD & PCA

Every matrix — square or not — factors into rotate, stretch, rotate. That single fact powers dimensionality reduction across all of machine learning.

💡 Singular value decomposition

Any $m\times n$ matrix $A$ factors as

$$ A = U\,\Sigma\,V^{\mathsf T} $$
  • $U$ ($m\times m$) — orthogonal; columns are the left singular vectors.
  • $\Sigma$ ($m\times n$) — diagonal of singular values $\sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\ge\dots\ge0$.
  • $V$ ($n\times n$) — orthogonal; columns are the right singular vectors.

Geometrically: $V^{\mathsf T}$ rotates, $\Sigma$ stretches along axes, $U$ rotates again.

🔗 Where the pieces come from
$$ \sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^{\mathsf T}A)},\qquad V:\text{eigenvectors of }A^{\mathsf T}A,\qquad U:\text{eigenvectors of }AA^{\mathsf T} $$

Singular values are always real and non-negative because $A^{\mathsf T}A$ is symmetric positive semi-definite. The number of non-zero $\sigma_i$ equals $\operatorname{rank}(A)$.

🗜️ Low-rank approximation

Keeping only the largest $k$ singular values gives the best rank-$k$ approximation of $A$ (Eckart–Young):

$$ A \approx \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i\, u_i v_i^{\mathsf T} $$

This is the engine behind image compression, latent semantic analysis, and recommender systems.

📉 PCA — the ML connection

Principal Component Analysis finds the directions of maximum variance in centered data $X$. Those directions are the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix:

$$ C = \tfrac{1}{n-1} X^{\mathsf T} X,\qquad C = W \Lambda W^{\mathsf T} $$
  • Principal components = eigenvectors of $C$ (= right singular vectors of $X$).
  • Variance explained by each = its eigenvalue $\lambda_i$ (= $\sigma_i^2/(n-1)$).
  • Keep the top components to reduce dimensions while preserving the most variance.
⚡ GATE insight

PCA is the SVD of the centered data matrix. Running SVD on $X$ directly is the numerically stable way to do PCA.
