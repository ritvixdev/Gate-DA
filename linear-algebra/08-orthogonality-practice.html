Orthogonality

8 questions. Tap any option — you'll see why it's right or wrong, and the correct one lights up.

Q1. Are $(1,2)$ and $(2,-1)$ orthogonal?
✅ Correct — dot product $=1\cdot2+2\cdot(-1)=0$, so they're perpendicular.
The dot product is $0$, so they are orthogonal.
Orthogonality only needs dot $=0$; length doesn't matter.
Just compute the dot product — it's $0$.
Q2. "Orthonormal" means…
That's just "orthogonal" — orthonormal adds unit length.
Unit length alone isn't orthonormal.
✅ Correct — orthonormal = orthogonal + each vector has length $1$.
Orthogonal means perpendicular, the opposite of parallel.
Q3. For an orthogonal matrix $Q$, $Q^{\mathsf T}Q=?$
Only the zero matrix gives $0$.
That would need $Q^{\mathsf T}=I$.
✅ Correct — orthonormal columns give $Q^{\mathsf T}Q=I$, so $Q^{-1}=Q^{\mathsf T}$.
Orthogonality gives the identity, not $Q^2$.
Q4. An orthogonal transformation preserves…
It preserves a lot — it's a rigid motion.
It keeps more than direction.
✅ Correct — $\|Qx\|=\|x\|$, so lengths (and hence angles) are preserved.
Area too, but the key fact is lengths and angles.
Q5. Project $(3,4)$ onto $(1,0)$.
✅ Correct — $\frac{u^{\mathsf T}v}{u^{\mathsf T}u}u=\frac{3}{1}(1,0)=(3,0)$ (the $x$-shadow).
That's the projection onto the $y$-axis.
Projecting onto a line can't keep the full vector.
The coefficient is $u^{\mathsf T}v/u^{\mathsf T}u=3$, giving $(3,0)$.
Q6. A set of mutually orthogonal non-zero vectors is…
Opposite — orthogonality forces independence.
✅ Correct — orthogonal non-zero vectors are always linearly independent.
They can form a basis (an orthogonal basis).
Orthogonal doesn't imply unit length (that's orthonormal).
Q7. Gram–Schmidt is used to…
That's the characteristic equation, not Gram–Schmidt.
Not its purpose (though it gives $QR$).
✅ Correct — it turns any basis into an orthonormal one (and yields $A=QR$).
Unrelated to determinants.
Q8. $Q^{\mathsf T}Q=I$ implies $Q$ is…
Trap — it means $Q^{-1}=Q^{\mathsf T}$, not $Q=Q^{\mathsf T}$.
✅ Correct — the inverse is simply the transpose.
Orthogonal matrices are invertible ($\det=\pm1$).
That would need $Q^2=Q$.
