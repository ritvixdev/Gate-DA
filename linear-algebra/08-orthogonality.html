Orthogonality

Right angles make linear algebra easy: projections become simple, bases become stable, and inverses become transposes.

📐 Orthogonal & orthonormal

Two vectors are orthogonal when their dot product is zero. A set is orthonormal if its vectors are mutually orthogonal and each has unit length.

$$ u \cdot v = u^{\mathsf T} v = 0 \quad(\text{orthogonal}),\qquad q_i^{\mathsf T} q_j=\begin{cases}1 & i=j\\ 0 & i\neq j\end{cases}\ (\text{orthonormal}) $$

Orthogonal sets of non-zero vectors are automatically linearly independent.

🔦 Projection

The projection of $v$ onto the line spanned by $u$ is the “shadow” of $v$ along $u$:

$$ \operatorname{proj}_u(v) = \frac{u^{\mathsf T} v}{u^{\mathsf T} u}\, u $$

This is the closest point to $v$ on that line — the foundation of least-squares fitting.

🟦 Orthogonal matrices

A square matrix $Q$ is orthogonal when its columns are orthonormal:

$$ Q^{\mathsf T}Q = I \;\Rightarrow\; Q^{-1}=Q^{\mathsf T},\qquad \det Q=\pm1,\qquad \|Qx\|=\|x\| $$
⚡ GATE insight

Orthogonal matrices preserve length and angle — they are exactly the rotations and reflections. That is why $\|Qx\|=\|x\|$.
