Orthogonality

Right angles make everything easy: projections become simple, bases become stable, and inverses become transposes.

2 Why this topic exists

When directions are perpendicular they don't interfere, so calculations get clean and stable. Orthogonality powers least-squares fitting, QR factorization, and the orthogonal eigenvectors of symmetric matrices — and it's the backbone of PCA.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

Two arrows at $90°$ are orthogonal — neither casts any shadow on the other. A projection is the shadow one arrow drops onto another's line. An orthogonal matrix is a rigid move (rotation/reflection): it never stretches, so lengths and angles are preserved.

4 Basic definitions
  • Dot product: $u\cdot v=u^{\mathsf T}v=\sum u_iv_i$; zero means perpendicular.
  • Norm (length): $\|v\|=\sqrt{v\cdot v}$. Unit vector: a vector of length 1.
  • Angle: $\cos\theta = \dfrac{u\cdot v}{\|u\|\,\|v\|}$ between two vectors.
  • Orthogonal set: every pair is perpendicular. Orthonormal: orthogonal and each of unit length.
  • Projection: the closest point to $v$ on another vector's line (or onto a subspace).
  • Orthogonal matrix: square with orthonormal columns ($Q^{\mathsf T}Q=I$); it preserves lengths and angles.
  • Gram–Schmidt: a procedure that turns any basis into an orthonormal one.
📖 Study in Depth — orthogonality, projections & least squares from zero
1. What is orthogonality?

Orthogonal = perpendicular. The x-axis [1;0] and y-axis [0;1] meet at 90°, so they are orthogonal.

The power of linear algebra: orthogonality works in any dimension (ℝ¹⁰, ℝ¹⁰⁰⁰), not just 2D/3D — which is why it underpins data science.

Plain idea: orthogonal directions do not disturb each other.

2. The dot product 
u·v = u₁v₁ + u₂v₂ + … + uₙvₙ = uᵀv

For column vectors, uᵀ is a row, so uᵀv multiplies out to the sum of products.

3. Dot product example 
u = [1; 2] ,  v = [3; 4]
u·v = 1×3 + 2×4 = 3 + 8 = 11
4. The orthogonality condition 
u ⊥ v  ⟺  u·v = 0
[1;0]·[0;1] = 0  → perpendicular
5. Finding a perpendicular vector 
u = [1; 2] ,  v = [2; −1]
u·v = 1×2 + 2×(−1) = 0  → orthogonal

In 2D, a vector perpendicular to [a; b] is [b; −a] (or [−b; a]) — swap and negate one.

6. Dot product & angle 
u·v = ||u|| ||v|| cos θ

If θ = 90°, then cos 90° = 0, so u·v = 0. That is exactly why a zero dot product means perpendicular.

7. Norm (length) of a vector 
||v|| = √(v₁² + v₂² + … + vₙ²) = √(v·v)
v = [3; 4] → ||v|| = √(9+16) = √25 = 5

It is just Pythagoras in n dimensions.

8. Unit vector

A unit vector has length 1.

[1;0] and [0;1] both have norm 1 → unit vectors
9. Normalization (make length 1)

Divide a vector by its norm:

v = [3; 4] ,  ||v|| = 5
u = v/||v|| = [3/5; 4/5]   (check: √(9/25+16/25) = 1)

This process is called normalization.

10. Orthogonal vs orthonormal 
orthogonal : u·v = 0                 (e.g. [2;0] & [0;5])
orthonormal: orthogonal AND each length 1  (e.g. [1;0] & [0;1])

Memory: orthonormal = orthogonal + normalized.

11. Orthogonal basis

A basis whose vectors are mutually orthogonal. The cleanest example is the standard basis of ℝ²: [1;0], [0;1] (orthonormal).

🎮 Game terms: moving right never accidentally moves you up — the directions do not interfere.

12. Orthogonal ⇒ independent

Non-zero, mutually orthogonal vectors are automatically linearly independent.

v₁·v₂ = v₁·v₃ = v₂·v₃ = 0 (all non-zero)  ⇒  independent

None can be built from the others when they point in completely separate directions.

13. Inner product

The dot product is the standard inner product: ⟨u,v⟩ = u·v = uᵀv. For GATE DA, treat them as the same thing.

14. Projection — the core idea

Projection = the shadow of one vector onto another. With light falling straight down, vector b casts a shadow on vector a:

proj_a(b) = the part of b lying along a
15. Projection of b onto a (formula) 
proj_a(b) = (a·b / a·a) a = (a·b / ||a||²) a

The denominator a·a = ||a||² adjusts for the length of a.

16. Projection example + the error vector 
a = [1; 0] ,  b = [3; 4]
a·b = 3 ,  a·a = 1  →  proj = 3·[1;0] = [3; 0]

error e = b − proj = [0; 4]   and  e·a = 0  → e ⊥ a

Key idea: b = projection + orthogonal error.

17. Projection onto a unit vector

If a is not unit length the denominator handles it; a=[2;0], b=[3;4] still gives [3;0].

If u is a unit vector, u·u = 1, so it simplifies:

proj_u(b) = (u·b) u

For an orthonormal basis, the coefficient along uᵢ is just b·uᵢ — no denominator. That is why orthonormal bases are so clean.

18. Projection onto a subspace

To project b onto the column space of A (independent columns):

p = A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ b
projection matrix  P = A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ ,   p = Pb

(AᵀA is invertible when A's columns are independent.)

19. Projection matrix & its properties 
P² = P     (idempotent — projecting twice changes nothing)
Pᵀ = P     (orthogonal projection is symmetric)
eigenvalues of P are 0 or 1   (since λ² = λ)

λ = 1 directions are kept (already in the subspace); λ = 0 directions are removed. The error e = b − Pb is orthogonal to the subspace, so Aᵀe = 0 → least squares.

20. The least-squares problem

When Ax = b has no exact solution (overdetermined: more equations than unknowns), find the best approximation:

minimize ||Ax − b||²    (make the error e = b − Ax as small as possible)

This is the heart of fitting models to data.

21. Least-squares geometry

Ax always lies in the column space of A, but b may not. The closest point is the projection of b onto C(A):

p = Ax̂ ,  error e = b − Ax̂ ⊥ C(A)  ⇒  Aᵀe = 0  ⇒  Aᵀ(b − Ax̂) = 0
22. The normal equations 
AᵀA x̂ = Aᵀb        (the normal equations)
if AᵀA invertible:  x̂ = (AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀb
projection:  p = A x̂ = A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ b

This is exactly where the projection matrix P = A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ comes from.

23. Least squares in linear regression 
Xβ ≈ y   (X = features, β = parameters, y = targets)
normal equation:  XᵀX β = Xᵀy   →   β = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy

The residual e = y − Xβ is orthogonal to the column space of X (Xᵀe = 0). This is why orthogonality powers data science.

24. Gram–Schmidt — the idea

Gram–Schmidt turns a set of independent vectors into an orthonormal basis: messy independent directions → clean perpendicular unit vectors.

keep v₁'s direction → remove from v₂ the part along v₁ → normalize the leftover → repeat
25. Gram–Schmidt — the formula 
u₁ = v₁ / ||v₁||
w₂ = v₂ − (v₂·u₁)u₁ ,        u₂ = w₂ / ||w₂||
w₃ = v₃ − (v₃·u₁)u₁ − (v₃·u₂)u₂ , u₃ = w₃ / ||w₃||

general:  wₖ = vₖ − Σ_{i
26. Gram–Schmidt — worked example 
v₁ = [1; 0] ,  v₂ = [1; 1]

u₁ = [1; 0]
v₂·u₁ = 1 → w₂ = [1;1] − 1·[1;0] = [0; 1] → u₂ = [0; 1]

orthonormal basis: [1;0], [0;1]

Messy vectors became clean x–y axes.

27. Orthogonal matrix

A matrix Q is orthogonal when its columns are orthonormal:

QᵀQ = I ;   if Q is square, Q⁻¹ = Qᵀ
Q = [0 −1; 1 0]  (90° rotation): columns [0;1], [−1;0] — unit & perpendicular
28. Why QᵀQ = I

The (i, j) entry of QᵀQ is qᵢ·qⱼ. For orthonormal columns: qᵢ·qᵢ = 1 and qᵢ·qⱼ = 0 (i ≠ j) — exactly the identity matrix.

29. Properties of orthogonal matrices
  • QᵀQ = QQᵀ = I,   Q⁻¹ = Qᵀ (square Q).
  • det(Q) = ±1.
  • ||Qx|| = ||x|| (lengths preserved).
  • (Qu)·(Qv) = u·v (angles preserved).
  • eigenvalues satisfy |λ| = 1.

They represent rotations, reflections, or combinations.

30. Length & angle preservation (proofs) 
||Qx||² = (Qx)ᵀ(Qx) = xᵀQᵀQx = xᵀIx = xᵀx = ||x||²
(Qu)·(Qv) = uᵀQᵀQv = uᵀv = u·v

🎮 Imagine: rotate/flip an object without stretching it.

31. Determinant of an orthogonal matrix 
QᵀQ = I ⇒ det(Qᵀ)det(Q) = 1 ⇒ det(Q)² = 1 ⇒ det(Q) = ±1

+1 = rotation (orientation kept); −1 = reflection (orientation flipped).

32. Eigenvalues of an orthogonal matrix 
Qx = λx ⇒ ||Qx|| = |λ|·||x||, but ||Qx|| = ||x|| ⇒ |λ| = 1

Real eigenvalues can only be ±1; complex ones lie on the unit circle.

33. Orthogonal matrix vs orthogonal vectors 
orthogonal vectors:  u·v = 0
orthogonal matrix:   QᵀQ = I  (columns orthonormal)

An orthogonal matrix is NOT "a matrix with dot product zero" — it is a matrix whose columns are unit perpendicular directions.

34. Orthogonal complement (W⊥)

W⊥ ("W perp") = all vectors perpendicular to every vector in W.

W = x-axis = span{[1;0]}   →   W⊥ = y-axis = span{[0;1]}

The four-subspace relations (Row ⊥ Null, Col ⊥ Left-null) are the big example — see the Rank & Nullity topic.

35. Symmetric matrices & orthogonality

For a real symmetric matrix (A = Aᵀ), eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal, so they can be chosen orthonormal.

That gives orthogonal diagonalization A = QDQᵀ (the spectral theorem) — built out fully in the Diagonalization topic.

36. PCA connection (preview)

A covariance matrix is symmetric, so its eigenvectors are orthonormal — these become the principal components: orthogonal directions of maximum variance (PC1 the most, PC2 ⊥ PC1 the next, …). Full PCA lives in the SVD & PCA topic.

37. QR decomposition (preview) GATE extra

Gram–Schmidt on the columns of A produces:

A = Q R     (Q orthonormal columns, R upper triangular)

Used for stable least squares and numerical linear algebra. Know the idea: Q comes from orthonormalizing A's columns.

38. Cauchy–Schwarz inequality 
|u·v| ≤ ||u|| · ||v||     (equality ⟺ u and v are parallel)

It follows from u·v = ||u|| ||v|| cos θ and |cos θ| ≤ 1. It guarantees the cosine/correlation between two vectors stays in [−1, 1].

39. Reflection & Householder matrices GATE extra

Besides rotations, orthogonal matrices include reflections. A Householder matrix reflects across the hyperplane perpendicular to a unit vector v:

H = I − 2 v vᵀ        (v a unit vector)

Properties: H is symmetric and orthogonal, H² = I (its own inverse), and det(H) = −1 (orientation flips). Householder reflections are the workhorse of QR decomposition.

40. Common GATE traps
  • "Orthogonal means length 1"? No — orthogonal = dot product 0; length 1 = unit; both = orthonormal.
  • u·v = 0 ⇒ one vector is zero? No ([1;0]·[0;1]=0, both non-zero).
  • Normal equation is Ax = b? No — it is AᵀA x = Aᵀb.
  • det(Q) = 1 only? No — ±1.
  • Non-zero orthogonal vectors can be dependent? No — they are independent.
  • Projection matrix P² = P, orthogonal projection Pᵀ = P, residual ⊥ C(A)? All true.

42. GATE cheatsheet
  • u·v = uᵀv;   ||v|| = √(v·v);   orthogonal ⟺ u·v = 0.
  • Orthonormal = orthogonal + unit; non-zero orthogonal vectors are independent.
  • proj_a(b) = (aᵀb/aᵀa)a; unit a → (aᵀb)a.
  • Projection matrix P = A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ; P² = P, Pᵀ = P, eigenvalues 0/1.
  • Least squares: minimize ||Ax−b||² → normal equations AᵀAx̂ = Aᵀb; residual ⊥ C(A).
  • Orthogonal Q: QᵀQ = I, Q⁻¹ = Qᵀ, det = ±1, |λ| = 1, preserves length & angle.
  • Gram–Schmidt → orthonormal basis → A = QR; symmetric → A = QDQᵀ.
43. Best mental model
  • Orthogonality = clean 90° directions that don't interfere.
  • Dot product = interference test (0 = none); norm = length.
  • Orthonormal basis = the perfect coordinate system.
  • Projection = shadow onto a direction/subspace; least squares = closest shadow when no exact solution exists.
  • Orthogonal matrix = rotation/reflection that preserves shape.

6 Main formulas

Dot product & length

Perpendicular test & norm — a zero dot product means perpendicular:

$$ u\cdot v=0 \iff u\perp v,\qquad \|v\|=\sqrt{v\cdot v} $$

Unit vector — scale to length 1:

$$ \hat v=\frac{v}{\|v\|} $$

Projection & orthogonal matrices

Projection of $v$ onto $u$ — the closest point on $u$'s line:

$$ \operatorname{proj}_u v=\frac{u^{\mathsf T}v}{u^{\mathsf T}u}\,u $$

Orthogonal matrix — orthonormal columns; its inverse is its transpose and it preserves length:

$$ Q^{\mathsf T}Q=I \Rightarrow Q^{-1}=Q^{\mathsf T},\quad \det Q=\pm1,\quad \|Qx\|=\|x\| $$
7 Step-by-step: Gram–Schmidt
  1. Keep the first vector: $u_1=v_1$.
  2. For each next $v_k$, subtract its projections onto all previous $u_j$.
  3. That remainder $u_k$ is orthogonal to all earlier ones.
  4. Normalize each $u_i$ (divide by its length) to get an orthonormal basis.
8 Worked example
✦ Projection

Project $v=(3,4)$ onto $u=(1,0)$:   $\operatorname{proj}_u v=\dfrac{u^{\mathsf T}v}{u^{\mathsf T}u}u=\dfrac{3}{1}(1,0)=(3,0)$.

The shadow of $(3,4)$ on the x-axis is $(3,0)$ — exactly the $x$-part.

9 GATE cheat sheet
⚡ High-value facts
  • Orthogonal matrix: $Q^{-1}=Q^{\mathsf T}$ (inverse for free), $\det=\pm1$, preserves length.
  • Orthogonal sets of non-zero vectors are automatically independent.
  • Symmetric matrices have orthogonal eigenvectors (for distinct $\lambda$).
  • Gram–Schmidt gives the $QR$ factorization $A=QR$.
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "Orthogonal = orthonormal." Reality: orthonormal also requires unit length.

Trap: "$Q^{\mathsf T}Q=I$ means $Q$ is symmetric." Reality: it means $Q^{-1}=Q^{\mathsf T}$, not $Q=Q^{\mathsf T}$.

Trap: "Projection changes a vector's length unpredictably." Reality: a projection never lengthens; it gives the closest point.
