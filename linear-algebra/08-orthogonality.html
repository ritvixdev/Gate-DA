Orthogonality

Right angles make everything easy: projections become simple, bases become stable, and inverses become transposes.

2 Why this topic exists

When directions are perpendicular they don't interfere, so calculations get clean and stable. Orthogonality powers least-squares fitting, QR factorization, and the orthogonal eigenvectors of symmetric matrices — and it's the backbone of PCA.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

Two arrows at $90°$ are orthogonal — neither casts any shadow on the other. A projection is the shadow one arrow drops onto another's line. An orthogonal matrix is a rigid move (rotation/reflection): it never stretches, so lengths and angles are preserved.

4 Basic definitions

Dot product: $u\cdot v=u^{\mathsf T}v=\sum u_iv_i$. Zero means perpendicular.

Orthogonal set: every pair is perpendicular. Orthonormal: orthogonal and each has length 1.

Projection: the closest point to $v$ on another vector's line.

Orthogonal matrix: a square matrix whose columns are orthonormal ($Q^{\mathsf T}Q=I$).

6 Main formulas
$$ u \cdot v = 0 \iff u \perp v,\qquad \|v\|=\sqrt{v\cdot v} $$
$$ \operatorname{proj}_u v = \frac{u^{\mathsf T}v}{u^{\mathsf T}u}\,u $$
$$ Q^{\mathsf T}Q=I \;\Rightarrow\; Q^{-1}=Q^{\mathsf T},\quad \det Q=\pm1,\quad \|Qx\|=\|x\| $$
7 Step-by-step: Gram–Schmidt
  1. Keep the first vector: $u_1=v_1$.
  2. For each next $v_k$, subtract its projections onto all previous $u_j$.
  3. That remainder $u_k$ is orthogonal to all earlier ones.
  4. Normalize each $u_i$ (divide by its length) to get an orthonormal basis.
8 Worked example
✦ Projection

Project $v=(3,4)$ onto $u=(1,0)$:   $\operatorname{proj}_u v=\dfrac{u^{\mathsf T}v}{u^{\mathsf T}u}u=\dfrac{3}{1}(1,0)=(3,0)$.

The shadow of $(3,4)$ on the x-axis is $(3,0)$ — exactly the $x$-part.

9 GATE shortcuts
⚡ High-value facts
  • Orthogonal matrix: $Q^{-1}=Q^{\mathsf T}$ (inverse for free), $\det=\pm1$, preserves length.
  • Orthogonal sets of non-zero vectors are automatically independent.
  • Symmetric matrices have orthogonal eigenvectors (for distinct $\lambda$).
  • Gram–Schmidt gives the $QR$ factorization $A=QR$.
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "Orthogonal = orthonormal." Reality: orthonormal also requires unit length.

Trap: "$Q^{\mathsf T}Q=I$ means $Q$ is symmetric." Reality: it means $Q^{-1}=Q^{\mathsf T}$, not $Q=Q^{\mathsf T}$.

Trap: "Projection changes a vector's length unpredictably." Reality: a projection never lengthens; it gives the closest point.

11 Connections with other topics
  • Vectors: orthogonal sets are a special, well-behaved kind of independent set.
  • Eigenvalues: symmetric matrices → orthogonal eigenvectors.
  • Diagonalization: symmetric ⇒ orthogonal diagonalization $A=QDQ^{\mathsf T}$.
  • SVD & PCA: $U$ and $V$ are orthogonal; principal axes are orthogonal.
12 Practice problems

Q1. Are $(1,2)$ and $(2,-1)$ orthogonal?

Dot $=1\cdot2+2\cdot(-1)=0$ → yes, orthogonal.

Q2. For orthogonal $Q$, what is $\det Q$?

$\pm1$.

Q3. Project $(0,5)$ onto $(0,1)$.

$\dfrac{5}{1}(0,1)=(0,5)$ — it's already on that line.

Quick MCQ

If $Q$ is orthogonal, what is $Q^{\mathsf T}Q$?
Only the zero matrix gives $0$.
That needs $Q^{\mathsf T}=I$.
✅ Correct — orthonormal columns give $Q^{\mathsf T}Q=I$, so $Q^{-1}=Q^{\mathsf T}$.
No — orthogonality gives the identity.
13 Final memory box
🧠 Remember
  • Dot product $0$ ⇒ perpendicular; orthonormal = orthogonal + unit length.
  • $\operatorname{proj}_u v=\dfrac{u^{\mathsf T}v}{u^{\mathsf T}u}u$ (the shadow).
  • Orthogonal $Q$: $Q^{-1}=Q^{\mathsf T}$, $\det=\pm1$, lengths preserved.
  • Gram–Schmidt builds an orthonormal basis ⇒ $QR$.

GATE takeaway — for an orthogonal matrix Q, Q⁻¹ = Qᵀ and lengths are preserved (‖Qx‖ = ‖x‖).
