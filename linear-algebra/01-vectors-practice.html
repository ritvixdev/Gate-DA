Vectors & Spaces

8 questions. Tap any option — you'll see why it's right or wrong, and the correct one lights up.

Q1. Which set is not a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^2$?
A line through the origin — contains $0$ and is closed. It is a subspace.
The trivial subspace $\{0\}$ — it is a subspace.
$\mathbb{R}^2$ is a subspace of itself.
✅ Correct — $x+y=1$ excludes $(0,0)$, so it can't be a subspace.
Q2. $\dim\big(\operatorname{span}\{(1,2,3),(2,4,6)\}\big)=?$
✅ Correct — the second vector is $2\times$ the first, so they're parallel; the span is a line.
They're dependent (one is a multiple of the other), so the span is 1-D, not 2-D.
Two vectors span at most 2-D, and here they're dependent → 1-D.
Both vectors are non-zero, so the span is at least 1-D.
Q3. In $\mathbb{R}^3$, how many vectors can be linearly independent at most?
Too few — the standard basis already gives 3 independent vectors.
In $\mathbb{R}^3$ you can have 3 (e.g. the axes).
✅ Correct — $\dim(\mathbb{R}^n)=n$, so at most $n=3$; any 4 vectors are dependent.
Impossible — more than $n$ vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ are always dependent.
Q4. Are $(1,0),(0,1),(4,5)$ linearly independent?
No — 3 vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$ can't all be independent.
✅ Correct — 3 vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$ ($>n$) are always dependent. Indeed $(4,5)=4(1,0)+5(0,1)$.
You can tell instantly: count $>n$ ⇒ dependent.
Independence doesn't require any vector to be zero.
Q5. A set of vectors is a basis when it is…
Spanning alone may include redundant vectors.
Independent alone may not reach the whole space.
✅ Correct — a basis is the minimal team: independent and spans.
A basis is minimal, not maximal — no redundant vectors.
Q6. Which is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^2$?
✅ Correct — a line through the origin; contains $0$ and is closed under $+$ and scaling.
A half-plane isn't closed under scaling by $-1$ — not a subspace.
A circle doesn't contain $(0,0)$ — not a subspace.
The two axes together aren't closed under addition (e.g. $(1,0)+(0,1)$).
Q7. $(5,7)$ as a linear combination of $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$ is…
Coefficients are swapped — that gives $(7,5)$.
✅ Correct — the coefficients are exactly the components: $(5,7)=5(1,0)+7(0,1)$.
That gives $(5,-7)$.
The standard basis spans $\mathbb{R}^2$, so every vector is such a combination.
Q8. What is $\dim(\mathbb{R}^5)$?
That's a line, not all of $\mathbb{R}^5$.
Off by one — the standard basis has 5 vectors.
✅ Correct — $\dim(\mathbb{R}^n)=n$, so $\dim(\mathbb{R}^5)=5$.
$\mathbb{R}^5$ is finite-dimensional with dimension 5.
