Determinants

One number that tells you whether a matrix is invertible, how much it scales area/volume, and whether it flips space.

2 Why this topic exists

The determinant squeezes a whole square matrix into a single number that answers the most important yes/no question: is this matrix invertible? If $\det=0$, the matrix crushes space and can't be undone. It also measures how much the transformation scales area (2-D) or volume (3-D), and its sign tells you if orientation flipped.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

Take the unit square and push it through the matrix portal. $|\det|$ = the area of the squished/stretched shape. $\det=2$ → area doubles. $\det=0$ → the square collapses to a line (information lost, not invertible). $\det<0$ → space got flipped like a mirror.

Use the Matrix Transformer: the shaded square's area is $|\det|$; it turns coral when $\det<0$ (flip), and collapses to a line on the Singular preset ($\det=0$).

4 Basic definitions

Determinant: a single number $\det(A)$ (also written $|A|$) defined only for square matrices.

Singular: $\det(A)=0$ — not invertible, columns are dependent. Non-singular: $\det(A)\neq0$ — invertible.

Minor / cofactor: a minor is the determinant of a smaller matrix left after deleting a row and column; a cofactor is a minor with a $+/-$ sign from the checkerboard pattern.

6 Main formulas
$$ \det\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}=ad-bc $$
$$ \det\begin{bmatrix}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i\end{bmatrix}=a(ei-fh)-b(di-fg)+c(dh-eg) $$

Cofactor signs follow the checkerboard $\begin{smallmatrix}+&-&+\\-&+&-\\+&-&+\end{smallmatrix}$.

7 Step-by-step (3×3 by cofactors)
  1. Pick a row (row 1 is fine). Take its three entries $a,b,c$.
  2. For each, cross out its row and column; the leftover $2\times2$ determinant is its minor.
  3. Apply the signs $+,-,+$ along the row.
  4. Compute $a(\text{minor}) - b(\text{minor}) + c(\text{minor})$ and add up.
  5. Shortcut: if the matrix is triangular, just multiply the diagonal.
8 Worked example
✦ Compute

$\det\begin{bmatrix}2&1&3\\0&4&2\\1&0&5\end{bmatrix}=2(4\cdot5-2\cdot0)-1(0\cdot5-2\cdot1)+3(0\cdot0-4\cdot1)$

$=2(20)-1(-2)+3(-4)=40+2-12=\mathbf{30}$.

9 GATE shortcuts
⚡ High-value facts
  • $\det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B)$;   $\det(A^{\mathsf T})=\det(A)$;   $\det(A^{-1})=1/\det(A)$.
  • $\det(cA)=c^{\,n}\det(A)$ for an $n\times n$ matrix (the size is the exponent!).
  • Triangular / diagonal → $\det=$ product of the diagonal.
  • $\det(A)=\prod \lambda_i$ (product of eigenvalues).
  • Swap two rows → sign flips; add a multiple of one row to another → $\det$ unchanged.
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "$\det(2A)=2\det(A)$." Reality: $\det(2A)=2^n\det(A)$ — e.g. $2^3$ for a $3\times3$.

Trap: "$\det(A+B)=\det(A)+\det(B)$." Reality: false in general.

Trap: "Non-square matrices have determinants." Reality: only square matrices do.

11 Connections with other topics
  • Inverse: $A^{-1}$ exists iff $\det(A)\neq0$.
  • Rank: $\det=0$ means rank $
  • Eigenvalues: $\det=\prod\lambda$; a zero eigenvalue ⇒ $\det=0$.
  • Linear equations: $\det(A)\neq0$ ⇒ $Ax=b$ has a unique solution.
12 Practice problems

Q1. $\det(A)=5$ for a $3\times3$ matrix. Find $\det(2A)$.

$\det(2A)=2^3\cdot5=40$.

Q2. $\det\begin{bmatrix}3&0&0\\7&2&0\\1&9&5\end{bmatrix}=?$

Lower-triangular → product of diagonal $=3\cdot2\cdot5=30$.

Q3. If $\det(A)=4$ and $\det(B)=3$, find $\det(AB)$ and $\det(A^{-1})$.

$\det(AB)=12$; $\det(A^{-1})=1/4$.

13 Final memory box
🧠 Remember
  • $|\det|$ = area/volume scale; sign = orientation; $\det=0$ ⇒ collapse, not invertible.
  • $2\times2$: $ad-bc$. Triangular: product of diagonal.
  • $\det(AB)=\det A\det B$, $\det(A^{\mathsf T})=\det A$, $\det(A^{-1})=1/\det A$.
  • $\det(cA)=c^n\det A$; $\det=\prod\lambda_i$.

GATE takeaway — det(cA) = cⁿ·det(A), and a triangular matrix's det is the product of its diagonal.
