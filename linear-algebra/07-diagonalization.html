Diagonalization

Rewrite a matrix in its eigenvector basis, where it acts as simple scaling. This turns hard operations like high powers into one-liners.

📊 The factorization

A square matrix $A$ is diagonalizable if it can be written

$$ A = P D P^{-1} $$

where $D$ is diagonal with the eigenvalues on its diagonal, and the columns of $P$ are the corresponding eigenvectors.

$$ P=\big[\,x_1\ \ x_2\ \cdots\ x_n\,\big],\qquad D=\operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots,\lambda_n) $$
✅ When is it possible?
  • $A$ ($n\times n$) is diagonalizable $\iff$ it has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors.
  • If all $n$ eigenvalues are distinct, $A$ is automatically diagonalizable.
  • Every symmetric matrix is diagonalizable — in fact orthogonally: $A=QDQ^{\mathsf T}$ with $Q^{\mathsf T}Q=I$.
  • A repeated eigenvalue is fine only if its geometric multiplicity matches its algebraic multiplicity.
⚡ GATE check

Failure case: $\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$ has $\lambda=1$ twice but only one independent eigenvector — not diagonalizable (it is defective).

⚡ Why it's useful — matrix powers

Because the $P^{-1}P$ pairs cancel, powers collapse to powering the diagonal:

$$ A^k = P D^k P^{-1},\qquad D^k=\operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1^{k},\dots,\lambda_n^{k}) $$
✦ Worked example

$A=\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\0&3\end{bmatrix}$ is already diagonal, so $A^{10}=\begin{bmatrix}2^{10}&0\\0&3^{10}\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1024&0\\0&59049\end{bmatrix}$. For a general diagonalizable $A$, change to the eigenbasis, raise to the power, change back.

🧠 Big idea

$P^{-1}$ rotates into the eigenvector world, $D^k$ just stretches, $P$ rotates back. Hard work becomes easy work in the right basis.

Practice

Q1. Which matrix is not diagonalizable?
Diagonal matrices are already diagonalized.
Symmetric matrices are always diagonalizable.
✅ Correct — repeated $\lambda=1$ with only one independent eigenvector; it is defective.
Distinct (complex) eigenvalues ⇒ diagonalizable over $\mathbb{C}$.

GATE takeaway — A is diagonalizable iff it has n independent eigenvectors; distinct eigenvalues guarantee it.
