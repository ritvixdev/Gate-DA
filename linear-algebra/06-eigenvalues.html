Eigenvalues &
Eigenvectors

The single most important topic in GATE DA linear algebra. Eigenvalues reveal the intrinsic behaviour of a linear transformation.

🌀 The core idea

An eigenvector of $A$ is a non-zero vector $x$ that only gets scaled — not rotated — when multiplied by $A$. The scale factor is the eigenvalue $\lambda$.

$$ A x = \lambda x, \qquad x \neq 0 $$

🔍 Finding eigenvalues

Eigenvalues are the roots of the characteristic equation:

$$ \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 $$

Then for each $\lambda$, solve $(A-\lambda I)x=0$ to get its eigenvectors.

✦ Full example

$A=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\2&3\end{bmatrix}$, so $\det(A-\lambda I)=(4-\lambda)(3-\lambda)-2=\lambda^2-7\lambda+10=0$.

$(\lambda-5)(\lambda-2)=0 \Rightarrow \lambda_1=5,\ \lambda_2=2$.

$\lambda=5$: $(A-5I)x=0 \Rightarrow x=(1,1)^{\mathsf T}$.   $\lambda=2$: $x=(1,-2)^{\mathsf T}$.

📋 Key properties — GATE gold
$$ \textstyle\sum \lambda_i = \operatorname{tr}(A), \qquad \prod \lambda_i = \det(A) $$

If $A$ has eigenvalue $\lambda$ (with eigenvector $x$), then:

  • $A^k$ has eigenvalue $\lambda^k$;   $A^{-1}$ has $1/\lambda$.
  • $A+cI$ has $\lambda+c$;   $cA$ has $c\lambda$.
  • $A^{\mathsf T}$ has the same eigenvalues as $A$.
⚡ Symmetric matrices ($A=A^{\mathsf T}$)

All eigenvalues are real, eigenvectors for distinct $\lambda$ are orthogonal, and the matrix is always diagonalizable.

Practice

Q1. If the eigenvalues of $A$ are $2$ and $3$, what are the eigenvalues of $A^{-1}$?
Inversion gives reciprocals, not negatives.
✅ Correct — $A^{-1}$ has eigenvalues $1/\lambda$, i.e. $\tfrac12$ and $\tfrac13$.
$4,9$ would be the eigenvalues of $A^2$.
$A^{-1}$ flips each eigenvalue to its reciprocal.

GATE takeaway — Σλ = trace, Πλ = det, and Aᵏ has eigenvalues λᵏ — these appear almost every year.
