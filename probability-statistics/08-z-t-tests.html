Hypothesis Testing:
z-test & t-test

A courtroom for data: assume "nothing's going on", then check whether the evidence is surprising enough to reject that assumption.

2 Why this topic exists

Is a new drug actually better, or did we just get lucky? Hypothesis testing gives a disciplined yes/no: assume no effect (the null), measure how extreme the data is under that assumption, and reject only if it's too unlikely to be chance.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

It's a trial. The null hypothesis $H_0$ is "innocent" (no effect). The test statistic measures how far your data is from $H_0$ in standard-error units. The p-value is "how often pure chance would give evidence this extreme". Small p ⇒ the evidence is surprising ⇒ reject $H_0$.

4 Basic definitions

Null $H_0$: the "no effect / no difference" claim. Alternative $H_1$: what you suspect.

Significance level $\alpha$: the risk of a false alarm you'll tolerate (often 0.05).

p-value: probability of data this extreme if $H_0$ were true. Reject $H_0$ when p $\lt\alpha$.

z-test: population $\sigma$ known (or large $n$). t-test: $\sigma$ unknown, estimated by $s$ (small samples).

6 Main formulas
$$ z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\quad(\sigma\text{ known}) \qquad t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}\quad(\sigma\text{ unknown}) $$

The $t$ statistic uses $n-1$ degrees of freedom. Reject $H_0$ if $|z|$ (or $|t|$) exceeds the critical value, or equivalently if p $\lt\alpha$.

7 Step-by-step method
  1. State $H_0$ and $H_1$ (one- or two-tailed) and pick $\alpha$.
  2. Choose the test: $\sigma$ known / large $n$ → z; $\sigma$ unknown / small $n$ → t.
  3. Compute the test statistic from $\bar{x},\mu_0,$ and the standard error.
  4. Find the p-value (or compare to the critical value).
  5. If p $\lt\alpha$, reject $H_0$; otherwise fail to reject.
8 Worked example
✦ z-test

Claim $\mu_0=100$. Sample: $\bar{x}=104$, $\sigma=15$, $n=36$. SE $=15/6=2.5$.

$z=(104-100)/2.5=1.6$. At $\alpha=0.05$ two-tailed the critical $z$ is $1.96$. Since $1.6\lt1.96$, fail to reject $H_0$.

9 GATE shortcuts
⚡ High-value facts
  • $\sigma$ known / large $n$ → z; $\sigma$ unknown / small $n$ → t (with $n-1$ df).
  • Two-tailed critical $z$: $1.96$ at $\alpha=0.05$, $2.58$ at $\alpha=0.01$.
  • Reject when p $\lt\alpha$ (equivalently $|z|\gt z_{\alpha/2}$).
  • The t-distribution is fatter-tailed than z and approaches z as $n$ grows.
  • Type I error = reject a true $H_0$ (rate $\alpha$); Type II = fail to reject a false $H_0$ (rate $\beta$).
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "p-value = probability $H_0$ is true." Reality: it's $P(\text{data}\mid H_0)$, not $P(H_0\mid\text{data})$.

Trap: "Fail to reject = $H_0$ is proven true." Reality: just not enough evidence against it.

Trap: "Use z when $\sigma$ is unknown." Reality: use t (estimate with $s$).

12 Practice problems

Q1. $\bar{x}=52,\ \mu_0=50,\ \sigma=8,\ n=64$. Find $z$.

SE $=8/8=1$; $z=(52-50)/1=2$.

Q2. At $\alpha=0.05$ two-tailed, is $z=2$ significant?

Yes — $|2|\gt1.96$, so reject $H_0$.

Q3. Population $\sigma$ unknown and $n=12$. z or t?

t-test, with $n-1=11$ degrees of freedom.

Quick MCQ

You use a t-test (instead of z) when…
Large samples lean toward z (t approaches z anyway).
Known $\sigma$ → z-test.
✅ Correct — unknown $\sigma$ (estimated by $s$), especially small $n$ → t-test.
Categorical data uses chi-square, not t.
13 Final memory box
🧠 Remember
  • $H_0$ = no effect; reject when p $\lt\alpha$.
  • $z=(\bar x-\mu_0)/(\sigma/\sqrt n)$; $t$ same with $s$, $n-1$ df.
  • z: $\sigma$ known / large $n$. t: $\sigma$ unknown / small $n$.
  • Two-tailed critical z: 1.96 ($\alpha=.05$), 2.58 ($\alpha=.01$).
  • p-value $=P(\text{data}\mid H_0)$, not $P(H_0)$.
