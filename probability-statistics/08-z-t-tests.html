Topic 8 · GATE weight: Very High Core

Hypothesis Testing:
z-test & t-test

A courtroom for data: assume "nothing's going on", then check whether the evidence is surprising enough to reject that assumption.

2 Why this topic exists

Is a new drug actually better, or did we just get lucky? Hypothesis testing gives a disciplined yes/no: assume no effect (the null), measure how extreme the data is under that assumption, and reject only if it's too unlikely to be chance.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

It's a trial. The null hypothesis $H_0$ is "innocent" (no effect). The test statistic measures how far your data is from $H_0$ in standard-error units. The p-value is "how often pure chance would give evidence this extreme". Small p ⇒ the evidence is surprising ⇒ reject $H_0$.

4 Basic definitions
  • Null $H_0$: the "no effect / no difference" claim. Alternative $H_1$: what you suspect instead.
  • Test statistic: a number ($z$ or $t$) measuring how far the data sit from $H_0$.
  • Significance level $\alpha$: the false-alarm risk you accept (often 0.05).
  • p-value: chance of data this extreme if $H_0$ were true; reject $H_0$ when p $\lt\alpha$.
  • Critical value: the cutoff the statistic must beat to reject $H_0$.
  • One- vs two-tailed: testing a specific direction vs just "different".
  • Type I / Type II error: false alarm (reject a true $H_0$) vs a miss (keep a false $H_0$). Power $=1-P(\text{Type II})$.
  • z-test: $\sigma$ known (or large $n$). t-test: $\sigma$ unknown, estimated by $s$ (small samples), using $n-1$ df.
📖 Study in Depth — the t-distribution plus z / t / paired tests from zero
1. Why the t-distribution exists

If the population SD σ is known, standardize with z. But usually σ is unknown, so we estimate it with the sample SD s — and that estimate adds uncertainty.

Student's t-distribution absorbs that extra uncertainty. Use it when σ is unknown, especially for small samples.

2. The t-statistic vs the z-statistic 
z = (x̄ − μ) / (σ/√n)      σ known
t = (x̄ − μ) / (s/√n)      σ unknown (estimated by s)

Same shape of formula — the only change is σ → s, which is exactly why we switch from z to t.

3. Degrees of freedom = n − 1 
one-sample t:  df = n − 1
n = 10 → df = 9

We "spend" one degree of freedom estimating the mean from the data before measuring spread.

4. t vs normal — heavier tails

The t-curve is bell-shaped like the normal but with heavier tails — more probability in the extremes, reflecting small-sample uncertainty.

small df → fatter tails (more spread)
df → ∞     → t approaches the standard normal

6. Confidence interval when σ is unknown

The mean CI with σ unknown swaps z for a t critical value and σ for s:

x̄ ± t · s/√n            t from the t-table at df = n − 1

(Compare the σ-known interval x̄ ± z·σ/√n from the CLT topic — same skeleton, wider because t > z.)

7. Where the t-distribution is used
  • one-sample and two-sample t-tests
  • confidence intervals for a mean when σ is unknown
  • small-sample inference
  • testing regression coefficients
8. One- vs two-tailed tests & critical values

The alternative hypothesis decides which tail(s) you check (α = 0.05):

two-tailed  H₁: μ ≠ μ₀  → reject if Z < −1.96 or Z > 1.96
right-tailed H₁: μ > μ₀ → reject if Z > 1.645
left-tailed  H₁: μ < μ₀ → reject if Z < −1.645

"Different" → two tails; "greater/less" → one tail. A one-tailed test puts all of α in a single tail, so its cutoff is closer to 0.

10. One-sample z-test — worked 
claim μ₀ = 100, σ = 15 (known), n = 25, x̄ = 106, α = 0.05 two-tailed
SE = σ/√n = 15/5 = 3
Z = (106 − 100)/3 = 2
2 > 1.96 → reject H₀

Conclusion: significant evidence the mean differs from 100. (Z = how many standard errors x̄ sits from μ₀.)

11. Two-sample z-test (independent means)

Compare two independent means when both σ are known / samples large:

Z = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / √(σ₁²/n₁ + σ₂²/n₂)

Testing a claimed gap Δ₀: Z = [(x̄₁ − x̄₂) − Δ₀] / √(σ₁²/n₁ + σ₂²/n₂); usually Δ₀ = 0 (H₀: μ₁ = μ₂).

12. One-sample t-test — worked 
μ₀ = 50, n = 16, x̄ = 52, s = 8 (σ unknown), α = 0.05 two-tailed
t = (52 − 50)/(8/√16) = 2/2 = 1
df = n − 1 = 15 → critical t ≈ ±2.131
|1| < 2.131 → fail to reject H₀

Not enough evidence the mean differs from 50.

13. Independent samples t-test (pooled variance)

Two independent groups, σ unknown, assumed equal variances:

pooled  sp² = [(n₁−1)s₁² + (n₂−1)s₂²] / (n₁ + n₂ − 2)
t = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / [ sp · √(1/n₁ + 1/n₂) ]
df = n₁ + n₂ − 2

The pooled variance blends both samples' spreads into one estimate.

14. Paired samples t-test — why & how

When the two samples are linked — same subject measured twice (before/after), or matched pairs — they are not independent. Work with the per-pair differences:

d = after − before
H₀: μ_d = 0     t = d̄ / (s_d/√n)     df = n − 1

One mean difference replaces two separate means — that's the whole trick.

15. Paired t-test — worked 
5 students, d = after − before = 5, 5, 3, 4, 5
d̄ = 22/5 = 4.4
Σ(d − d̄)² = 0.36+0.36+1.96+0.16+0.36 = 3.2
s_d² = 3.2/(5−1) = 0.8 → s_d ≈ 0.894
t = 4.4 / (0.894/√5) = 4.4/0.4 = 11 ,  df = 4

Huge t ⇒ reject H₀: the training significantly improved performance.

16. Paired vs independent — pick correctly
SituationTest
same subject before/afterpaired t-test

two unrelated groupsindependent t-test
two separate classesindependent t-test

Trap: "before-after" is paired, never independent.

17. Common GATE traps
  • Use z when σ is unknown and n small? No — use t.
  • t has thinner tails than normal? No — heavier (fatter) tails.
  • df = n? No — one-sample t uses df = n − 1.
  • t and z always differ a lot? No — they converge as n grows.

19. Memory box & mental model
  • σ known → z; σ unknown → t (df = n − 1).
  • t = (x̄ − μ)/(s/√n); heavier tails than z.
  • t → normal as df grows.
  • CI (σ unknown): x̄ ± t·s/√n.
t = "z for when you had to guess σ from the data" — extra caution = fatter tails.

6 Main formulas
$$ z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\quad(\sigma\text{ known}) \qquad t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}\quad(\sigma\text{ unknown}) $$

The $t$ statistic uses $n-1$ degrees of freedom. Reject $H_0$ if $|z|$ (or $|t|$) exceeds the critical value, or equivalently if p $\lt\alpha$.

Two-sample & paired versions:

$$ z=\frac{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{\sigma_1^2/n_1+\sigma_2^2/n_2}} \qquad t=\frac{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2}{s_p\sqrt{1/n_1+1/n_2}},\ \ s_p^2=\frac{(n_1-1)s_1^2+(n_2-1)s_2^2}{n_1+n_2-2} $$
$$ \text{paired: } t=\frac{\bar{d}}{s_d/\sqrt{n}},\quad d=\text{after}-\text{before},\ \ df=n-1 $$
7 Step-by-step method
  1. State $H_0$ and $H_1$ (one- or two-tailed) and pick $\alpha$.
  2. Choose the test: $\sigma$ known / large $n$ → z; $\sigma$ unknown / small $n$ → t.
  3. Compute the test statistic from $\bar{x},\mu_0,$ and the standard error.
  4. Find the p-value (or compare to the critical value).
  5. If p $\lt\alpha$, reject $H_0$; otherwise fail to reject.
8 Worked example
✦ z-test

Claim $\mu_0=100$. Sample: $\bar{x}=104$, $\sigma=15$, $n=36$. SE $=15/6=2.5$.

$z=(104-100)/2.5=1.6$. At $\alpha=0.05$ two-tailed the critical $z$ is $1.96$. Since $1.6\lt1.96$, fail to reject $H_0$.

✦ One-sample z-test

Claim $\mu=100$. Sample: $n=36$, $\bar{x}=104$, known $\sigma=12$.

$z=\dfrac{104-100}{12/\sqrt{36}}=\dfrac{4}{2}=2.0$.

Two-tailed at $\alpha=0.05$, critical value $1.96$. Since $|z|=2.0$ exceeds $1.96$, reject $H_0$.

9 GATE cheat sheet
⚡ High-value facts
  • $\sigma$ known / large $n$ → z; $\sigma$ unknown / small $n$ → t (with $n-1$ df).
  • Two-tailed critical $z$: $1.96$ at $\alpha=0.05$, $2.58$ at $\alpha=0.01$; one-tailed $1.645$ at $\alpha=0.05$.
  • Paired t-test = run a one-sample t-test on the differences $d=\text{after}-\text{before}$ ($H_0:\mu_d=0$).
  • Independent two-group t uses pooled variance with $df=n_1+n_2-2$.
  • Reject when p $\lt\alpha$ (equivalently $|z|\gt z_{\alpha/2}$).
  • The t-distribution is fatter-tailed than z and approaches z as $n$ grows.
  • Type I error = reject a true $H_0$ (rate $\alpha$); Type II = fail to reject a false $H_0$ (rate $\beta$).
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "p-value = probability $H_0$ is true." Reality: it's $P(\text{data}\mid H_0)$, not $P(H_0\mid\text{data})$.

Trap: "Fail to reject = $H_0$ is proven true." Reality: just not enough evidence against it.

Trap: "Use z when $\sigma$ is unknown." Reality: use t (estimate with $s$).

Trap: "Before-after data uses an independent two-sample test." Reality: it's paired — test the per-pair differences.

12 Practice problems

Q1. $\bar{x}=52,\ \mu_0=50,\ \sigma=8,\ n=64$. Find $z$.

SE $=8/8=1$; $z=(52-50)/1=2$.

Q2. At $\alpha=0.05$ two-tailed, is $z=2$ significant?

Yes — $|2|\gt1.96$, so reject $H_0$.

Q3. Population $\sigma$ unknown and $n=12$. z or t?

t-test, with $n-1=11$ degrees of freedom.

Q4. Differences (after−before): 5,5,3,4,5. Compute the paired-t numerator $\bar d$ and state the df.

$\bar d=22/5=4.4$; $df=n-1=4$. (Then $t=\bar d/(s_d/\sqrt n)$ with $s_d\approx0.894$ gives $t=11$.)

Q5. Comparing the same patients' blood pressure before and after a drug — which test?

Paired t-test (the two readings are linked per patient).

Quick MCQ

You use a t-test (instead of z) when…
Large samples lean toward z (t approaches z anyway).
Known $\sigma$ → z-test.
✅ Correct — unknown $\sigma$ (estimated by $s$), especially small $n$ → t-test.
Categorical data uses chi-square, not t.
13 Final memory box
🧠 Remember
  • $H_0$ = no effect; reject when p $\lt\alpha$.
  • $z=(\bar x-\mu_0)/(\sigma/\sqrt n)$; $t$ same with $s$, $n-1$ df.
  • z: $\sigma$ known / large $n$. t: $\sigma$ unknown / small $n$.
  • Two-tailed critical z: 1.96 ($\alpha=.05$), 2.58 ($\alpha=.01$); one-tailed 1.645.
  • Two-sample: $z=(\bar x_1-\bar x_2)/\sqrt{\sigma_1^2/n_1+\sigma_2^2/n_2}$; pooled-t df $=n_1+n_2-2$.
  • Paired (before-after): test $\bar d$ via $t=\bar d/(s_d/\sqrt n)$, df $=n-1$.
  • p-value $=P(\text{data}\mid H_0)$, not $P(H_0)$.
