Joint & Covariance

8 questions. Tap any option — you'll see why it's right or wrong, and the correct one lights up.

Q1. $E[XY]=10,\ E[X]=2,\ E[Y]=4$. $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=?$
Subtract $E[X]E[Y]$.
✅ Correct — $10-2\cdot4=2$.
That's a sum, not the covariance.
That's $E[X]E[Y]$ alone.
Q2. $X,Y$ independent, $\operatorname{Var}X=3,\ \operatorname{Var}Y=5$. $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=?$
That's a product; variances add.
That's a difference.
✅ Correct — independent ⇒ $3+5+0=8$.
There's no $+2\operatorname{Cov}$ here (Cov is 0).
Q3. Correlation $\rho$ always lies in…
It can be negative too.
It can't exceed 1.
✅ Correct — $-1\le\rho\le1$.
It's bounded to $[-1,1]$.
Q4. Independent $X,Y$ ⇒ $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=?$
Independence forces 0, not 1.
✅ Correct — $E[XY]=E[X]E[Y]$ ⇒ Cov $=0$.
That's $\operatorname{Cov}(X,X)$.
It's defined and equals 0.
Q5. Does $\rho=0$ guarantee independence?
No — zero correlation can hide a nonlinear relationship.
✅ Correct — independence ⇒ $\rho=0$, but not the reverse in general.
For jointly Normal it does, but the general answer is "no".
Not always.
Q6. $\operatorname{Cov}(X,X)=?$
Only if $X$ is constant.
Not in general.
✅ Correct — covariance of a variable with itself is its variance.
That's the mean, not the spread.
Q7. For dependent $X,Y$, $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=?$
That drops the covariance term.
✅ Correct — add $2\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$ when dependent.
Variances don't multiply.
They add (plus covariance), not subtract.
Q8. $\rho=1$ means…
$\rho=0$ means no linear relationship.
Independence gives $\rho=0$.
✅ Correct — $\rho=1$ is a perfect positive line.
That's $\rho=-1$.
