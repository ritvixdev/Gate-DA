2 Probability language & set theory

Sample space $S$ = the collection of every possible outcome. An event is any subset of $S$. For one fair die:

$$S=\{1,2,3,4,5,6\},\qquad A=\{\text{even result}\}=\{2,4,6\},\qquad P(A)=\tfrac{3}{6}=\tfrac12$$
⚠️ Trap

The "favourable outcomes ÷ total outcomes" formula is valid only when the elementary outcomes are equally likely.

Set operations — $A\cup B$ means A or B (including both); $A\cap B$ means both; $A^c$ means A does not happen, with the complement rule:

$$P(A^c)=1-P(A)$$

Q · Subset logic. Three events $A,B,C$ satisfy $A\cap B\cap C=A$. Which conclusion follows necessarily, with no extra assumptions?
A. $A\subset B\subset C$    B. $A\subseteq B\subseteq C$    C. $A\subset B$ and $A\subset C$    D. $A\subseteq B$ and $A\subseteq C$

  1. $A\cap B\cap C$ keeps only the elements simultaneously present in all three sets.
  2. For this intersection to equal the whole of $A$, every element of $A$ must also be inside $B$ and inside $C$: so $A\subseteq B$ and $A\subseteq C$.
  3. We cannot use the strict subset symbol $\subset$, because $A$ might be exactly equal to $B$ or $C$.

Answer: D.

🧠 Memory rule
  • $A\cap B=A \iff A\subseteq B$ — "intersecting with something bigger changes nothing".
  • Strict subset $\subset$ needs proof that the sets are not equal; the safe symbol is $\subseteq$.
3 Addition rule & inclusion–exclusion

For two events, the intersection is subtracted because it was counted once inside $P(A)$ and again inside $P(B)$:

$$P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)$$

For three events:

$$P(A\cup B\cup C)=P(A)+P(B)+P(C)-P(A\cap B)-P(A\cap C)-P(B\cap C)+P(A\cap B\cap C)$$
🧠 Memory pattern
  • Add singles, subtract pairs, add the triple overlap. Signs alternate: $+,-,+$.

Q · Three-event union. Events $A,B,C$ satisfy $C\subset B$, with $P(A)=0.6$, $P(B)=0.3$, $P(C)=0.05$, $P(A\cap B)=0.2$, $P(A\cap C)=0$, $P(B\cap C)=0.05$. Find $P(A\cup B\cup C)$.

  1. Since $A\cap C=\varnothing$, the triple intersection $A\cap B\cap C\subseteq A\cap C$ must also be empty: $P(A\cap B\cap C)=0$.
  2. Inclusion–exclusion: $0.6+0.3+0.05-0.2-0-0.05+0 = 0.95-0.25=0.70$.

Answer: 0.70. The key spot: an empty pairwise intersection forces the triple intersection empty too.

4 Mutually exclusive vs independent

These are completely different ideas and GATE loves testing the confusion.

Mutually exclusiveIndependent
MeaningCannot happen togetherOne gives no information about the other
Formula$P(A\cap B)=0$$P(A\cap B)=P(A)\,P(B)$, i.e. $P(A\mid B)=P(A)$
PictureNon-overlapping regionsOverlap exactly proportional to sizes
⚠️ The classic trap

If two mutually exclusive events both have positive probability, they can never be independent: exclusivity forces $P(A\cap B)=0$, but independence would need $P(A)P(B)\gt 0$. A positive number cannot equal zero.

Q · Exclusive and independent? Events $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive with $P(A)\gt 0$, $P(B)\gt 0$. Which is correct?
A. It is possible, in some cases, that $A$ and $B$ are also independent.    B. It is never possible for $A$ and $B$ to be independent.

Exclusivity: $P(A\cap B)=0$. Independence would require $P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)\gt 0$ — impossible. Answer: B.

Q · Two-or-more heads and tails. Three fair coins are tossed independently. Let $T=\{\text{two or more heads}\}$ and $S=\{\text{two or more tails}\}$. What is $P(T\cap S)$?
A. $0$    B. $0.5$    C. $0.25$    D. $1$

  1. Both events together would need at least $2+2=4$ coin outcomes — but only three coins were tossed.
  2. So $T\cap S=\varnothing$ and $P(T\cap S)=0$.

Answer: A (0). No computation needed — a pure counting-capacity argument.

5 Complement rule & "at least one"

"At least one" means one or more. Listing all those cases is slow; the complement ("none") is a single case:

$$P(\text{at least one})=1-P(\text{none})$$

Q · At least one die shows 1. 100 fair dice are thrown. Let $A$ = at least one die shows 1. Then $P(A)$ is:
A. $0$    B. $1$    C. $1-\left(\tfrac{5}{6}\right)^{100}$    D. $\left(\tfrac{5}{6}\right)^{100}$

  1. One die: $P(\text{not }1)=\tfrac56$.
  2. Independence across 100 dice: $P(\text{no die shows }1)=\left(\tfrac56\right)^{100}$.
  3. Complement: $P(A)=1-\left(\tfrac56\right)^{100}$.

Answer: C. Trap: option D is the probability of the complement — read which event is being asked.

6 Permutations, combinations & counting

The first question to ask: does order matter?

$$\text{Permutation (order matters): } {}^nP_r=\frac{n!}{(n-r)!}\qquad \text{Combination (order doesn't): } \binom{n}{r}=\frac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!}$$
🧠 Memory rule
  • Permutation = Position.   Combination = Choose.

Q · Equal heads and tails. A fair coin is tossed $2n$ times independently. The probability of an equal number of heads and tails is:
A. $\dfrac{\binom{2n}{n}}{2^{2n}}$    B. $\dfrac{1}{(2n)!}$    C. $\dfrac{1}{\binom{2n}{n}}$    D. $\dfrac{\binom{2n}{n}}{2^{n}}$

  1. Equal counts means exactly $n$ heads and $n$ tails.
  2. Choose which $n$ of the $2n$ positions are heads: $\binom{2n}{n}$ ways.
  3. Every particular sequence has probability $\left(\tfrac12\right)^{2n}$.
  4. So $P=\binom{2n}{n}\left(\tfrac12\right)^{2n}=\dfrac{\binom{2n}{n}}{2^{2n}}$.

Answer: A. Pattern: count the favourable sequences, multiply by the probability of one sequence.

Q · Prime sum of two dice. Two dice are rolled simultaneously. What is the probability that their sum is a prime number?
A. $\tfrac{17}{36}$    B. $\tfrac{1}{24}$    C. $\tfrac{5}{12}$    D. $\tfrac{1}{6}$

There are $6\times6=36$ equally likely ordered outcomes. Prime sums: $2,3,5,7,11$ with ordered-pair counts $1,2,4,6,2$.

Total favourable $=1+2+4+6+2=15$, so $P=\tfrac{15}{36}=\tfrac{5}{12}$. Answer: C.

Trap: always count ordered pairs (36 total), not the 21 unordered sums.

7 Subset counting

A set of $n$ elements has $2^n$ subsets — every element independently chooses "in" or "out". Non-empty subsets: $2^n-1$.

Q · Even product of a subset (GATE DA 2026). Let $M$ be a randomly selected non-empty subset of $S=\{1,2,\dots,2026\}$. What is the probability that the product of all elements of $M$ is even?
A. $\dfrac{2^{1013}(2^{1013}-1)}{2^{2026}}$    B. $\dfrac{2^{1013}}{2^{2026}}$    C. $\dfrac{2^{1013}(2^{1013}-1)}{2^{2026}-1}$    D. $\dfrac{1}{2^{2026}-1}$

  1. $S$ has 1013 odd and 1013 even numbers. A product is odd only if every chosen element is odd.
  2. Total non-empty subsets: $2^{2026}-1$. Non-empty all-odd subsets: $2^{1013}-1$.
  3. Even-product subsets: $(2^{2026}-1)-(2^{1013}-1)=2^{2026}-2^{1013}=2^{1013}(2^{1013}-1)$.
  4. $P=\dfrac{2^{1013}(2^{1013}-1)}{2^{2026}-1}$.

Answer: C. Trap: the denominator is $2^{2026}-1$ (non-empty only), not $2^{2026}$.

🧠 Memory rule
  • "Product is odd" ⇒ all factors odd. "Product is even" ⇒ at least one even factor ⇒ complement trick.
8 Stars and bars

Use stars and bars for counting integer solutions of $x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_k=n$.

$$\text{Non-negative }(x_i\ge 0):\ \binom{n+k-1}{k-1}\qquad \text{Positive }(x_i\ge 1):\ \binom{n-1}{k-1}$$

For positive solutions, give each variable 1 first ($m_i=x_i-1$), reducing to a non-negative problem with total $n-k$.

Q · Positive integer solutions (GATE DA 2026). A program selects uniformly at random a non-negative integer solution of $n_1+n_2+n_3+n_4=20$. What is the probability that all four variables are positive?
A. $\dfrac{\binom{19}{3}}{\binom{23}{3}}$    B. $\dfrac{\binom{20}{4}}{\binom{24}{4}}$    C. $\dfrac{\binom{20}{3}}{\binom{23}{3}}$    D. $\dfrac{\binom{19}{4}}{\binom{24}{4}}$

  1. Total non-negative solutions: $\binom{20+4-1}{4-1}=\binom{23}{3}$.
  2. Positive: set $m_i=n_i-1$, so $m_1+m_2+m_3+m_4=16$ with $m_i\ge 0$: $\binom{16+4-1}{3}=\binom{19}{3}$.
  3. $P=\binom{19}{3}\big/\binom{23}{3}$.

Answer: A.

🧠 Memory rule
  • Non-negative: top $=n+k-1$, bottom $=k-1$ ("add the bars to the stars").
  • Positive: first pay ₹1 to each variable, then use the non-negative formula.
9 Deterministic vs stochastic phenomena
  • Deterministic: same inputs and initial conditions ⇒ same output, every time. Example: a fixed arithmetic algorithm.
  • Stochastic: has random variation; described using probabilities. Example: a coin toss.
  • Mixed: regression $Y=f(X)+\epsilon$ — a deterministic pattern plus random error.

Q · True or false. I. A deterministic phenomenon is one whose outcome can be predicted with a high degree of confidence. II. A stochastic phenomenon is one whose outcome can be predicted with limited confidence.
A. I true, II false    B. Both true    C. I false, II true    D. Both false

Under the intended exam wording, both statements are accepted as true. Answer: B.

Technical refinement: a deterministic outcome is not merely "highly probable" — it is fixed once the inputs and starting conditions are fixed.

10 Conditional probability

Conditional probability = probability after receiving additional information. "Given $B$" means $B$ has become the new, reduced sample space.

$$P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}$$
⚠️ Trap

$P(A\mid B)$ is generally not equal to $P(B\mid A)$. Reversing the conditioning is exactly what Bayes' theorem is for.

Q · Exhaustive urn choices. Two urns $U_1,U_2$ contain red and blue balls. One urn is selected, then one ball is drawn. Which statements are always true?
A. $P(U_1\mid R)+P(U_2\mid R)=1$    B. $P(R\mid U_1)+P(R\mid U_2)=1$
C. $P(U_1\mid B)+P(U_2\mid B)=1$    D. $P(B\mid U_1)+P(B\mid U_2)=1$

The selected urn must be $U_1$ or $U_2$ — so given any observed colour, the urn posteriors sum to 1 (A and C true). But $P(R\mid U_1)$ and $P(R\mid U_2)$ condition on different events; nothing forces them to add to 1 (B, D false).

Answer: A and C.

🧠 Rule: probabilities sum to 1 only when the left side varies over a partition and the conditioning event stays fixed.

11 Law of total probability

If $B_1,\dots,B_k$ are non-overlapping groups that together cover everything, then add all the routes through which $A$ can occur:

$$P(A)=\sum_{i=1}^{k}P(A\mid B_i)\,P(B_i)$$

Q · Fair and double-headed coins. A bowl has three coins: two fair and one double-headed. A coin is selected uniformly at random and tossed. What is $P(\text{heads})$?
A. $\tfrac12$    B. $1$    C. $\tfrac34$    D. $\tfrac23$

  1. Three routes: $P(H)=\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac13+\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac13+1\cdot\tfrac13$.
  2. $=\tfrac16+\tfrac16+\tfrac13=\tfrac23$.

Answer: D.

12 Bayes' theorem

Bayes reverses conditioning. Use it when the question gives "probability of result given cause" but asks "probability of cause given result".

$$P(B\mid A)=\frac{P(A\mid B)\,P(B)}{P(A)} \qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad \text{Posterior}=\frac{\text{Likelihood}\times\text{Prior}}{\text{Evidence}}$$

If $P(A)$ is not given directly, build it with total probability first.

Q · Smoker is male. 40% of a population is male, 60% female. Half of the males smoke, and 40% of the entire population smokes. Given a randomly selected person is a smoker, what is the probability the person is male?

Given $P(M)=0.4$, $P(S\mid M)=0.5$, $P(S)=0.4$:

$$P(M\mid S)=\frac{P(S\mid M)P(M)}{P(S)}=\frac{0.5\times0.4}{0.4}=0.5$$

Answer: 0.50.

Q · Infection given positive result. A test is positive with probability 50% when a person is infected. Infection probability is 50%, and the overall probability of a positive test is 80%. Find $P(\text{infected}\mid +)$.
A. 100%    B. ≈50%    C. ≈80%    D. ≈31%

$$P(I\mid +)=\frac{P(+\mid I)P(I)}{P(+)}=\frac{0.5\times0.5}{0.8}=\frac{0.25}{0.8}=0.3125$$

Answer: D (31.25%).

Q · Disease-test posterior (GATE DA 2026, NAT). Disease $D$ occurs in 30% of a population. A test is positive 80% of the time when the person has $D$, and 10% of the time when they don't. Given a positive test, find $P(D\mid +)$ to two decimal places.

  1. Total probability: $P(+)=0.8(0.3)+0.1(0.7)=0.24+0.07=0.31$.
  2. Bayes: $P(D\mid +)=\dfrac{0.8\times0.3}{0.31}=\dfrac{0.24}{0.31}\approx0.7742$.

Answer: 0.77.

Q · Selecting a box. Box 1: 2 black, 1 white. Box 2: 1 black, 2 white. Box 3: 3 black, 3 white. A box is selected with $P(B_1)=\tfrac12$, $P(B_2)=\tfrac16$, $P(B_3)=\tfrac13$; a ball is drawn and found white. Find $P(B_2\mid W)$.

  1. $P(W\cap B_1)=\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac13=\tfrac16$;   $P(W\cap B_2)=\tfrac16\cdot\tfrac23=\tfrac19$;   $P(W\cap B_3)=\tfrac13\cdot\tfrac12=\tfrac16$.
  2. $P(W)=\tfrac16+\tfrac19+\tfrac16=\tfrac49$.
  3. $P(B_2\mid W)=\dfrac{1/9}{4/9}=\tfrac14$.

Answer: 0.25.

Q · Conditional events. Given $P(T^c)=0.6$, $P(S\mid T)=0.3$, $P(S\mid T^c)=0.6$. Find $P(T\mid S)$.

  1. $P(T)=1-0.6=0.4$.
  2. $P(S)=0.3(0.4)+0.6(0.6)=0.12+0.36=0.48$.
  3. $P(T\mid S)=\dfrac{0.3\times0.4}{0.48}=0.25$.

Answer: 0.25.

🧠 Bayes workflow (same 3 steps every time)
  • Step 1 — write the prior(s) of each cause.
  • Step 2 — total probability for the evidence: $P(\text{evidence})=\sum(\text{likelihood}\times\text{prior})$.
  • Step 3 — posterior $=$ (your cause's likelihood $\times$ prior) $\div$ evidence.
13 Bayesian networks

A Bayesian network encodes a joint distribution on a directed graph. The single rule:

$$P(X_1,\dots,X_n)=\prod_i P\big(X_i\mid \text{Parents}(X_i)\big)$$

A root node has no parents, so use its marginal probability.

Q · Joint probability in a network (image-based). Four Bernoulli variables with graph $U\to V$, $U\to W$, $V\to Z$, $W\to Z$. $P(U{=}1)=0.5$;   $P(V{=}1\mid U{=}1)=0.5$;   $P(W{=}1\mid U{=}1)=1$;   $P(Z{=}1\mid V{=}1,W{=}1)=0.5$. Find $P(U{=}1,V{=}1,W{=}1,Z{=}1)$.

  1. Factorize along the graph: $P(U,V,W,Z)=P(U)\,P(V\mid U)\,P(W\mid U)\,P(Z\mid V,W)$.
  2. Pick the required table values: $0.5,\ 0.5,\ 1,\ 0.5$.
  3. Multiply: $0.5\times0.5\times1\times0.5=0.125$.

Answer: 0.125.

🧠 Rule: start at every node, condition it only on its parents, and multiply.

14 Conditional independence & colliders

$A$ and $B$ are conditionally independent given $X$ when $P(A,B\mid X)=P(A\mid X)P(B\mid X)$. The tricky structure is the collider $V\to X\leftarrow W$: before conditioning on $X$, $V$ and $W$ can be independent; after conditioning on their common effect $X$, they may become dependent.

Q · Identify the false statement. Five variables have joint distribution $P(V,W,X,Y,Z)=P(V)\,P(W)\,P(X\mid V,W)\,P(Y\mid X)\,P(Z\mid X)$. Which is false?
A. $Y\perp V$ given $X$    B. $Z\perp W$ given $X$    C. $V\perp W$ given $X$    D. $Y\perp Z$ given $X$

The graph is $V\to X\leftarrow W$ with $X\to Y$ and $X\to Z$. Given $X$: $Y$ is cut off from $V$ (A true), $Z$ from $W$ (B true), and $Y,Z$ only share $X$ (D true). But $X$ is a collider for $V,W$ — conditioning on it can create dependence between them.

Answer: C is false.

🧠 Rule: conditioning on a common effect connects its causes ("explaining away").

15 Random variables, expectation & variance

A random variable assigns a number to each random outcome (discrete = countable values; continuous = any value in an interval).

$$E[X]=\sum_x x\,P(X{=}x)\ \ \text{(discrete)}\qquad E[X]=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}x f(x)\,dx\ \ \text{(continuous)}$$

Linearity (works even without independence!):

$$E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b\qquad E[X+Y]=E[X]+E[Y]$$

Variance measures spread around the mean; the shortcut form is what you'll use in exams:

$$\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2\qquad \operatorname{SD}(X)=\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(X)}\qquad \operatorname{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\operatorname{Var}(X)$$
⚠️ Trap

The constant $b$ shifts location but never changes spread — it vanishes from the variance. And $a$ comes out squared.

Q · Tower property. $E\big[E[X\mid Y]\big]$ equals:
A. $E[X\mid Y]$    B. $E[X]E[Y]$    C. $E[X]$    D. $E[Y]$

Average $X$ separately inside each $Y$-group, then average those group averages — you get the overall average of $X$.

Answer: C. $E[E[X\mid Y]]=E[X]$ (the tower property).

Q · Expected number of black balls. A bag has 5 white and 10 black balls. 100 balls are drawn one at a time with replacement. Let $S_{100}$ be the number of black balls drawn. Find $E[S_{100}]$ (1 dp).

$p=\tfrac{10}{15}=\tfrac23$, so $S_{100}\sim\text{Binomial}(100,\tfrac23)$ and $E=np=100\cdot\tfrac23=66.67$.

Answer: 66.7.

Q · Transformed Bernoulli and normal (GATE DA 2026). $X\sim\text{Bernoulli}(0.3)$ and $Y\sim N(0,100)$, independent. Variance of $(2X-1)Y$?
A. 100    B. 90    C. 49    D. 21

  1. $2X-1\in\{-1,+1\}$, so $(2X-1)^2=1$ always.
  2. $E[Y]=0$ gives $E[Z]=0$ for $Z=(2X-1)Y$, so $\operatorname{Var}(Z)=E[Z^2]$.
  3. $E[Z^2]=E[(2X-1)^2Y^2]=E[Y^2]=\operatorname{Var}(Y)=100$.

Answer: A (100). Insight: a random $\pm1$ sign never changes the spread of a zero-mean variable.

Q · Pairwise variance identity. For $\{x_1,\dots,x_{100}\}$ it is given that $\frac{1}{2000}\sum_i\sum_j(x_i-x_j)^2=99$. Find $\frac{1}{99}\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2$.

Identity: $\displaystyle\sum_i\sum_j (x_i-x_j)^2 = 2n\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2$.

  1. $\sum\sum (x_i-x_j)^2 = 99\times2000=198000$.
  2. $198000 = 2(100)\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2 \Rightarrow \sum(x_i-\bar x)^2=990$.
  3. $\dfrac{990}{99}=10$.

Answer: 10. 🧠 Remember: pairwise squared differences $=2n\times$ sum of squared deviations.

16 Expected waiting time via states

When a question asks "expected number of trials until a pattern occurs", define states by progress toward the pattern and write one equation per state.

Q · Two consecutive even numbers. A fair die is thrown repeatedly. Expected number of throws until two consecutive even numbers appear?
A. 2    B. 4    C. 6    D. 8

Let $a$ = expected remaining throws when the previous throw was not even, $b$ = when it was even. $P(\text{even})=\tfrac12$.

  1. From $a$: one throw used; half the time move to $b$, half stay at $a$:   $a=1+\tfrac12 b+\tfrac12 a$.
  2. From $b$: one throw used; half the time finish, half fall back to $a$:   $b=1+\tfrac12 a$.
  3. Substitute: $a=1+\tfrac12(1+\tfrac12 a)+\tfrac12 a=1.5+\tfrac34 a \Rightarrow \tfrac14 a=1.5 \Rightarrow a=6$.

Answer: C (6).

17 PMF, PDF & CDF
  • PMF (discrete): $p(x)=P(X{=}x)$; valid if $p(x)\ge0$ and $\sum_x p(x)=1$.
  • PDF (continuous): probability is area: $P(a\le X\le b)=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$; valid if $f(x)\ge0$ and total area $=1$. For continuous $X$: $P(X{=}a)=0$.
  • CDF: $F(x)=P(X\le x)$ — non-decreasing, right-continuous, $0$ on the far left, $1$ on the far right. Where differentiable, $f(x)=F'(x)$.
$$P(a\le X\le b)=F(b)-F(a)\qquad \text{Median }m:\ F(m)=0.5$$

Q · Uniform CDF. $F(x)=0$ for $x\lt 0$; $F(x)=x$ for $0\le x\le1$; $F(x)=1$ for $x\gt 1$. Select the correct option.
A. This is the CDF of a uniform random variable.   B. Not a valid CDF.   C. More information required.   D. Valid, but not uniform.

For $X\sim U(0,1)$, $F(x)=x$ on $[0,1]$ exactly. The function is non-decreasing, right-continuous, runs from 0 to 1. Answer: A.

Q · Find the PDF support. $f(x)=\tfrac{x}{2}$. Which is a valid support?
A. $0\le x\le3$    B. $0\le x\le1$    C. $0\le x\le2$    D. $0\le x\le4$

Total area must equal 1: $\int_0^a \tfrac{x}{2}dx=\tfrac{a^2}{4}=1\Rightarrow a=2$. Answer: C.

🧠 Rule: unknown constant or endpoint in a PDF ⇒ set the total integral to 1.

Q · Properties of a discrete CDF (GATE DA 2026, MSQ). Which are correct for a discrete random variable's CDF $F(x)$?
A. Always positive    B. Non-decreasing    C. Has jump discontinuities    D. Left-continuous

Below the smallest possible value $F(x)=0$, so not always positive (A false). Cumulative probability can't decrease (B true). Discrete masses produce jumps (C true). Every CDF is right-continuous (D false).

Answer: B and C.

Q · Median from a CDF. $F_X(x)=\dfrac{x-t}{4-t}$ for $t\le x\le 4$ (0 below, 1 above). If the median is 3, what is $t$?
A. 2    B. 1    C. $-1$    D. 0

Set $F(3)=0.5$: $\dfrac{3-t}{4-t}=\tfrac12 \Rightarrow 2(3-t)=4-t \Rightarrow 6-2t=4-t \Rightarrow t=2$. Answer: A.

Q · CDF of $X^2$. $F_X(x)=\tfrac14(x+1)^2$ for $-1\le x\le1$ (0 below, 1 above). Find $P(X^2\le 0.25)$.
A. 0.625    B. 0.25    C. 0.5    D. 0.5625

  1. Convert the squared inequality: $X^2\le0.25 \iff -0.5\le X\le 0.5$.
  2. $F(0.5)=\tfrac14(1.5)^2=0.5625$;   $F(-0.5)=\tfrac14(0.5)^2=0.0625$.
  3. $P=0.5625-0.0625=0.5$.

Answer: C. Trap: don't forget the negative half of the interval.

18 Bernoulli & Binomial

Bernoulli = one success/failure trial: $P(X{=}1)=p$, $E[X]=p$, $\operatorname{Var}(X)=p(1-p)$.

Binomial — use when there is a fixed number of independent two-outcome trials with the same success probability:

$$P(X{=}k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\qquad E[X]=np\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=np(1-p)$$

Q · Lottery wins and non-wins (MSQ). A person buys one ticket in each of 10 independent lotteries, each with win probability 0.001. $X$ = number of wins, $Y$ = number of non-wins. Which are correct?
A. $Y\sim\text{Bin}(10,0.001)$   B. $Y\sim\text{Bin}(10,0.999)$   C. $X\sim\text{Bin}(10,0.999)$   D. $X\sim\text{Bin}(10,0.001)$

Win probability $0.001$ ⇒ $X\sim\text{Bin}(10,0.001)$; non-win probability $0.999$ ⇒ $Y\sim\text{Bin}(10,0.999)$.

Answer: B and D.

Q · Overlapping row and column sums (NAT). A $5\times5$ matrix has independent Bernoulli(0.5) entries. Find the probability that the sum of the second row and the sum of the third column both equal 3 (2 dp).

Key difficulty: the row and column share the entry $A_{2,3}$ — they are not independent. Split on the shared entry.

  1. Shared entry $=1$ (prob $\tfrac12$): each remaining group of 4 needs exactly two 1s: $\binom42(\tfrac12)^4=\tfrac{6}{16}$ each. Contribution $\tfrac12\left(\tfrac{6}{16}\right)^2=\tfrac{18}{256}$.
  2. Shared entry $=0$ (prob $\tfrac12$): each group needs exactly three 1s: $\binom43(\tfrac12)^4=\tfrac{4}{16}$ each. Contribution $\tfrac12\left(\tfrac{4}{16}\right)^2=\tfrac{8}{256}$.
  3. Total: $\tfrac{18+8}{256}=\tfrac{26}{256}=0.1015625$.

Answer: 0.10. 🧠 Rule: when two counts overlap, condition on the shared cell.

19 Poisson

Counts of events in an interval. The one fact GATE tests again and again:

$$P(X{=}k)=\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}\qquad E[X]=\operatorname{Var}(X)=\lambda$$

Q · Poisson & standard normal facts. I. The mean and variance of a Poisson random variable are equal. II. A standard normal random variable has mean 0 and variance 1.
A. Both true    B. Only I    C. Only II    D. Both false

Both are definitional facts. Answer: A.

20 Uniform
$$X\sim U(a,b):\quad f(x)=\frac{1}{b-a}\qquad E[X]=\frac{a+b}{2}\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=\frac{(b-a)^2}{12}$$

For independent continuous variables, probability often becomes favourable area ÷ total area.

Q · Two independent uniforms (NAT). $X\sim U[1,3]$ and $Y\sim U[2,4]$ independent. Find $P(X\ge Y)$ (3 dp).

  1. Total region: rectangle $1\le x\le3,\ 2\le y\le4$, area $=2\times2=4$.
  2. Favourable ($x\ge y$): only possible for $2\le y\le3$; for fixed $y$, $x$ runs from $y$ to 3. Area $=\int_2^3(3-y)\,dy=\tfrac12$.
  3. $P=\dfrac{0.5}{4}=0.125$.

Answer: 0.125. 🧠 Draw the square first — geometry beats integration for uniforms.

21 Normal distribution & z-score

$X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$: $\mu$ = mean, $\sigma^2$ = variance (watch the notation!), $\sigma$ = standard deviation. Standardize with

$$Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\sim N(0,1)$$

The z-score = how many standard deviations a value lies above/below the mean.

Q · Normal linear transformation. $X=aZ+b$ with $Z\sim N(0,1)$. Given $E[X]=1$, $E[(X-E[X])Z]=-2$, $E[(X-E[X])^2]=4$, find $a,b$.
A. $a=-2,b=1$   B. $a=2,b=-1$   C. $a=-2,b=-1$   D. $a=1,b=1$

  1. $E[Z]=0 \Rightarrow E[X]=b=1$.
  2. $X-E[X]=aZ$, so $E[(X-E[X])Z]=aE[Z^2]=a=-2$.
  3. Check: $\operatorname{Var}(X)=a^2=4$ ✓.

Answer: A.

22 Exponential distribution

Waiting times. For rate $\lambda$:

$$f(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}\quad(x\ge0)\qquad P(X\gt x)=e^{-\lambda x}\qquad E[X]=\frac{1}{\lambda}\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=\frac{1}{\lambda^2}$$

And the star property — memorylessness:

$$P(X\gt s+t\mid X\gt s)=P(X\gt t)$$

Q · Find the rate. $P(X\ge2)=0.25$. What is $\lambda$?
A. $\ln 2$    B. $\ln 4$    C. $\ln 3$    D. $\ln 0.25$

$e^{-2\lambda}=\tfrac14 \Rightarrow -2\lambda=-\ln4 \Rightarrow \lambda=\tfrac{\ln4}{2}=\ln2$. Answer: A.

Q · Mean–variance relation (NAT). $X\sim\text{Exp}(\lambda)$ with $5E[X]=\operatorname{Var}(X)$. Find $\lambda$ (1 dp).

$\dfrac{5}{\lambda}=\dfrac{1}{\lambda^2}\Rightarrow 5\lambda=1\Rightarrow\lambda=0.2$. Answer: 0.2.

Q · Memorylessness (GATE DA 2026, NAT). $P(X\gt5)=0.35$. Find $P(X\gt10\mid X\gt5)$ (2 dp).

Memoryless with $s=t=5$: $P(X\gt10\mid X\gt5)=P(X\gt5)=0.35$. Answer: 0.35. No computation — recognize the property.

Q · Floor of an exponential. $Y=\lfloor X\rfloor$ where $X$ is exponential with mean $\tfrac{1}{\ln 10}$. For positive integer $\ell$, $P(Y{=}\ell)=q^{\ell}(1-q)$. Find $q$.
A. 0.1    B. 0.01    C. 0.5    D. 0.434

  1. Rate $\lambda=\ln10$. Event $Y=\ell$ means $\ell\le X\lt \ell+1$.
  2. $P(Y{=}\ell)=e^{-\lambda\ell}-e^{-\lambda(\ell+1)}=e^{-\lambda\ell}(1-e^{-\lambda})$.
  3. Compare with $q^{\ell}(1-q)$: $q=e^{-\lambda}=e^{-\ln10}=\tfrac{1}{10}$.

Answer: A (0.1). 🧠 The floor of an exponential is geometric with $q=e^{-\lambda}$.

23 Student's t vs normal
  • $t$ is symmetric around 0 like the normal but has heavier tails.
  • As degrees of freedom grow, $t\to N(0,1)$.
  • $t_1$ is the Cauchy distribution.

Q · Compare normal and $t_1$ (GATE DA 2026, MSQ). $Z\sim N(0,1)$ with pdf $g$, CDF $G$; $Y\sim t_1$ with pdf $h$, CDF $H$. Let $c\gt0$ satisfy $g(c)=h(c)$. Which are correct?
A. $G(0)=H(0)$   B. $G(c)\lt H(c)$   C. $G(-c)\lt H(-c)$   D. $g(0)=h(0)$

  1. Both symmetric about 0: $G(0)=H(0)=0.5$ — A true.
  2. Between 0 and $c$ the normal density is taller, so it accumulates more: $G(c)\gt H(c)$ — B false.
  3. Symmetry: $G(-c)=1-G(c)$, $H(-c)=1-H(c)$; since $G(c)\gt H(c)$, we get $G(-c)\lt H(-c)$ — C true.
  4. Peaks: $g(0)=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\ne\tfrac{1}{\pi}=h(0)$ — D false.

Answer: A and C.

24 Chi-square distribution

Sum of squared independent standard normals:

$$Z_1^2+\cdots+Z_k^2\sim\chi^2_k\qquad E[\chi^2_k]=k\qquad \operatorname{Var}(\chi^2_k)=2k$$

Bonus fact: $\chi^2_2$ is exponential with mean 2.

Q · Square of a standard normal. $Y=Z^2$ where $Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}$, $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$. Variance of $Y$?
A. 1    B. 2    C. 3    D. 4

$Z\sim N(0,1)$, so $Y\sim\chi^2_1$ and $\operatorname{Var}(Y)=2k=2$. Answer: B.

Q · Sums of squared normals (GATE DA 2026, MSQ). $X_1,\dots,X_n$ iid $N(0,1)$, $\bar X=\tfrac1n\sum X_i$. Which are correct?
A. $\sum X_i^2\sim\chi^2_n$   B. $\sum(X_i-\bar X)^2\sim\chi^2_{n-1}$   C. $X_1^2+X_n^2$ is exponential with mean 2   D. $(\sqrt n\,\bar X)^2\sim\chi^2_2$

A: standard result — true. B: estimating $\bar X$ consumes one degree of freedom — true. C: $X_1^2+X_n^2\sim\chi^2_2$ = exponential with mean 2 — true. D: $\sqrt n\,\bar X\sim N(0,1)$, so its square is $\chi^2_{1}$, not $\chi^2_2$ — false.

Answer: A, B and C.

25 Joint distributions, marginals & covariance

A joint distribution describes two variables together. To get one variable alone, sum (or integrate) out the other — the result is the marginal:

$$P(X{=}x)=\sum_y P(X{=}x,Y{=}y)\qquad f_X(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dy$$

Covariance measures linear co-movement:

$$\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]$$
  • Positive → move together; negative → move oppositely; zero → no linear co-movement.
  • Independence ⇒ zero covariance. Zero covariance does NOT imply independence.

Q · Covariance of coin indicators (NAT). Two fair coins tossed independently. $X=1$ if both are heads, else 0. $Y=1$ if at least one head, else 0. Find $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$ (3 dp).

  1. $E[X]=P(HH)=\tfrac14$;   $E[Y]=1-P(TT)=\tfrac34$.
  2. Whenever $X=1$, also $Y=1$, so $XY=X$ and $E[XY]=\tfrac14$.
  3. $\operatorname{Cov}=\tfrac14-\tfrac14\cdot\tfrac34=\tfrac{1}{16}=0.0625$.

Answer: 0.063. 🧠 For 0/1 indicators, $E[XY]=P(\text{both events happen})$.

26 Pearson & Spearman correlation

Pearson standardizes covariance to $[-1,1]$ and measures linear association; Spearman applies Pearson to ranks and measures monotonic association (works for ordinal data).

$$\rho_{XY}=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y}\qquad r=\frac{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sqrt{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2}\sqrt{\sum_i(y_i-\bar y)^2}}$$

Q · Pearson and Spearman (MSQ). Which are correct?
A. If $|r_{xy}|$ is close to 1, then $x,y$ have a linear relationship.
B. If $x,y$ have a linear relationship, then $|r_{xy}|$ is close to 1.
C. If $|r_S|$ is close to 1, then $x,y$ have a monotonic relationship.
D. If $|r_S|$ is close to 0, there is no monotonic association between $x,y$.

Intended answer: A and C.

Wording nuance: exact points on a non-horizontal line give $|r|=1$; but a noisy linear trend need not have $r$ near 1 (so B fails under the noisy reading). And $|r_S|\approx0$ means no strong monotonic rank association — weak/noisy patterns may still exist (D too absolute).

Q · Spearman and concordant pairs. Which statements are not true?
I. Spearman rank correlation can be used for ordinal variables.
II. Pearson correlation takes a value between 0 and +1.
III. Two observations are concordant when one variable rises while the other falls.
A. II and III    B. I and III    C. I and II    D. III

I is true (ranks handle ordinal data). II is false — Pearson's range is $[-1,1]$. III is false — opposite-direction pairs are discordant; concordant pairs move the same direction.

Answer: A.

Q · Calculate Pearson correlation. $X=\{5.1,4.9,4.7,4.6,5.0\}$, $Y=\{3.5,3.0,3.2,3.1,3.6\}$.
A. 1.00    B. 0.00    C. $-0.68$    D. 0.68

$\bar x=4.86$, $\bar y=3.28$; $\sum(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)=0.146$; $\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2=0.172$; $\sum(y_i-\bar y)^2=0.268$.

$$r=\frac{0.146}{\sqrt{0.172\times0.268}}\approx0.68$$

Answer: D.

Q · Non-linear dependence with zero correlation. $X\sim U(-1,1)$ and $Y\mid X{=}x\sim U(x^2-0.1,\ x^2+0.1)$. Find $\operatorname{Corr}(X,Y)$.

  1. Conditional mean = interval midpoint: $E[Y\mid X{=}x]=x^2$.
  2. $E[XY]=E[X\,E(Y\mid X)]=E[X^3]=0$ by symmetry of $U(-1,1)$; also $E[X]=0$.
  3. $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=0 \Rightarrow \operatorname{Corr}(X,Y)=0$.

Answer: 0.

⚠️ Crucial lesson

$Y$ clearly depends on $X$ (through $X^2$), yet Pearson correlation is zero — the relationship is symmetric and non-linear. Zero correlation ≠ independence.

27 Covariance matrix

With $n$ numerical features, $\Sigma\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$: diagonal entries are variances $\Sigma_{ii}=\operatorname{Var}(X_i)$; off-diagonals are covariances $\Sigma_{ij}=\operatorname{Cov}(X_i,X_j)$. It is symmetric.

Q. If a dataset contains $n$ features, is the variance-covariance matrix $n\times n$?   A. False   B. True

Answer: B. One row and one column per feature.

28 Descriptive statistics
  • Mean: $\bar x=\tfrac1n\sum x_i$   Median: middle value after sorting   Mode: most frequent value.
  • Standard deviation: square root of variance.

Q · Updated mean. The average of 50 data points is 40. What is the average after adding a new observation of value 142?

Old total $=50\times40=2000$; new total $=2142$; new count $=51$; new mean $=2142/51=42$. Answer: 42.

🧠 Rule: update means through totals, never by averaging averages.

Q · z-score normalization. Income attribute: min 46000, max 170000, mean 96000, SD 21000. The z-score-normalized value of ₹106000 is closest to:
A. 0.217    B. 0.476    C. 0.623    D. 2.304

$z=\dfrac{x-\mu}{\sigma}=\dfrac{106000-96000}{21000}\approx0.476$. Answer: B. The min and max are pure distractors (they belong to min-max normalization).

29 Skewness

The mean is pulled toward the long tail:

$$\text{Right-skewed: Mean}\gt\text{Median}\gt\text{Mode}\qquad \text{Left-skewed: Mean}\lt\text{Median}\lt\text{Mode}$$
🧠 Memory rule
  • The mean follows the tail. Alphabetical order (Mean, Median, Mode) matches the right-skewed inequality.

Q · Mean, median, mode (MSQ). Which usually hold?
A. Left-skewed: Mean > Median > Mode   B. Right-skewed: Mean < Median < Mode
C. Left-skewed: Mean < Median < Mode   D. Right-skewed: Mean > Median > Mode

Answer: C and D.

30 Q–Q plots

A Q–Q plot compares sample quantiles with theoretical quantiles. Match ⇒ points on a straight reference line. Curvature ⇒ shape mismatch; tail deviations ⇒ different tail behaviour; isolated points ⇒ outliers.

Q · Exponential Q–Q plot (MSQ). An exponential Q–Q plot is made for a sample that follows the exponential distribution. Which are correct?
A. Points lie only in the first and third quadrants.   B. Points fall along the line through the origin with slope 1.
C. Points lie only in the first quadrant.   D. Points fall along any arbitrary straight line through the origin.

Exponential values are non-negative ⇒ both axes non-negative ⇒ first quadrant (C). Comparing against the same fully specified exponential, the reference line is $y=x$, slope 1 (B).

Answer: B and C. Nuance: two exponentials with different scales give a straight line with slope $\ne1$.

31 Central Limit Theorem

For iid observations with mean $\mu$ and finite variance $\sigma^2$, the sample mean becomes approximately normal for large $n$:

$$\bar X\approx N\!\left(\mu,\ \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)\qquad SE(\bar X)=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}$$
⚠️ Trap

The CLT applies to sums and sample means — it does not say the original observations become normal.

Q · Binomial sum via CLT. $Y=\sum_{i=1}^{300}X_i$, $X_i\sim\text{Bernoulli}(0.25)$ iid. Approximate $P(60\le Y\le90)$.
A. $\Phi(2)-\Phi(-2)$   B. $\Phi(1)-\Phi(-1)$   C. $\Phi(3)-\Phi(-3)$   D. $\Phi(90)-\Phi(60)$

  1. $E[Y]=np=75$; $\operatorname{Var}(Y)=np(1-p)=56.25$; $SD=7.5$.
  2. $z_L=\dfrac{60-75}{7.5}=-2$; $z_U=\dfrac{90-75}{7.5}=2$.
  3. $P\approx\Phi(2)-\Phi(-2)$.

Answer: A.

32 Sample proportion
$$\hat p=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i\qquad E[\hat p]=p\qquad \operatorname{Var}(\hat p)=\frac{p(1-p)}{n}$$

More data ⇒ smaller variance ⇒ a more reliable estimate.

Q · Sample proportion (MSQ). Which are correct?
A. $E[\hat p]=p$   B. $E[\hat p]=p/n$   C. As $n$ increases, $\operatorname{Var}(\hat p)$ decreases   D. $\operatorname{Var}(\hat p)$ doesn't depend on $n$

Answer: A and C — directly from the formulas above.

33 Hypothesis testing, p-values & errors

Set up $H_0$ (null) vs $H_1$ (alternative), pick significance level $\alpha$, then:

$$p\text{-value}\lt\alpha\ \Rightarrow\ \text{reject }H_0\qquad p\text{-value}\ge\alpha\ \Rightarrow\ \text{fail to reject }H_0$$
⚠️ Trap

"Fail to reject" is not "accept" or "prove" the null. And you may never switch a two-sided test to one-sided after seeing the result.

ErrorMeaningProbabilityThink
Type IRejecting a true $H_0$$\alpha$False alarm 🚨
Type IIFailing to reject a false $H_0$$\beta$Missed detection 😴
$$\text{Power}=1-\beta$$

Q · Interpret a p-value. Two-sided t-test at $\alpha=0.05$ of $H_0:\mu=50$ vs $H_1:\mu\ne50$; p-value $=0.03$. Which is correct?
A. Convert to a one-sided test.   B. Reject $H_0$ at 5%.   C. Accept $H_0$ because $0.03\lt0.05$.   D. A Type II error has definitely occurred.

$0.03\lt0.05$ ⇒ reject $H_0$. Changing the test direction after the result is invalid; a Type II error concerns not rejecting a false null.

Answer: B.

35 Simple linear regression & OLS

The model, with every symbol decoded:

$$y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1 x_i+\epsilon_i$$
  • $x_i$: input · $y_i$: observed output · $\beta_0$: intercept · $\beta_1$: slope · $\epsilon_i$: error.
  • One input, one output = SISO. Fitted value $\hat y_i=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1 x_i$; residual $e_i=y_i-\hat y_i$.

Classical OLS assumptions: linearity in parameters; errors with zero mean; constant error variance; independent observations/errors; no perfect multicollinearity; predictors measured accurately.

Q · Regression model properties (MSQ). For $y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1x_i+\epsilon_i$, which are true?
A. It is MISO.   B. It is SISO.   C. The independent variable is assumed measured without error.   D. $\partial SSE/\partial\beta_0=0$ gives $\sum\epsilon_i=0$, where $SSE=\sum\epsilon_i^2$.

One predictor, one output ⇒ SISO (B). Classical model treats $x$ as exact (C). Differentiating $SSE=\sum(y_i-\beta_0-\beta_1x_i)^2$ w.r.t. $\beta_0$ gives $-2\sum e_i=0\Rightarrow\sum e_i=0$ (D).

Answer: B, C and D.

Q · OLS assumptions (MSQ). Which are assumptions of ordinary least squares?
A. Independent-variable measurements are accurate.   B. Errors have expected value zero.
C. The relationship is linear.   D. Errors have constant variance.

Answer: A, B, C and D — all four are classical assumptions.

Q · Interpreting unbiasedness. If OLS assumptions hold, $E[\hat\beta_0]=\beta_0$, $E[\hat\beta_1]=\beta_1$. Select the correct option.
A. The true parameter values are usually unknown because the population / data-generating process is unknown.
B. The true parameter values can always be calculated exactly from a finite sample.

Unbiased means centred on the truth over repeated sampling — not that one finite sample reveals the exact truth. Answer: A.

36 Correlation, linearity & $R^2$

If $Y=a+bX$ exactly ($b\ne0$) then $|r|=1$; a noisy line $Y=a+bX+\epsilon$ has smaller $|r|$.

$$R^2=1-\frac{SSE}{SST},\qquad SSE=\sum(y_i-\hat y_i)^2,\quad SST=\sum(y_i-\bar y)^2$$

$R^2$ = proportion of response variation explained by the model. In simple linear regression with intercept, $R^2=r^2$.

Q · Linearity vs correlation (MSQ). Statement A: $X,Y$ have a linear relationship. Statement B: Pearson correlation is close to $\pm1$. Which are correct?
A. B implies A.   B. A implies B.   C. B does not necessarily imply A.   D. A does not necessarily imply B.

Supplied answer: B and C — reading "linear relationship" as an exact deterministic line, A ⇒ B; while $r$ near 1 doesn't prove exact linearity.

Exam caution: check whether "linear relationship" means an exact line or a noisy tendency — the answer can flip with the wording.

Q · Strong $R^2$. Which value indicates a strong linear relationship?   A. 0.90   B. 0.00   C. 0.30   D. $-0.86$

Answer: A — the model explains 90% of response variation. Nuance: in-sample OLS with intercept has $0\le R^2\le1$; negative $R^2$ can appear on test data or intercept-free models.

Q · Observations and $R^2$. The higher the $R^2$, the more closely observations group around:
A. Averages of the predicted variable   B. The intercept   C. The origin   D. The best-fit line

Answer: D.

37 Linear boundaries & feature transformation

A linear/affine decision boundary in 2-D: $w_1x+w_2y+b=0$ — any straight line qualifies. A boundary that is non-linear in the original space may become linear after a feature transformation.

Q · Identify linear boundaries (MSQ). Which are linear decision boundaries in $(x,y)$?
A. $y=a_0+a_1x$   B. $y=a_0$   C. $y=x$   D. $y=a_1x$

Every option is a straight line. Answer: A, B, C and D.

Q · Circular boundary transformation. Class 0: $x^2+y^2\le c^2$; class 1: outside. Define $z=x^2+y^2$. Can the classes be separated by a linear boundary in $z$-space?   A. True   B. False

In $z$-space: class 0 is $z\le c^2$, class 1 is $z\gt c^2$ — the boundary $z=c^2$ is a linear threshold in one dimension. Answer: A.

38 Correlation vs causation & analytics basics

Correlation = varying together. Causation = changing one creates change in the other. A correlation may come from direct causation, reverse causation, a confounder, selection effects, or coincidence — so correlation alone does not prove causation.

Q · Smoking and lung cancer. $A$ = cigarettes/day, $B$ = lung cancer status; a study finds $A,B$ correlated. Select the correct option.
A. A causal relationship exists owing to the harmful effects of cigarettes, not merely because the two are correlated.
B. A causal relationship exists because the variables are correlated.   C. None of the above.   D. No causal relationship exists.

B claims correlation itself establishes causation — wrong. A separates the independent causal evidence from the correlation. Answer: A.

Q · Analytics, classification, imputation (MSQ). Which are true?
A. Data analytics is particularly useful for high-dimensional data that cannot be fully visualized.
B. In supervised classification, data points have class labels.
C. Data imputation can be regarded as function approximation.
D. The same data may be separable by a linear boundary and also by a non-linear one.

All four are standard facts. Answer: A, B, C and D.

39 Entropy & information gain

Entropy measures impurity/uncertainty of class labels ($0$ = pure, higher = more mixed):

$$H(C)=-\sum_{i=1}^{k}p_i\log_2 p_i$$

Information gain = impurity before split − weighted impurity after split:

$$IG(C,A)=H(C)-\sum_v \frac{|C_v|}{|C|}\,H(C_v)$$

Q · Cricket matches and pitch (image-based, NAT). Ten matches between Green and Blue: spin-friendly (S) pitches — matches 1,2,4,7,10 with winners G,B,B,G,G; pace-friendly (F) — matches 3,5,6,8,9 with winners B,G,B,B,B. Overall: Green 4, Blue 6. Find the information gain of splitting Winner by Pitch (2 dp).

  1. Parent entropy: $H(C)=-0.4\log_2 0.4-0.6\log_2 0.6\approx0.97095$.
  2. Spin group (3 G, 2 B): $H(S)=-\tfrac35\log_2\tfrac35-\tfrac25\log_2\tfrac25\approx0.97095$.
  3. Pace group (1 G, 4 B): $H(F)=-\tfrac15\log_2\tfrac15-\tfrac45\log_2\tfrac45\approx0.72193$.
  4. Weighted child entropy: $0.5(0.97095)+0.5(0.72193)\approx0.84644$.
  5. $IG=0.97095-0.84644=0.12451$.

Answer: 0.12. Meaning: knowing the pitch reduces winner-uncertainty by ≈0.12 bits.

🧠 Recognition clue: "decision tree + split by attribute + information gain" ⇒ parent entropy − weighted child entropy. Always.

Distributions

Bernoulli: $E=p$, $\operatorname{Var}=p(1-p)$  ·  Binomial: $P(X{=}k)=\binom nk p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$, $E=np$, $\operatorname{Var}=np(1-p)$  ·  Poisson: $P(X{=}k)=\dfrac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}$, $E=\operatorname{Var}=\lambda$

Uniform: $E=\dfrac{a+b}{2}$, $\operatorname{Var}=\dfrac{(b-a)^2}{12}$  ·  Exponential: $P(X\gt x)=e^{-\lambda x}$, $E=\dfrac1\lambda$, $\operatorname{Var}=\dfrac{1}{\lambda^2}$, memoryless  ·  Normal: $Z=\dfrac{X-\mu}{\sigma}$  ·  Chi-square: $\sum Z_i^2\sim\chi^2_k$, $E=k$, $\operatorname{Var}=2k$

CDF, CLT & sample statistics

$F(x)=P(X\le x)$ · $P(a\le X\le b)=F(b)-F(a)$ · $f=F'$ · $E[\bar X]=\mu$, $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X)=\dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}$, $SE=\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}$ · $E[\hat p]=p$, $\operatorname{Var}(\hat p)=\dfrac{p(1-p)}{n}$

Tests, correlation, regression, trees & networks

41 Final twenty memory rules
🧠 The twenty rules — one line each
  • "At least one" → calculate none and subtract from 1.
  • "Given that" → conditional probability.
  • Reverse conditioning → Bayes' theorem.
  • Several sources → total probability before Bayes.
  • Fixed independent success/failure trials → binomial.
  • Waiting time → exponential.
  • Exponential → remember memorylessness.
  • Unknown PDF endpoint → total integral equals 1.
  • CDF interval probability → upper CDF minus lower CDF.
  • Sample mean or large sum → CLT.
  • Pearson measures linear association.
  • Spearman measures monotonic rank association.
  • Zero correlation does not prove independence.
  • The mean follows the long tail in a skewed distribution.
  • Known population σ → z-test.
  • Unknown population σ → t-test.
  • Categorical counts → chi-square.
  • Compare variances → F-test.
  • Decision-tree split → parent entropy minus weighted child entropy.
  • Bayesian-network joint → multiply every node conditioned on its parents.
