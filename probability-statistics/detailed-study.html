2 Probability language & set theory
🌱 The idea in plain words

Probability starts with one honest admission: we don't know what will happen. So the first thing we do is write down everything that could happen. That complete list is called the sample space, written $S$. For one die, the list is short and friendly: $S=\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$.

An event is just a part of that list you care about. "I want an even number" is the event $A=\{2,4,6\}$. Nothing scary — an event is a wishlist cut out of the full list.

🤔 Why does the counting formula work?

If all six faces are equally likely, then each face carries an equal share of the total probability, $\tfrac16$ each. Your wishlist $\{2,4,6\}$ holds three of those shares, so $P(A)=\tfrac36=\tfrac12$. That's the whole logic behind "favourable ÷ total" — it's just counting equal shares.

⚠️ The hidden condition students miss

"Favourable ÷ total" works only when the outcomes are equally likely. A biased coin, a weighted die, a loaded deck — for these you cannot just count. GATE loves hiding this assumption; always ask yourself "are all outcomes really equal here?" before counting.

Three small symbols describe how events combine — and they map perfectly to everyday English:

  • $A\cup B$ — "$A$ or $B$" (or both). Think of the ∪ as a cup collecting everything from both events.
  • $A\cap B$ — "$A$ and $B$", both happen together. The ∩ is a cap squeezing down to only the common part.
  • $A^c$ — "$A$ does not happen". The little $c$ means complement: everything left over.

Since $A$ either happens or doesn't — there is no third option — their probabilities must add to 1. That single sentence is the complement rule:

$$P(A^c)=1-P(A)$$
🔍 Spot it in the exam

Any question about sets containing sets ($A\subseteq B$), or "which relation must hold" — slow down and think about elements, one at a time. Set questions are logic questions in costume.

Q · Subset logic. Three events $A,B,C$ satisfy $A\cap B\cap C=A$. Which conclusion follows necessarily, with no extra assumptions?

A. $A\subset B\subset C$    B. $A\subseteq B\subseteq C$    C. $A\subset B$ and $A\subset C$    D. $A\subseteq B$ and $A\subseteq C$

Solution — step by step

Let's reason it out slowly, element by element:

  1. $A\cap B\cap C$ keeps only the elements that live in all three sets at once.
  2. The question says this filtered set equals the whole of $A$. That can only happen if no element of $A$ got filtered out — meaning every element of $A$ was already inside $B$ and inside $C$. So $A\subseteq B$ and $A\subseteq C$.
  3. Why not the strict symbol $\subset$? Because nothing stops $A$ from being exactly equal to $B$ or $C$. Strict subset claims they're different — we can't promise that.

Answer: D.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • $A\cap B=A \iff A\subseteq B$ — "intersecting with something bigger changes nothing". Like filtering a playlist by a genre it's already entirely made of.
  • ∪ looks like a cup (collects everything) · ∩ looks like a cap (squeezes to the overlap).
  • When unsure between $\subset$ and $\subseteq$, choose $\subseteq$ — the safe symbol that allows equality.
🔗 Connect the dots

Everything in probability is built on these sets: the addition rule (next section) is about ∪, conditional probability is about ∩, and the "at least one" trick is the complement rule wearing a disguise.

3 Addition rule & inclusion–exclusion
🌱 The idea in plain words

Imagine two clubs at college: the music club and the dance club. If you count music members, then count dance members, and simply add — the students who joined both clubs got counted twice. To get the true headcount you must subtract them once. That's the entire addition rule. It isn't a formula to memorize — it's fixing a double-count.

$$P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)$$
  • $P(A\cup B)$ — chance that $A$ or $B$ (or both) happens: the "true headcount".
  • $P(A)+P(B)$ — the naive sum, which counts the overlap twice.
  • $-P(A\cap B)$ — the correction: remove the double-counted overlap once.
🤔 Why does the three-event version alternate signs?

With three clubs, subtracting all three pairwise overlaps over-corrects: a student in all three clubs was added 3 times and subtracted 3 times — now counted zero times! So we add the triple overlap back once. The pattern add singles − subtract pairs + add triple is just repeatedly fixing the count until it's honest.

$$\begin{aligned}P(A\cup B\cup C)={}&P(A)+P(B)+P(C)\\[2px]&-P(A\cap B)-P(A\cap C)-P(B\cap C)\\[2px]&+P(A\cap B\cap C)\end{aligned}$$
🔍 Spot it in the exam

The word "or", or the symbol ∪, or a question giving you several $P(\cdot)$ values and pairwise intersections and asking for the union. If a pairwise intersection is zero, immediately note what that forces (see the question below).

Q · Three-event union. Events $A,B,C$ satisfy $C\subset B$, with $P(A)=0.6$, $P(B)=0.3$, $P(C)=0.05$, $P(A\cap B)=0.2$, $P(A\cap C)=0$, $P(B\cap C)=0.05$. Find $P(A\cup B\cup C)$.

Solution — step by step
  1. First, the clever observation: the triple overlap $A\cap B\cap C$ sits inside $A\cap C$. We're told $P(A\cap C)=0$ — so the triple overlap has nowhere to live: $P(A\cap B\cap C)=0$.
  2. Now apply the rule calmly, term by term: $P = 0.6+0.3+0.05 \;-\; 0.2-0-0.05 \;+\; 0$.
  3. $=0.95-0.25=0.70$.

Answer: 0.70.

💬 The real lesson: the exam gave six numbers but hid the seventh (the triple overlap). Finding "hidden zero" values from the given structure is the actual skill being tested.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • "Add singles, subtract pairs, add the triple." Say it like a rhythm — the signs alternate $+,-,+$.
  • Picture the Venn diagram of two overlapping circles: adding both circles paints the middle twice, so peel one layer off.
  • Sanity check: the union can never exceed 1, and never be smaller than the biggest single event.
🔗 Connect the dots

This rule is your bridge to "at least one" questions (Section 5): "at least one of $A,B,C$" IS the union. When the union has many pieces, the complement trick is usually faster.

4 Mutually exclusive vs independent — they are NOT twins
🌱 The idea in plain words

These two ideas sound similar, and that's exactly why exams test them. Here's the honest difference:

  • Mutually exclusive means the two events are enemies — if one happens, the other simply cannot. A single die roll cannot be both a 3 and a 5.
  • Independent means the two events are strangers — one happening tells you absolutely nothing about the other. Rain in Mumbai and a coin flip in Delhi.
  • Enemies and strangers are very different relationships! Enemies react to each other (strongly — by forbidding each other). Strangers don't react at all.
Mutually exclusive (enemies)Independent (strangers)
MeaningCannot happen togetherOne gives no information about the other
Formula$P(A\cap B)=0$$P(A\cap B)=P(A)\,P(B)$
Knowing $B$ happened……kills $A$ completely…changes nothing about $A$
Venn pictureSeparate, non-touching circlesOverlap exactly proportional to sizes
🤔 Why can't they be both at once?

Suppose $A$ and $B$ both have positive probability. If they were mutually exclusive, then $P(A\cap B)=0$. If they were also independent, we'd need $P(A\cap B)=P(A)\,P(B)$, which is a product of two positive numbers — so it's positive. A positive number can never equal zero. Contradiction, case closed.

Intuition version: if $A$ and $B$ are enemies, then learning "$B$ happened" is huge news about $A$ (it kills it!). Huge news is the opposite of independence.

Q · Exclusive and independent? Events $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive with $P(A)\gt 0$, $P(B)\gt 0$. Which is correct?

A. It is possible, in some cases, that $A$ and $B$ are also independent.
B. It is never possible for $A$ and $B$ to be independent.

Solution — step by step

This is exactly the argument above: exclusivity forces $P(A\cap B)=0$, but independence would need $P(A)P(B)\gt 0$. Zero can't equal a positive number.

Answer: B.

Q · Two-or-more heads and tails. Three fair coins are tossed independently. Let $T=\{\text{two or more heads}\}$ and $S=\{\text{two or more tails}\}$. What is $P(T\cap S)$?

A. $0$    B. $0.5$    C. $0.25$    D. $1$

Solution — step by step

Pause before computing anything — is this even possible?

  1. "Two or more heads" needs at least 2 of the 3 coins to be heads.
  2. "Two or more tails" needs at least 2 of the 3 coins to be tails.
  3. Together that demands at least $2+2=4$ coins. We only have 3. The two events physically cannot coexist — they are mutually exclusive.

Answer: A (0).

💬 The real lesson: sometimes the fastest solution is realizing the event is impossible. Always spend five seconds asking "can this even happen?" before calculating.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Exclusive = enemies, Independent = strangers. Enemies can't share a room; strangers don't affect each other.
  • Exclusive lives in the world of sets (no overlap). Independent lives in the world of information (no news).
  • If both events have positive probability: exclusive ⇒ definitely NOT independent.
🔗 Connect the dots

Independence is the quiet hero of later topics: the binomial distribution (Section 18) assumes independent trials, and "zero covariance vs independence" (Section 26) is this same confusion returning in a statistics costume.

5 The complement trick — "at least one"
🌱 The idea in plain words

"At least one" sounds innocent, but it hides an avalanche: at least one means one, or two, or three, or… — dozens of cases to add up. Here's the escape route: the opposite of "at least one" is a single, clean case — "none". So instead of climbing the mountain, walk around it:

$$P(\text{at least one})=1-P(\text{none})$$
🤔 Why does this work?

"At least one" and "none" split the world perfectly between them — exactly one of the two must happen, and they can't both happen. Whenever two events split the world like that, their probabilities sum to 1. This is just the complement rule from Section 2, pointed at a useful target.

🔍 Spot it in the exam

The literal words "at least one". Also watch for their cousins: "at least once", "some die shows…", "the product is even" (= at least one even factor!). The moment you see them, your pen should already be writing $1-P(\text{none})$.

Q · At least one die shows 1. 100 fair dice are thrown. Let $A$ = at least one die shows 1. Then $P(A)$ is:

A. $0$    B. $1$    C. $1-\left(\tfrac{5}{6}\right)^{100}$    D. $\left(\tfrac{5}{6}\right)^{100}$

Solution — step by step
  1. Flip to the opposite: "none of the 100 dice shows a 1".
  2. For one die, $P(\text{not }1)=\tfrac56$.
  3. The dice don't influence each other (independent), so multiply the same chance 100 times: $P(\text{none})=\left(\tfrac56\right)^{100}$.
  4. Come back through the complement: $P(A)=1-\left(\tfrac56\right)^{100}$.

Answer: C.

⚠️ Watch out: option D is sitting there hoping you forget the final "1 −" step. Exams love offering you your own half-finished work as a wrong option.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • "At least one? Think none!" — six words that solve a whole family of GATE questions.
  • The pattern $1-(\text{something})^n$ in the options is a fingerprint of this trick — recognize it on sight.
  • Why multiplication works for "none": independent events multiply (Section 4 — strangers don't interfere).
6 Counting — permutations & combinations
🌱 The idea in plain words

Probability often reduces to counting, and all of counting hangs on one question: does the order matter?

  • Choosing a captain and a vice-captain from your friends? Order matters — (Asha, Ravi) is different from (Ravi, Asha). That's a permutation.
  • Choosing two friends for a movie? Order doesn't matter — going with Asha and Ravi is the same plan either way. That's a combination.
$$\text{Permutation (order matters): } {}^nP_r=\frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$$ $$\text{Combination (order doesn't): } \binom{n}{r}=\frac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!}$$
🤔 Why does the combination formula divide by $r!$?

Start with the permutation count — all ordered ways. But if order doesn't matter, every group of $r$ people got counted once for each way of arranging them, which is $r!$ times. So divide by $r!$ to undo the over-counting. Combinations are just permutations with the "order noise" divided out. (Notice the pattern? Same spirit as inclusion–exclusion: count naively, then fix the over-count.)

Q · Equal heads and tails. A fair coin is tossed $2n$ times independently. The probability of an equal number of heads and tails is:

A. $\dfrac{\binom{2n}{n}}{2^{2n}}$    B. $\dfrac{1}{(2n)!}$    C. $\dfrac{1}{\binom{2n}{n}}$    D. $\dfrac{\binom{2n}{n}}{2^{n}}$

Solution — step by step

The universal two-step recipe for coin-sequence questions:

  1. Count the favourable sequences. Equal counts means exactly $n$ heads among $2n$ tosses. Choosing which positions hold the heads is a combination (order inside the chosen group doesn't matter): $\binom{2n}{n}$ ways.
  2. Multiply by the probability of one sequence. Each specific sequence of $2n$ fair tosses has probability $\left(\tfrac12\right)^{2n}$.
  3. So $P=\binom{2n}{n}\cdot\dfrac{1}{2^{2n}}$.

Answer: A.

Q · Prime sum of two dice. Two dice are rolled simultaneously. What is the probability that their sum is a prime number?

A. $\tfrac{17}{36}$    B. $\tfrac{1}{24}$    C. $\tfrac{5}{12}$    D. $\tfrac{1}{6}$

Solution — step by step
  1. Two dice give $6\times6=36$ ordered, equally likely outcomes. (Ordered is important — see the note below.)
  2. Possible prime sums: $2,3,5,7,11$.
  3. Count the ordered pairs for each: sum 2 → 1 way; sum 3 → 2 ways; sum 5 → 4 ways; sum 7 → 6 ways; sum 11 → 2 ways.
  4. Total favourable $=1+2+4+6+2=15$, so $P=\tfrac{15}{36}=\tfrac{5}{12}$.

Answer: C.

⚠️ Why ordered? The outcome (1,2) and (2,1) are genuinely different physical results, each with probability $\tfrac1{36}$. If you count unordered sums, the outcomes stop being equally likely — and the counting formula silently breaks (remember the hidden condition from Section 2!).

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Permutation = Position (order matters). Combination = Choose (order doesn't).
  • Combination formula = permutation formula ÷ $r!$ — "divide out the ordering noise".
  • Dice questions: always work with $36$ ordered outcomes. Sum 7 is the most common sum (6 ways) — a handy anchor to check your table.
7 Subset counting — the power of $2^n$
🌱 The idea in plain words

How many subsets does a set of $n$ elements have? Walk up to each element and ask a yes/no question: "are you in the subset?" Each of the $n$ elements answers independently — 2 choices each — giving $2\times2\times\cdots\times2=2^n$ possible subsets. That includes the empty set (everyone said no), so the number of non-empty subsets is $2^n-1$.

🔍 Spot it in the exam

"A randomly chosen subset…" — and a parity condition like "the product is even" or "the sum is odd". Products and parity have a beautiful rule: a product is odd only when every single factor is odd. One even number anywhere makes the whole product even. So "product even" is secretly an "at least one" question (Section 5)!

Q · Even product of a subset (GATE DA 2026). Let $M$ be a randomly selected non-empty subset of $S=\{1,2,\dots,2026\}$. What is the probability that the product of all elements of $M$ is even?

A. $\dfrac{2^{1013}(2^{1013}-1)}{2^{2026}}$    B. $\dfrac{2^{1013}}{2^{2026}}$    C. $\dfrac{2^{1013}(2^{1013}-1)}{2^{2026}-1}$    D. $\dfrac{1}{2^{2026}-1}$

Solution — step by step
  1. Split $S$: it has 1013 odd numbers and 1013 even numbers.
  2. Flip to the opposite (complement trick!): the product is odd only if the subset contains only odd numbers.
  3. Count the total: non-empty subsets of $S$ = $2^{2026}-1$.
  4. Count the "all-odd" subsets: non-empty subsets of the 1013 odd numbers = $2^{1013}-1$.
  5. Even-product subsets = total − all-odd = $(2^{2026}-1)-(2^{1013}-1)=2^{2026}-2^{1013}=2^{1013}(2^{1013}-1)$.
  6. Divide: $P=\dfrac{2^{1013}(2^{1013}-1)}{2^{2026}-1}$.

Answer: C.

⚠️ Watch out: the denominator must be $2^{2026}-1$, because the question says non-empty. Option A dangles the wrong denominator in front of you.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • $2^n$ subsets — "every element gets a yes/no vote".
  • "Product odd" ⇔ all factors odd. "Product even" ⇔ at least one even ⇔ complement trick.
  • Read the fine print: non-empty changes the denominator by exactly 1 — and GATE will offer both versions as options.
8 Stars and bars — distributing identical things
🌱 The idea in plain words

You have $n$ identical chocolates 🍫 and $k$ children, and you want to count the ways to distribute them. Here's the beautiful trick: lay the $n$ chocolates in a row (the stars ★), and drop $k-1$ bars | between them to slice the row into $k$ portions. For example, with 5 chocolates and 3 children: ★★|★|★★ means the children get 2, 1, 2.

Every distribution = one arrangement of $n$ stars and $k-1$ bars. So counting distributions = choosing where the bars go among $n+k-1$ total positions.

$$\text{Non-negative solutions }(x_i\ge 0):\ \binom{n+k-1}{k-1}$$ $$\text{Positive solutions }(x_i\ge 1):\ \binom{n-1}{k-1}$$
🤔 Why does the "positive" version look different?

"Positive" means every child must get at least one chocolate. Easy fix: hand each child one chocolate first (that uses up $k$ chocolates), and now distribute the remaining $n-k$ freely with the non-negative formula. Substituting $m_i=x_i-1$ does exactly this bookkeeping. No new formula to learn — just a pre-payment step.

🔍 Spot it in the exam

"Number of integer solutions of $x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_k=n$" — the equation itself is the giveaway. Check whether it says $x_i\ge0$ (non-negative) or $x_i\ge1$ / "positive" — that decides which formula.

Q · Positive integer solutions (GATE DA 2026). A program selects uniformly at random a non-negative integer solution of $n_1+n_2+n_3+n_4=20$. What is the probability that all four variables are positive?

A. $\dfrac{\binom{19}{3}}{\binom{23}{3}}$    B. $\dfrac{\binom{20}{4}}{\binom{24}{4}}$    C. $\dfrac{\binom{20}{3}}{\binom{23}{3}}$    D. $\dfrac{\binom{19}{4}}{\binom{24}{4}}$

Solution — step by step

Probability = favourable count ÷ total count, and both counts are stars-and-bars:

  1. Total (non-negative, $n=20$, $k=4$): $\binom{20+4-1}{4-1}=\binom{23}{3}$.
  2. Favourable (all positive): pre-pay 1 to each variable — set $m_i=n_i-1$, leaving $m_1+m_2+m_3+m_4=16$ with $m_i\ge0$: $\binom{16+4-1}{3}=\binom{19}{3}$.
  3. $P=\dfrac{\binom{19}{3}}{\binom{23}{3}}$.

Answer: A.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Non-negative: $\binom{n+k-1}{k-1}$ — "add the bars ($k-1$ of them) to the stars, then choose the bars' seats".
  • Positive: pre-pay ₹1 to every variable, then it becomes the non-negative problem with $n-k$ left.
  • Bottom number is always $k-1$ (bars). Only the top changes between the two cases.
9 Deterministic vs stochastic — two kinds of world
🌱 The idea in plain words

A deterministic process is a vending machine: same coin, same button, same snack — every time. Fix the inputs and the output is fixed, not just likely.

  • A stochastic process has genuine randomness inside: a coin toss, tomorrow's stock price, the number of customers at a shop. We can't predict one outcome, but we can describe the pattern of outcomes using probabilities — and that is the entire mission of this subject.
  • Most of data science lives in between: a regression model $Y=f(X)+\epsilon$ is a deterministic pattern $f(X)$ plus a random wobble $\epsilon$. Splitting the world into "pattern + noise" is the single most important habit in ML.

Q · True or false.
I. A deterministic phenomenon is one whose outcome can be predicted with a high degree of confidence.
II. A stochastic phenomenon is one whose outcome can be predicted with limited confidence.

A. I true, II false    B. Both true    C. I false, II true    D. Both false

Solution — step by step

Under the intended exam wording, both statements are accepted as true. Answer: B.

💬 A finer point worth knowing: strictly speaking, a deterministic outcome isn't just "highly probable" — it is completely fixed once inputs are fixed. Exams sometimes use the softer wording; answer what the paper intends, but keep the sharp definition in your head.

🔗 Connect the dots

This "pattern + noise" split returns in Section 35 (regression): $\beta_0+\beta_1x$ is the pattern, $\epsilon$ is the noise, and OLS is the machine that separates them.

10 Conditional probability — updating what you know
🌱 The idea in plain words

You're guessing whether your friend is at the library. Initially, maybe 30%. Then someone tells you "she has an exam tomorrow" — suddenly your estimate jumps. New information changes probabilities. Conditional probability is simply the mathematics of that update.

The notation $P(A\mid B)$ reads "probability of $A$ given $B$". The bar | is a little door: once you walk through it, you live in a smaller world where $B$ has definitely happened.

$$P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}$$
🤔 Why divide by $P(B)$?

Once you know $B$ happened, outcomes outside $B$ are impossible — the sample space shrinks down to just $B$. Inside this smaller world, the part where $A$ also happens is $A\cap B$. But probabilities inside the new world must still total 1, so we rescale by dividing by the new world's size, $P(B)$. That division isn't a rule to memorize — it's re-zooming the picture so the new world fills the frame.

⚠️ The most tested trap in this whole subject

$P(A\mid B)$ and $P(B\mid A)$ are different things. "Probability of testing positive given you're sick" is not "probability of being sick given you tested positive". Confusing them even has a name in courtrooms — the prosecutor's fallacy. The machine for flipping between them is Bayes' theorem (Section 12).

Q · Exhaustive urn choices. Two urns $U_1,U_2$ contain red and blue balls. One urn is selected, then one ball is drawn. Which statements are always true?

A. $P(U_1\mid R)+P(U_2\mid R)=1$     B. $P(R\mid U_1)+P(R\mid U_2)=1$
C. $P(U_1\mid B)+P(U_2\mid B)=1$     D. $P(B\mid U_1)+P(B\mid U_2)=1$

Solution — step by step

The key question for each option: what varies, and what stays fixed?

  1. In A and C, the conditioning event (the observed colour) is fixed, and the urns vary over all possibilities. The ball came from some urn, so the urn probabilities — given whatever we saw — must total 1. Both true.
  2. In B and D, it's backwards: the condition itself changes between the two terms ($U_1$ in one, $U_2$ in the other). These are answers to two different questions ("how red is urn 1?" and "how red is urn 2?") — there's no reason for them to add to 1. If both urns are 80% red, B sums to 1.6!

Answer: A and C.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • "Given" = a door into a smaller world. Divide by the size of that world.
  • Probabilities sum to 1 only when the left side of the bar runs over all possibilities and the right side stays fixed.
  • Never swap the two sides of the bar casually — that swap is precisely Bayes' theorem's job.
🔗 Connect the dots

Conditional probability is the parent of the next three sections: total probability (build up $P(A)$ from conditional pieces), Bayes (flip the bar), and Bayesian networks (a whole graph of conditionals).

11 Law of total probability — add up the routes
🌱 The idea in plain words

You want the chance of reaching the city centre, but there are three roads that lead there. Naturally, you'd compute: (chance I take road 1 × chance road 1 gets me there) + (same for road 2) + (same for road 3). You just invented the law of total probability. It says: when an event can happen through several non-overlapping routes, add up the contribution of every route.

$$P(A)=\sum_{i=1}^{k}P(A\mid B_i)\,P(B_i)$$
  • $B_1,\dots,B_k$ — the routes. They must not overlap, and together they must cover every possibility (a partition).
  • $P(B_i)$ — how likely you are to be on route $i$.
  • $P(A\mid B_i)$ — how likely $A$ is, once you're on route $i$.
  • Multiply along each route, then add the routes.
🔍 Spot it in the exam

Several sources, boxes, urns, machines, or coins — and a question about the overall probability of something. A probability tree is the same idea drawn as a picture: multiply along branches, add across branches.

Q · Fair and double-headed coins. A bowl has three coins: two fair and one double-headed. A coin is selected uniformly at random and tossed. What is $P(\text{heads})$?

A. $\tfrac12$    B. $1$    C. $\tfrac34$    D. $\tfrac23$

Solution — step by step

Three routes to heads — one per coin, each picked with probability $\tfrac13$:

  1. Fair coin #1: contributes $\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac13=\tfrac16$.
  2. Fair coin #2: contributes $\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac13=\tfrac16$.
  3. Double-headed coin: heads is guaranteed, contributes $1\cdot\tfrac13=\tfrac13$.
  4. Add the routes: $\tfrac16+\tfrac16+\tfrac13=\tfrac23$.

Answer: D.

💬 Feel the answer: more than $\tfrac12$ — makes sense, because one coin in the bowl is cheating in favour of heads. Checking whether an answer "feels right" is a genuine exam skill.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Multiply along a route, add across routes. That one sentence is the whole law.
  • The weights $P(B_i)$ must add to 1 — quick check that your routes truly cover everything.
  • Total probability computes the denominator of Bayes' theorem. Learn them as one combined move.
12 Bayes' theorem — reasoning backwards
🌱 The idea in plain words

Nature works forwards: cause → effect. Disease causes a positive test. Cloud causes rain. But in real life we usually see the effect and must guess the cause: the test is positive — am I sick? The ground is wet — did it rain?

Bayes' theorem is the machine for reasoning backwards. You feed in the forward probabilities (which are usually easy to measure) and it hands back the backward one you actually want.

$$P(B\mid A)=\frac{P(A\mid B)\,P(B)}{P(A)}$$ $$\text{Posterior}=\frac{\text{Likelihood}\times\text{Prior}}{\text{Evidence}}$$
  • Prior $P(B)$ — your belief in the cause before seeing anything.
  • Likelihood $P(A\mid B)$ — how strongly the cause produces the observed effect.
  • Evidence $P(A)$ — the overall chance of the observation, from all causes (this is where total probability comes in).
  • Posterior $P(B\mid A)$ — your updated belief after seeing the evidence. The answer.
🤔 Why does it work?

The intersection $P(A\cap B)$ can be built two ways: $P(A\mid B)P(B)$ or $P(B\mid A)P(A)$. They describe the same overlap, so they're equal — and rearranging that equality is Bayes' theorem. Nothing deeper than "one quantity, two viewpoints".

🔍 Spot it in the exam + the 3-step recipe

Given "result given cause", asked "cause given result" → Bayes. Then always the same three moves:

Step 1 — write the prior of each cause.   Step 2 — evidence via total probability: $P(\text{evidence})=\sum(\text{likelihood}\times\text{prior})$.   Step 3 — posterior $=\dfrac{\text{your cause's likelihood}\times\text{prior}}{\text{evidence}}$.

Q · Smoker is male. 40% of a population is male, 60% female. Half of the males smoke, and 40% of the entire population smokes. Given a randomly selected person is a smoker, what is the probability the person is male?

Solution — step by step
  1. Translate: $P(M)=0.4$ (prior), $P(S\mid M)=0.5$ (likelihood), $P(S)=0.4$ (evidence — given directly this time, no total-probability step needed).
  2. Bayes: $P(M\mid S)=\dfrac{P(S\mid M)\,P(M)}{P(S)}=\dfrac{0.5\times0.4}{0.4}=0.5$.

Answer: 0.50.

Q · Infection given positive result. A test is positive with probability 50% when a person is infected. Infection probability is 50%, and the overall probability of a positive test is 80%. Find $P(\text{infected}\mid +)$.

A. 100%    B. ≈50%    C. ≈80%    D. ≈31%

Solution — step by step
  1. Prior $P(I)=0.5$, likelihood $P(+\mid I)=0.5$, evidence $P(+)=0.8$.
  2. $P(I\mid +)=\dfrac{0.5\times0.5}{0.8}=\dfrac{0.25}{0.8}=0.3125$.

Answer: D (≈31%).

💬 Why so low, when the test catches 50% of infections? Because positives are very common overall (80%) — so a positive result is weak evidence. Bayes automatically balances "how well the cause explains the result" against "how easily the result happens anyway". That balancing is the theorem's whole personality.

Q · Disease-test posterior (GATE DA 2026, NAT). Disease $D$ occurs in 30% of a population. A test is positive 80% of the time when the person has $D$, and 10% of the time when they don't. Given a positive test, find $P(D\mid +)$ to two decimal places.

Solution — step by step

This time the evidence isn't given — build it with total probability (Step 2 of the recipe):

  1. Two routes to a positive test: sick-and-positive, healthy-and-positive. $P(+)=0.8(0.3)+0.1(0.7)=0.24+0.07=0.31$.
  2. Bayes: $P(D\mid +)=\dfrac{0.8\times0.3}{0.31}=\dfrac{0.24}{0.31}\approx0.7742$.

Answer: 0.77.

Q · Selecting a box. Box 1: 2 black, 1 white. Box 2: 1 black, 2 white. Box 3: 3 black, 3 white. A box is selected with $P(B_1)=\tfrac12$, $P(B_2)=\tfrac16$, $P(B_3)=\tfrac13$; a ball is drawn and found white. Find $P(B_2\mid W)$.

Solution — step by step
  1. Weigh each route to a white ball (prior × likelihood): $P(W\cap B_1)=\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac13=\tfrac16$;   $P(W\cap B_2)=\tfrac16\cdot\tfrac23=\tfrac19$;   $P(W\cap B_3)=\tfrac13\cdot\tfrac12=\tfrac16$.
  2. Evidence: $P(W)=\tfrac16+\tfrac19+\tfrac16=\tfrac49$.
  3. Posterior: $P(B_2\mid W)=\dfrac{1/9}{4/9}=\tfrac14$.

Answer: 0.25.

Q · Conditional events. Given $P(T^c)=0.6$, $P(S\mid T)=0.3$, $P(S\mid T^c)=0.6$. Find $P(T\mid S)$.

Solution — step by step
  1. Prior: $P(T)=1-0.6=0.4$ (complement rule from Section 2 — see how topics interlock?).
  2. Evidence: $P(S)=0.3(0.4)+0.6(0.6)=0.12+0.36=0.48$.
  3. Posterior: $P(T\mid S)=\dfrac{0.3\times0.4}{0.48}=0.25$.

Answer: 0.25.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Posterior = (Likelihood × Prior) ÷ Evidence. Chant it. It's the same three steps in every single question.
  • The numerator of Bayes is always one route; the denominator is all routes added up. So the answer is "my route's share of the total" — automatically between 0 and 1.
  • Rare disease + decent test can still mean a low posterior. When intuition screams, trust the arithmetic.
🔗 Connect the dots

Bayes = conditional probability (Section 10) + total probability (Section 11) working together. Scale it up from two events to a whole graph of variables and you get Bayesian networks — the very next section.

13 Bayesian networks — probability with a map
🌱 The idea in plain words

Imagine a family tree of causes: rain → wet ground → slippery road. A Bayesian network draws variables as nodes and "directly influences" as arrows. Its power is a huge simplification: each variable only cares about its direct parents — not grandparents, not siblings.

That single assumption collapses an impossibly large joint probability into small, manageable pieces.

$$P(X_1,\dots,X_n)=\prod_i P\big(X_i\mid \text{Parents}(X_i)\big)$$
  • For every node, write one factor: the node given its parents.
  • A root node has no parents → its factor is just its own marginal probability.
  • Multiply all the factors. Done. The joint of any full assignment is one clean product.
🔍 Spot it in the exam

A picture of nodes and arrows plus probability tables → they want a joint probability. Don't panic at the tables. Just walk the graph: one factor per node, look up each value, multiply.

Q · Joint probability in a network (image-based). Four Bernoulli variables with graph $U\to V$, $U\to W$, $V\to Z$, $W\to Z$. Given: $P(U{=}1)=0.5$;   $P(V{=}1\mid U{=}1)=0.5$;   $P(W{=}1\mid U{=}1)=1$;   $P(Z{=}1\mid V{=}1,W{=}1)=0.5$. Find $P(U{=}1,V{=}1,W{=}1,Z{=}1)$.

Solution — step by step
  1. One factor per node, each conditioned only on its parents: $P(U,V,W,Z)=P(U)\cdot P(V\mid U)\cdot P(W\mid U)\cdot P(Z\mid V,W)$.
  2. Look up each needed value from the tables: $0.5$, $0.5$, $1$, $0.5$.
  3. Multiply: $0.5\times0.5\times1\times0.5=0.125$.

Answer: 0.125.

💬 Notice how gentle this actually was — the intimidating diagram reduced to four look-ups and a multiplication. Most Bayesian-network questions are exactly this.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • "Every node pays respect only to its parents." One factor per node; multiply them all.
  • Count check: number of factors in your product = number of nodes in the graph.
  • Root node → no parents → plain marginal probability.
14 Conditional independence & the collider surprise
🌱 The idea in plain words

Here's a genuinely surprising fact — one of the most counter-intuitive in probability. Suppose your college admits students who are either great at sports or great at academics. In the whole population, sports skill and academic skill are unrelated (independent). But among admitted students, they become negatively related: if an admitted student is bad at sports, they were probably admitted for academics!

Admission is a common effect of two causes — a structure called a collider: $V\to X\leftarrow W$. Conditioning on the collider $X$ (looking only at admitted students) creates a dependence between $V$ and $W$ that wasn't there before. This is called "explaining away".

🤔 Why does "explaining away" happen?

Once you know the effect happened, its causes start competing to explain it. Learning that one cause is weak makes the other cause more likely — so information about one now flows to the other. That flow IS dependence.

Q · Identify the false statement. Five variables have joint distribution $P(V,W,X,Y,Z)=P(V)\,P(W)\,P(X\mid V,W)\,P(Y\mid X)\,P(Z\mid X)$. Which is false?

A. $Y$ is conditionally independent of $V$ given $X$.
B. $Z$ is conditionally independent of $W$ given $X$.
C. $V$ and $W$ are conditionally independent given $X$.
D. $Y$ and $Z$ are conditionally independent given $X$.

Solution — step by step

First draw the graph from the factorization: $V\to X\leftarrow W$, then $X\to Y$ and $X\to Z$.

  1. Given $X$: everything $Y$ knows about the world came through $X$, so $Y$ learns nothing more from $V$ — A is true.
  2. Same story for $Z$ and $W$ — B is true.
  3. $Y$ and $Z$ are two children of $X$; given their shared parent, they have nothing else in common — D is true.
  4. But $X$ is a collider for $V$ and $W$. Conditioning on the common effect can create dependence between the causes (explaining away) — so C is the false one.

Answer: C is false.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Chains and forks: conditioning on the middle blocks information. Colliders: conditioning on the middle opens it. Colliders behave opposite to everything else!
  • Story hook: "sports vs academics after admission" — retell it to yourself and the collider rule comes back.
15 Random variables, expectation & variance
🌱 The idea in plain words

A random variable is just a rule that attaches a number to each random outcome — because numbers can be averaged, compared and plotted, while "heads" and "tails" cannot. Coin: heads → 1, tails → 0. Discrete = countable values (dice, counts); continuous = any value in an interval (heights, waiting times).

  • Expectation $E[X]$ is the long-run average: play the game a million times, average the results. Each value is weighted by how often it shows up — that's why the formula multiplies value × probability.
  • Variance answers a different question: not "where is the centre?" but "how far do results wander from the centre?" Two classes can both average 60 marks — one where everyone scores 58–62, another with scores from 20 to 100. Same mean, wildly different variance.
$$E[X]=\sum_x x\,P(X{=}x)\ \ \text{(discrete)}$$ $$E[X]=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}x f(x)\,dx\ \ \text{(continuous)}$$
$$\operatorname{Var}(X)=E\big[(X-E[X])^2\big]=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2$$ $$\operatorname{SD}(X)=\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(X)}$$
🤔 Why is variance built from squared distances?

If we averaged plain deviations $(x-\bar x)$, the positives and negatives would cancel to exactly zero — useless. Squaring makes every deviation positive and punishes big misses more than small ones. The square root at the end (standard deviation) brings the units back to normal — if $X$ is in metres, variance is in metres², but SD is in metres again.

Two transformation rules you will use constantly — and they make complete sense once you see why:

$$E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b$$ $$\operatorname{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\operatorname{Var}(X)$$
⚠️ Why does $b$ vanish from the variance?

Adding $b$ slides the whole distribution sideways — every value moves together, so the spread between values doesn't change at all. Stretching by $a$ stretches distances by $a$, and squared distances by $a^2$. That's why $b$ disappears and $a$ comes out squared. Also remember the friendly giant: $E[X+Y]=E[X]+E[Y]$ holds even when $X,Y$ are dependent — linearity of expectation asks no permission.

Q · Tower property. $E\big[E[X\mid Y]\big]$ equals:

A. $E[X\mid Y]$    B. $E[X]E[Y]$    C. $E[X]$    D. $E[Y]$

Solution — step by step

Picture a school: compute the average marks inside each class (that's $E[X\mid Y]$ — one number per class), then average those class-averages weighted by class size. You get… the overall school average, $E[X]$. Group-wise averaging then averaging the groups = averaging everyone.

Answer: C. This is called the tower property: $E[E[X\mid Y]]=E[X]$.

Q · Expected number of black balls. A bag has 5 white and 10 black balls. 100 balls are drawn one at a time with replacement. Let $S_{100}$ be the number of black balls drawn. Find $E[S_{100}]$ (1 dp).

Solution — step by step
  1. Each draw is black with probability $p=\tfrac{10}{15}=\tfrac23$ — and "with replacement" means every draw is a fresh, independent trial.
  2. Fixed number of independent yes/no trials, constant $p$ → binomial (Section 18): $S_{100}\sim\text{Bin}(100,\tfrac23)$.
  3. $E=np=100\times\tfrac23=66.67$.

Answer: 66.7.

Q · Transformed Bernoulli and normal (GATE DA 2026). $X\sim\text{Bernoulli}(0.3)$ and $Y\sim N(0,100)$, independent. What is the variance of $(2X-1)Y$?

A. 100    B. 90    C. 49    D. 21

Solution — step by step

Don't reach for heavy machinery — first understand what $(2X-1)$ is:

  1. $X$ is 0 or 1, so $2X-1$ is $-1$ or $+1$: a random sign flip. And $(2X-1)^2=1$, always.
  2. Let $Z=(2X-1)Y$. Since $E[Y]=0$, flipping signs keeps $E[Z]=0$, so $\operatorname{Var}(Z)=E[Z^2]$.
  3. $E[Z^2]=E[(2X-1)^2\,Y^2]=E[Y^2]=\operatorname{Var}(Y)=100$.

Answer: A (100).

💬 The intuition worth keeping: randomly flipping the sign of a zero-centred variable rearranges its values but doesn't change how spread out they are.

Q · Pairwise variance identity. For $\{x_1,\dots,x_{100}\}$ it is given that $\frac{1}{2000}\sum_i\sum_j(x_i-x_j)^2=99$. Find $\frac{1}{99}\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2$.

Solution — step by step

There's a lovely identity connecting "everyone compared with everyone" to "everyone compared with the mean":

$$\sum_i\sum_j (x_i-x_j)^2 = 2n\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2$$
  1. From the given: $\sum\sum(x_i-x_j)^2=99\times2000=198000$.
  2. Apply the identity with $n=100$: $198000=200\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2$, so $\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2=990$.
  3. Requested value: $\dfrac{990}{99}=10$.

Answer: 10.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Expectation = centre of mass. Variance = spread. SD = spread in original units.
  • Variance shortcut chant: "mean of the square minus square of the mean".
  • $b$ slides, $a$ stretches: slides don't change spread, stretches change it by $a^2$.
  • Tower property = "average of group-averages is the overall average".
  • Pairwise identity: all-pairs squared differences $=2n\times$ deviations from the mean.
🔗 Connect the dots

Every distribution you'll meet (Sections 18–24) is summarized by these two numbers — its mean and variance. The CLT (Section 31) is entirely a story about the mean and variance of averages.

16 Expected waiting time — thinking in states
🌱 The idea in plain words

"How many throws until two even numbers in a row?" You can't just list cases — the game could last forever. The professional trick: describe your progress as a small set of states, and write one equation per state. Here there are only two situations you can be in: "my last throw wasn't even" (no progress) and "my last throw was even" (halfway there). The equations then solve themselves.

Q · Two consecutive even numbers. A fair die is thrown repeatedly. What is the expected number of throws until two consecutive even numbers appear?

A. 2    B. 4    C. 6    D. 8

Solution — step by step

Let $a$ = expected throws still needed when the previous throw was not even (or we just started), and $b$ = expected throws still needed when the previous throw was even. Note $P(\text{even})=\tfrac12$.

  1. From state $a$: we spend one throw. Half the time it's even (move to $b$), half the time odd (stay at $a$): $$a=1+\tfrac12 b+\tfrac12 a$$
  2. From state $b$: we spend one throw. Half the time it's even — done! Half the time odd — painful slide back to $a$: $$b=1+\tfrac12 a$$
  3. Substitute the second into the first: $a=1+\tfrac12\left(1+\tfrac12 a\right)+\tfrac12 a=1.5+\tfrac34 a$, so $\tfrac14 a=1.5$, giving $a=6$.

Answer: C (6).

💬 Feel it: a single even takes 2 throws on average; needing two in a row costs more than double because a failure at the second step throws you all the way back.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Waiting for a pattern? Define states by progress: "no progress", "halfway", … one equation each.
  • Every equation starts with "$1+$" — you always pay one trial to move.
  • Quick anchor: waiting for one success with probability $p$ takes $1/p$ trials on average.
17 PMF, PDF & CDF — three photographs of one variable
🌱 The idea in plain words

These three scary-sounding names are just three different photographs of the same random variable:

  • PMF (probability mass function) — for discrete variables. It's a bar chart: "how much probability sits exactly on this value?" $p(x)=P(X{=}x)$.
  • PDF (probability density function) — for continuous variables. Here's the mind-shift: a continuous variable has infinitely many possible values, so any single exact value has probability zero! Probability lives in area under the curve between two points, not at points. Think of density like population density — the density at one exact GPS point means nothing until you cover an area.
  • CDF (cumulative distribution function) — the running total for both kinds: $F(x)=P(X\le x)$, "how much probability has accumulated up to $x$?"
$$P(a\le X\le b)=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b)-F(a)$$ $$\text{Median }m:\ F(m)=0.5$$
🤔 Why must the total be exactly 1, and why is the CDF so well-behaved?

Total = 1: the variable must take some value, so all the probability in the world — the full bar heights or the full area under the curve — adds to exactly 1. This is what lets us find unknown constants in a PDF.

CDF rules: a running total can never decrease (you never "un-accumulate" probability), starts at 0 (nothing accumulated at $-\infty$), ends at 1 (everything accumulated at $+\infty$). For a discrete variable the running total jumps at each possible value — and the jump height is exactly that value's probability. Every CDF is right-continuous: at a jump, the CDF has already swallowed the mass at that point.

🔍 Spot it in the exam

Unknown constant/endpoint in a PDF → set total area $=1$. Probability of an interval from a CDF → $F(b)-F(a)$. "Median" → solve $F(m)=0.5$. Squared variable inside a probability → unroll into an interval first.

Q · Uniform CDF. $F(x)=0$ for $x\lt 0$; $F(x)=x$ for $0\le x\le1$; $F(x)=1$ for $x\gt 1$. Select the correct option.

A. This is the CDF of a uniform random variable.    B. Not a valid CDF.
C. More information required.    D. Valid, but not uniform.

Solution — step by step

Check the CDF checklist: non-decreasing ✓, runs from 0 to 1 ✓, right-continuous ✓ — valid. And $F(x)=x$ on $[0,1]$ means probability accumulates at a perfectly steady rate — the signature of $U(0,1)$ (indeed its density is $F'(x)=1$, flat).

Answer: A.

Q · Find the PDF support. $f(x)=\tfrac{x}{2}$. Which is a valid support?

A. $0\le x\le3$    B. $0\le x\le1$    C. $0\le x\le2$    D. $0\le x\le4$

Solution — step by step

A PDF's whole area must be exactly 1 — that's our equation:

  1. $\displaystyle\int_0^a \frac{x}{2}\,dx=\left[\frac{x^2}{4}\right]_0^a=\frac{a^2}{4}$.
  2. Set it to 1: $\dfrac{a^2}{4}=1 \Rightarrow a=2$.

Answer: C.

Q · Properties of a discrete CDF (GATE DA 2026, MSQ). Which are correct for a discrete random variable's CDF $F(x)$?

A. Always positive    B. Non-decreasing    C. Has jump discontinuities    D. Left-continuous

Solution — step by step

Walk the checklist from the "why" box above:

  1. A — false: below the smallest possible value, nothing has accumulated yet, so $F(x)=0$ there (zero, not positive).
  2. B — true: a running total never decreases.
  3. C — true: discrete masses make the running total jump.
  4. D — false: CDFs are right-continuous. ("Left-continuous" is the classic bait.)

Answer: B and C.

Q · Median from a CDF. $F_X(x)=\dfrac{x-t}{4-t}$ for $t\le x\le 4$ (0 below, 1 above). If the median is 3, what is $t$?

A. 2    B. 1    C. $-1$    D. 0

Solution — step by step

The median is the point where half the probability has accumulated: $F(3)=0.5$.

  1. $\dfrac{3-t}{4-t}=\dfrac12$.
  2. Cross-multiply: $2(3-t)=4-t \Rightarrow 6-2t=4-t$.
  3. $t=2$.

Answer: A.

Q · CDF of $X^2$. $F_X(x)=\tfrac14(x+1)^2$ for $-1\le x\le1$ (0 below, 1 above). Find $P(X^2\le 0.25)$.

A. 0.625    B. 0.25    C. 0.5    D. 0.5625

Solution — step by step
  1. First unroll the square. $X^2\le0.25$ means $X$ is within $0.5$ of zero on both sides: $-0.5\le X\le0.5$.
  2. Now it's a plain interval question: $P=F(0.5)-F(-0.5)$.
  3. $F(0.5)=\tfrac14(1.5)^2=0.5625$ and $F(-0.5)=\tfrac14(0.5)^2=0.0625$.
  4. $P=0.5625-0.0625=0.5$.

Answer: C.

⚠️ The trap: forgetting the negative side of $-0.5\le X\le0.5$. A square hides two directions — always unroll it into a two-sided interval.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • PMF = bar heights. PDF = area, not height. CDF = running total.
  • CDF checklist (say it like a rhyme): starts at 0, ends at 1, never falls, jumps right.
  • Interval probability = big CDF minus small CDF. Median: set CDF $=0.5$.
  • Unknown constant in a PDF? The equation is always "total area $=1$".
🔗 Connect the dots

Every named distribution below is just a particular PMF or PDF with a story attached. Once you're friends with "area = probability", the uniform, exponential and normal all become pictures, not formulas.

18 Bernoulli & Binomial — the yes/no family
🌱 The idea in plain words

A Bernoulli variable is the smallest possible experiment: one trial, two outcomes — success (1) with probability $p$, failure (0) otherwise. One coin flip, one free throw, one email that is spam or not.

  • Repeat that Bernoulli trial $n$ times independently and count the successes — that count is a Binomial variable. That's the entire relationship: binomial = $n$ Bernoullis, added up.
  • Binomial checklist (all four must hold): fixed number of trials · exactly two outcomes each · trials independent · same $p$ throughout.
$$P(X{=}k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$$ $$E[X]=np$$ $$\operatorname{Var}(X)=np(1-p)$$
🤔 Why does the formula look like that?

Read it as a sentence, left to right: choose which $k$ trials succeed — $\binom nk$ ways; those $k$ successes cost $p^k$; the remaining $n-k$ failures cost $(1-p)^{n-k}$. Multiply. It's the "count sequences × probability of one sequence" recipe from Section 6 again. And $E[X]=np$ is just linearity: each trial contributes $p$ on average, and there are $n$ of them.

Q · Lottery wins and non-wins (MSQ). A person buys one ticket in each of 10 independent lotteries, each with win probability 0.001. $X$ = number of wins, $Y$ = number of non-wins. Which are correct?

A. $Y\sim\text{Bin}(10,0.001)$    B. $Y\sim\text{Bin}(10,0.999)$    C. $X\sim\text{Bin}(10,0.999)$    D. $X\sim\text{Bin}(10,0.001)$

Solution — step by step

Both $X$ and $Y$ count something over 10 independent trials — both are binomial. The only question is which probability belongs to which count: wins happen with $0.001$, so $X\sim\text{Bin}(10,0.001)$; non-wins happen with $0.999$, so $Y\sim\text{Bin}(10,0.999)$.

Answer: B and D.

💬 Note the symmetry: $Y=10-X$. Counting failures is just counting successes with $p$ replaced by $1-p$.

Q · Overlapping row and column sums (NAT). A $5\times5$ matrix has independent Bernoulli(0.5) entries. Find the probability that the sum of the second row and the sum of the third column both equal 3 (2 dp).

Solution — step by step

Tempting shortcut: "row sum is binomial, column sum is binomial, multiply them." Stop — the row and column share one cell, $A_{2,3}$, so they are not independent. The fix: condition on the shared cell, then everything else genuinely is independent.

  1. Case 1: shared cell $=1$ (probability $\tfrac12$). The row needs 2 more ones among its other 4 cells, and so does the column: each has probability $\binom42\left(\tfrac12\right)^4=\tfrac{6}{16}$. Contribution: $\tfrac12\left(\tfrac{6}{16}\right)^2=\tfrac{18}{256}$.
  2. Case 2: shared cell $=0$ (probability $\tfrac12$). Now each needs 3 ones among 4 cells: $\binom43\left(\tfrac12\right)^4=\tfrac{4}{16}$ each. Contribution: $\tfrac12\left(\tfrac{4}{16}\right)^2=\tfrac{8}{256}$.
  3. Add the cases: $\tfrac{18+8}{256}=\tfrac{26}{256}\approx0.1016$.

Answer: 0.10.

🧠 Transferable trick: whenever two counts overlap, split on the shared piece — inside each case, independence returns.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Binomial = Bernoulli × $n$, added. Mean $np$, variance $np(1-p)$ — variance is largest at $p=\tfrac12$ (maximum uncertainty!).
  • Formula as a sentence: choose the winners · pay for wins · pay for losses.
  • Four-point checklist before declaring "binomial": fixed $n$ · two outcomes · independent · same $p$.
🔗 Connect the dots

Binomial with large $n$: the CLT (Section 31) turns it approximately normal — that's exactly what the CLT question there does. Bernoulli also powers the sample proportion (Section 32).

19 Poisson — counting arrivals
🌱 The idea in plain words

How many customers walk into a shop in an hour? How many typos on a page? How many calls hit a helpline per minute? These are counts of events over an interval, when events arrive randomly at some average rate $\lambda$. That's the Poisson distribution's home turf.

Its most examinable property is almost poetic: its mean and variance are the same number, $\lambda$. If a shop averages 10 customers/hour, the variance of that count is also 10.

$$P(X{=}k)=\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}$$ $$E[X]=\operatorname{Var}(X)=\lambda$$
🤔 Where does Poisson come from?

Take a binomial with a huge number of trials $n$ and a tiny success chance $p$, keeping the average $np=\lambda$ fixed — like many many seconds in an hour, each with a tiny chance of a customer arriving. In that limit, the binomial formula melts into the Poisson formula. Poisson is "binomial for rare events", which is why it feels natural for arrivals and defects.

Q · Poisson & standard normal facts.
I. The mean and variance of a Poisson random variable are equal.
II. A standard normal random variable has mean 0 and variance 1.

A. Both true    B. Only I    C. Only II    D. Both false

Solution — step by step

Both are definitional: Poisson's mean and variance are both $\lambda$, and "standard" normal literally means mean 0, variance 1. Answer: A.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • "Poisson: one parameter to rule them all" — $\lambda$ is the mean AND the variance.
  • Keywords: arrivals, per hour, per page, rare events, rate → Poisson.
  • Poisson counts how many events in an interval; exponential (Section 22) measures how long until the next one. Same story, two cameras.
20 Uniform — perfect fairness on an interval
🌱 The idea in plain words

$X\sim U(a,b)$ means "any value between $a$ and $b$, all equally likely" — a spinner with no favourites. Its PDF is a flat roof of height $\tfrac{1}{b-a}$ (flat = no favourites; that height makes the rectangle's area 1). The mean sits dead centre at the midpoint, by symmetry.

$$f(x)=\frac{1}{b-a}\ \ (a\le x\le b)$$ $$E[X]=\frac{a+b}{2}$$ $$\operatorname{Var}(X)=\frac{(b-a)^2}{12}$$
🤔 The geometric superpower

For two independent uniforms, the pair $(X,Y)$ lands uniformly in a rectangle. Any event like "$X\ge Y$" becomes a region of that rectangle, and probability = favourable area ÷ total area. You literally solve probability with school geometry — draw first, integrate only if you must.

Q · Two independent uniforms (NAT). $X\sim U[1,3]$ and $Y\sim U[2,4]$ independent. Find $P(X\ge Y)$ (3 dp).

Solution — step by step
  1. Draw the world: the rectangle $1\le x\le3$, $2\le y\le4$, total area $2\times2=4$.
  2. Shade the wish: $x\ge y$. Since $y$ is at least 2, we need $x$ at least 2 as well — the region only exists for $2\le y\le3$, where $x$ runs from $y$ to 3. It's a small triangle with legs of length 1.
  3. Favourable area $=\displaystyle\int_2^3(3-y)\,dy=\tfrac12$ (or just: half of a $1\times1$ square).
  4. $P=\dfrac{0.5}{4}=0.125$.

Answer: 0.125.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Mean = midpoint (symmetry). Variance: "range squared over twelve" — the 12 is the uniform's signature.
  • Two uniforms → draw the rectangle, shade the region, take the area ratio.
21 Normal distribution & the z-score
🌱 The idea in plain words

The bell curve appears everywhere — heights, measurement errors, exam marks — and Section 31 (CLT) explains the deep reason why: anything that is the sum of many small independent influences ends up bell-shaped.

  • A normal variable is written $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$: centre $\mu$, and — careful — the second slot is the variance $\sigma^2$, not the SD. So $N(0,100)$ has SD $10$, not $100$. This tiny notation detail costs real marks every year.
  • The z-score converts any normal question into one universal currency: "how many standard deviations away from the mean is this value?" Marks in different exams, heights in different units — all become comparable after the conversion.
$$Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\sim N(0,1)$$
  • Subtract $\mu$ — slide the centre to 0.
  • Divide by $\sigma$ — rescale so "one step" = one standard deviation.
  • What remains is the standard normal $Z$, the one all tables and $\Phi(\cdot)$ values refer to.

Q · Normal linear transformation. $X=aZ+b$ with $Z\sim N(0,1)$. Given $E[X]=1$, $E[(X-E[X])Z]=-2$, $E[(X-E[X])^2]=4$, find $a,b$.

A. $a=-2,\ b=1$    B. $a=2,\ b=-1$    C. $a=-2,\ b=-1$    D. $a=1,\ b=1$

Solution — step by step

Use the expectation rules from Section 15 — no distribution theory needed:

  1. $E[X]=a\underbrace{E[Z]}_{0}+b=b$, so $b=1$.
  2. $X-E[X]=aZ$, so $E[(X-E[X])Z]=a\,\underbrace{E[Z^2]}_{=\operatorname{Var}(Z)=1}=a$. Given as $-2$, so $a=-2$.
  3. Consistency check: $E[(aZ)^2]=a^2=4$ ✓ — the given variance agrees.

Answer: A.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • z-score in words: "(value − mean) ÷ SD = how many steps from home".
  • $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$: second slot is variance. See $N(0,100)$? Whisper "SD is 10".
  • The 68–95–99.7 anchor: about 68% within 1 SD, 95% within 2, 99.7% within 3.
22 Exponential — the patient (and forgetful) distribution
🌱 The idea in plain words

The exponential distribution measures waiting time until a random event: the next bus, the next customer, a machine's failure. Rate $\lambda$ = events per unit time; mean wait $=1/\lambda$ (faster rate, shorter wait — pleasingly logical).

Its famous personality trait is memorylessness: if you've already waited 5 minutes, the chance of waiting 5 more minutes is the same as it was at the start. The distribution doesn't remember your suffering. (Like a coin: ten tails in a row change nothing about the next flip.)

$$f(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}\ (x\ge0)$$ $$P(X\gt x)=e^{-\lambda x}$$ $$E[X]=\frac{1}{\lambda}$$ $$\operatorname{Var}(X)=\frac{1}{\lambda^2}$$
$$\text{Memorylessness: }\ P(X\gt s+t\mid X\gt s)=P(X\gt t)$$
🤔 Why is it memoryless?

The survival function is an exponential curve, and exponentials multiply: $e^{-\lambda(s+t)}=e^{-\lambda s}\cdot e^{-\lambda t}$. Divide out the "already survived $s$" part and only $e^{-\lambda t}$ remains — a fresh start. The algebra of exponents is the forgetting.

🔍 Spot it in the exam

"Waiting time", "lifetime", "time until failure" → exponential. And any conditional like $P(X\gt10\mid X\gt5)$ → don't compute, just shift the clock using memorylessness.

Q · Find the rate. $P(X\ge2)=0.25$. What is $\lambda$?

A. $\ln 2$    B. $\ln 4$    C. $\ln 3$    D. $\ln 0.25$

Solution — step by step
  1. Survival formula: $e^{-2\lambda}=\tfrac14$.
  2. Take logs: $-2\lambda=\ln\tfrac14=-\ln4$.
  3. $\lambda=\tfrac{\ln4}{2}=\tfrac{2\ln2}{2}=\ln2$.

Answer: A. (Log rule used: $\ln 4=2\ln 2$.)

Q · Mean–variance relation (NAT). $X\sim\text{Exp}(\lambda)$ with $5E[X]=\operatorname{Var}(X)$. Find $\lambda$ (1 dp).

Solution — step by step
  1. Substitute the formulas: $\dfrac{5}{\lambda}=\dfrac{1}{\lambda^2}$.
  2. Multiply both sides by $\lambda^2$: $5\lambda=1$, so $\lambda=0.2$.

Answer: 0.2.

Q · Memorylessness (GATE DA 2026, NAT). $P(X\gt5)=0.35$. Find $P(X\gt10\mid X\gt5)$ (2 dp).

Solution — step by step

Reset the clock: having survived 5, the chance of surviving 5 more equals the original chance of surviving 5.

$$P(X\gt10\mid X\gt5)=P(X\gt5)=0.35$$

Answer: 0.35. Zero computation — the whole question is "do you know the property?"

Q · Floor of an exponential. $Y=\lfloor X\rfloor$ where $X$ is exponential with mean $\tfrac{1}{\ln 10}$. For positive integer $\ell$, $P(Y{=}\ell)=q^{\ell}(1-q)$. Find $q$.

A. 0.1    B. 0.01    C. 0.5    D. 0.434

Solution — step by step
  1. Mean $=\tfrac1\lambda=\tfrac{1}{\ln10}$, so $\lambda=\ln 10$.
  2. $Y=\ell$ means $X$ landed in the window $[\ell,\ \ell+1)$: $P(Y{=}\ell)=P(X\ge\ell)-P(X\ge\ell+1)=e^{-\lambda\ell}-e^{-\lambda(\ell+1)}$.
  3. Factor: $=e^{-\lambda\ell}\left(1-e^{-\lambda}\right)$. Matching with $q^{\ell}(1-q)$ gives $q=e^{-\lambda}$.
  4. $q=e^{-\ln10}=\tfrac{1}{10}=0.1$.

Answer: A.

💬 Bonus insight: the floor of an exponential is a geometric distribution — the discrete memoryless cousin. Continuous forgetfulness rounds down to discrete forgetfulness.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Survival first: $P(X\gt x)=e^{-\lambda x}$ — most questions need this, not the PDF.
  • Mean $=\tfrac1\lambda$, variance $=\tfrac1{\lambda^2}$: variance is the mean, squared.
  • "Already waited…" → memoryless → reset the clock.
🔗 Connect the dots

Poisson and exponential are the same process seen twice: Poisson counts arrivals per interval, exponential times the gap between arrivals. And $\chi^2_2$ (Section 24) is secretly an exponential with mean 2.

23 Student's t vs normal — heavier tails
🌱 The idea in plain words

The t-distribution looks like the normal's slightly nervous sibling: same bell shape, same centre at 0, but fatter tails — it believes extreme values are more possible. Why does it exist? When you estimate the standard deviation from a small sample (instead of knowing it), that extra uncertainty spreads the bell out.

Two facts to keep: as degrees of freedom grow, $t$ calms down and becomes the normal; and at the extreme other end, $t_1$ is the wild Cauchy distribution.

Q · Compare normal and $t_1$ (GATE DA 2026, MSQ). $Z\sim N(0,1)$ with pdf $g$, CDF $G$; $Y\sim t_1$ with pdf $h$, CDF $H$. Let $c\gt0$ satisfy $g(c)=h(c)$. Which are correct?

A. $G(0)=H(0)$    B. $G(c)\lt H(c)$    C. $G(-c)\lt H(-c)$    D. $g(0)=h(0)$

Solution — step by step

No formulas needed — just the picture: two bells centred at 0, the normal taller in the middle, the $t_1$ fatter in the tails, crossing at $\pm c$.

  1. A — both symmetric about 0, so each has half its probability below 0: $G(0)=H(0)=\tfrac12$. True.
  2. B — between 0 and $c$ the normal curve is taller, so the normal has accumulated more by $c$: $G(c)\gt H(c)$. So B is false.
  3. C — mirror with symmetry: $G(-c)=1-G(c)$ and $H(-c)=1-H(c)$; since $G(c)\gt H(c)$, flipping gives $G(-c)\lt H(-c)$. True. (Fatter left tail = more probability far left — matches intuition.)
  4. D — peak heights differ: $g(0)=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\approx0.399$, while the Cauchy peak is $h(0)=\tfrac1\pi\approx0.318$. False.

Answer: A and C.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • "t = normal + fat tails." Small samples → more uncertainty → fatter tails.
  • df ↑ ⇒ t → normal. df $=1$ ⇒ Cauchy (so wild its mean doesn't exist).
  • Comparison questions: draw the two bells — the picture answers everything.
🔗 Connect the dots

This is exactly why the t-test (Section 34) exists: unknown $\sigma$, estimated from a small sample → use the fatter-tailed t, not the normal.

24 Chi-square — the distribution of squared errors
🌱 The idea in plain words

Take $k$ independent standard normals — think of them as $k$ independent "errors" — square each (making everything positive) and add. The result follows the chi-square distribution with $k$ degrees of freedom. It's the natural home of "total squared error", which is why it shows up in tests about variances and counts.

$$Z_1^2+Z_2^2+\cdots+Z_k^2\sim\chi^2_k$$ $$E[\chi^2_k]=k$$ $$\operatorname{Var}(\chi^2_k)=2k$$
🤔 Why is the mean exactly $k$?

Each $Z_i^2$ has expectation $E[Z_i^2]=\operatorname{Var}(Z_i)=1$ (since the mean is 0). Add $k$ of them — linearity gives $k$. The mean of a chi-square is its degree count. Elegant, and free to remember.

Q · Square of a standard normal. $Y=Z^2$ where $Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}$, $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$. What is the variance of $Y$?

A. 1    B. 2    C. 3    D. 4

Solution — step by step
  1. Standardizing makes $Z\sim N(0,1)$ (Section 21).
  2. One squared standard normal: $Y=Z^2\sim\chi^2_1$.
  3. Variance $=2k=2\times1=2$.

Answer: B.

Q · Sums of squared normals (GATE DA 2026, MSQ). $X_1,\dots,X_n$ iid $N(0,1)$, $\bar X=\tfrac1n\sum X_i$. Which are correct?

A. $\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2\sim\chi^2_n$
B. $\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i-\bar X)^2\sim\chi^2_{n-1}$
C. $X_1^2+X_n^2$ is exponential with mean 2
D. $(\sqrt n\,\bar X)^2\sim\chi^2_2$

Solution — step by step
  1. A — the definition itself: $n$ squared standard normals. True.
  2. B — replacing the true mean 0 by the estimated $\bar X$ costs one degree of freedom (the deviations are forced to sum to zero, so only $n-1$ of them are free). True — and this is exactly why sample variance divides by $n-1$.
  3. C — two squared normals give $\chi^2_2$, and a $\chi^2_2$ is the same thing as an exponential with mean 2. True.
  4. D — careful: $\sqrt n\,\bar X$ is a single standard normal, so its square is $\chi^2_1$, not $\chi^2_2$. False.

Answer: A, B and C.

⚠️ The trap in D: "there's an $n$ in the formula so maybe more degrees of freedom?" No — count how many independent normals got squared. Here: one.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Mean = df, variance = 2·df. The simplest mean–variance pair in the whole subject.
  • Degrees of freedom = number of independent standard normals squared. Estimating a mean first? Pay one df.
  • Special guest: $\chi^2_2$ = exponential with mean 2.
25 Joint distributions, marginals & covariance
🌱 The idea in plain words

So far we've watched one variable at a time. But real questions are about pairs: height and weight, study hours and marks. A joint distribution is the full table of "how often does this combination occur?"

Want just one variable back? Sum away the other — like collapsing a spreadsheet's rows into row-totals written in the margin. That's literally why it's called the marginal distribution.

$$P(X{=}x)=\sum_y P(X{=}x,Y{=}y)$$ $$f_X(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dy$$

Covariance asks: do the two variables move together? When $X$ is above its mean, is $Y$ usually above its mean too?

$$\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E\big[(X-E[X])(Y-E[Y])\big]=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]$$
🤔 Why does this measure "moving together"?

Look at the product $(X-E[X])(Y-E[Y])$. If both variables are above their means together, both factors are positive — product positive. Both below together — two negatives — product positive again! Only when they move oppositely does the product go negative. So the average of this product is a "togetherness score": positive = together, negative = opposite, zero = no linear pattern.

⚠️ The one-way street

Independence ⇒ covariance is zero. But the reverse is false: zero covariance does NOT prove independence. Covariance only detects linear togetherness — a perfect U-shaped relationship can hide at covariance zero. You'll see a stunning example of exactly this in the next section.

Q · Covariance of coin indicators (NAT). Two fair coins tossed independently. $X=1$ if both are heads, else 0. $Y=1$ if at least one head, else 0. Find $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$ (3 dp).

Solution — step by step

For 0/1 indicator variables there's a gift: $E[X]$ is just $P(X{=}1)$, and $E[XY]=P(\text{both}=1)$.

  1. $E[X]=P(HH)=\tfrac14$.
  2. $E[Y]=P(\text{at least one head})=1-P(TT)=\tfrac34$ (complement trick again!).
  3. Key observation: if $X=1$ (both heads), then certainly $Y=1$. So the product $XY$ equals $X$, giving $E[XY]=\tfrac14$.
  4. $\operatorname{Cov}=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]=\tfrac14-\tfrac14\cdot\tfrac34=\tfrac14-\tfrac{3}{16}=\tfrac{1}{16}=0.0625$.

Answer: 0.063.

💬 Positive covariance makes sense: "both heads" is a special case of "at least one head" — when one happens, the other must.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Marginal = "written in the margin" — sum out the variable you don't want.
  • Covariance sign: same-side deviations → positive; opposite sides → negative.
  • Indicators: $E[\text{indicator}]=P(\text{event})$ and $E[XY]=P(\text{both events})$ — no sums needed.
  • Independence ⇒ Cov $=0$. Never the arrow backwards.
26 Pearson & Spearman correlation
🌱 The idea in plain words

Covariance has an annoying flaw: its size depends on units. Measure height in cm instead of metres and the covariance explodes ×100 — same relationship, different number. Pearson correlation fixes this by dividing out both spreads, forcing the score into $[-1,1]$: $+1$ = perfect straight line uphill, $-1$ = perfect line downhill, $0$ = no linear pattern.

Spearman correlation plays the same game but on the ranks (1st, 2nd, 3rd…) instead of raw values. That makes it perfect for ordinal data (ratings, grades) and for monotonic-but-curved relationships — it only asks "when one goes up, does the other go up?", not "is it a straight line?"

$$\rho_{XY}=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\,\sigma_Y}$$ $$r=\frac{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sqrt{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2}\ \sqrt{\sum_i(y_i-\bar y)^2}}$$
🔍 Spot it in the exam

Numerical variables + "linear relationship" → Pearson. Ranks, ordinal data, or "monotonic" → Spearman. "Concordant/discordant pairs" vocabulary → same-direction pairs are concordant, opposite-direction are discordant.

Q · Pearson and Spearman (MSQ). Which are correct?

A. If $|r_{xy}|$ is close to 1, then $x,y$ have a linear relationship.
B. If $x,y$ have a linear relationship, then $|r_{xy}|$ is close to 1.
C. If $|r_S|$ is close to 1, then $x,y$ have a monotonic relationship.
D. If $|r_S|$ is close to 0, there is no monotonic association between $x,y$.

Solution — step by step

Intended answer: A and C — high $|r|$ signals a linear pattern; high $|r_S|$ signals a monotonic one.

💬 Why B and D fail: B — if "linear relationship" means a noisy linear trend, the noise can drag $r$ well below 1 (only an exact line guarantees $|r|=1$). D — "close to 0" rules out a strong monotonic pattern, but a weak or noisy one may still exist; "no association at all" is too absolute a claim.

Q · Spearman and concordant pairs. Which statements are not true?

I. Spearman rank correlation can be used for ordinal variables.
II. Pearson correlation takes a value between 0 and +1.
III. Two observations are concordant when one variable rises while the other falls.

A. II and III    B. I and III    C. I and II    D. III

Solution — step by step
  1. I — true: Spearman works on ranks, and ordinal data is exactly "rankable" data.
  2. II — not true: Pearson's range is $[-1,1]$; it can absolutely be negative.
  3. III — not true: rising-while-falling is discordant. Concordant = same direction.

Answer: A (II and III are the untrue ones).

Q · Calculate Pearson correlation. $X=\{5.1,4.9,4.7,4.6,5.0\}$, $Y=\{3.5,3.0,3.2,3.1,3.6\}$.

A. 1.00    B. 0.00    C. $-0.68$    D. 0.68

Solution — step by step
  1. Means: $\bar x=4.86$, $\bar y=3.28$.
  2. Cross-products of deviations: $\sum(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)=0.146$.
  3. Squared deviations: $\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2=0.172$ and $\sum(y_i-\bar y)^2=0.268$.
  4. $r=\dfrac{0.146}{\sqrt{0.172\times0.268}}\approx0.68$.

Answer: D. (Quick sanity check before computing: bigger $x$'s mostly pair with bigger $y$'s, so the sign must be positive — options B and C were never candidates.)

Q · Non-linear dependence with zero correlation. $X\sim U(-1,1)$ and $Y\mid X{=}x\sim U(x^2-0.1,\ x^2+0.1)$. Find $\operatorname{Corr}(X,Y)$.

Solution — step by step

Here $Y$ hugs the parabola $x^2$ — clearly dependent on $X$. Watch what correlation says anyway:

  1. Conditional mean = midpoint of the little interval: $E[Y\mid X{=}x]=x^2$.
  2. Tower property (Section 15!): $E[XY]=E\big[X\,E(Y\mid X)\big]=E[X\cdot X^2]=E[X^3]$.
  3. $X$ is symmetric around 0, so its odd powers average to zero: $E[X^3]=0$, and $E[X]=0$ too.
  4. $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]=0-0=0$, so $\operatorname{Corr}(X,Y)=0$.

Answer: 0.

⚠️ The lesson worth a whole mark

$Y$ depends on $X$ completely — yet the correlation is exactly zero, because the parabola rises on one side and falls symmetrically on the other, and the two halves cancel. Correlation is blind to symmetric, non-linear patterns. This is the promised proof that zero correlation ≠ independence.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Pearson = covariance with the units divided out. Range $[-1,1]$, always.
  • Pearson → linear. Spearman → monotonic (ranks).
  • Concordant = same direction ("con" = with). Discordant = opposite.
  • Parabola story: dependence with zero correlation — carry this counterexample into the exam.
27 Covariance matrix
🌱 The idea in plain words

With many features, we organize all the pairwise covariances into one tidy grid $\Sigma$: cell $(i,j)$ holds $\operatorname{Cov}(X_i,X_j)$. The diagonal holds each feature's covariance with itself — which is just its variance. The matrix is symmetric, since $\operatorname{Cov}(X_i,X_j)=\operatorname{Cov}(X_j,X_i)$.

Q. If a dataset contains $n$ features, is the variance-covariance matrix $n\times n$?

A. False    B. True

Solution — step by step

One row and one column per feature → $n\times n$. Answer: B.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Diagonal = variances; off-diagonal = covariances; always square, always symmetric.
  • This matrix is the star of PCA in your Linear Algebra track — its eigenvectors are the principal components.
28 Descriptive statistics — summarizing data honestly
🌱 The idea in plain words

Given a pile of numbers, we compress it into a few honest summaries. The mean is the balance point. The median is the middle person when everyone lines up — famously immune to billionaires walking into the room. The mode is the most common value. The standard deviation says how far typical values sit from the mean.

Q · Updated mean. The average of 50 data points is 40. What is the average after adding a new observation of value 142?

Solution — step by step

Means hide totals inside them. Work with the total, never with the averages directly:

  1. Old total $=50\times40=2000$.
  2. New total $=2000+142=2142$, new count $=51$.
  3. New mean $=2142/51=42$.

Answer: 42.

💬 Feel it: one unusually large value (142) nudged the mean of fifty people by 2 points. Means are sensitive souls — a preview of skewness in the next section.

Q · z-score normalization. Income attribute: min 46000, max 170000, mean 96000, SD 21000. The z-score-normalized value of ₹106000 is closest to:

A. 0.217    B. 0.476    C. 0.623    D. 2.304

Solution — step by step
  1. z-score recipe (Section 21): $z=\dfrac{x-\mu}{\sigma}=\dfrac{106000-96000}{21000}$.
  2. $=\dfrac{10000}{21000}\approx0.476$.

Answer: B.

⚠️ The distractors: the min and max belong to a different technique (min-max normalization). The question plants them hoping you'll grab the wrong recipe. z-score uses only mean and SD.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Updating a mean? Totals, not averages. (Never average two averages with different group sizes.)
  • z-score needs $(\mu,\sigma)$; min-max needs (min, max). Match the recipe to the ingredients the question actually uses.
  • Median resists outliers; mean chases them.
29 Skewness — which way does the tail point?
🌱 The idea in plain words

Think of incomes in a city: most people earn moderate amounts, but a few earn enormously — the histogram has a long tail stretching right. Those few huge values drag the mean upward (means chase outliers, as we just saw), while the median barely moves and the mode stays at the crowd's peak. Hence, for a right-skewed distribution: mode < median < mean. Mirror the picture for left skew.

$$\text{Right-skewed: }\ \text{Mean}\gt\text{Median}\gt\text{Mode}$$ $$\text{Left-skewed: }\ \text{Mean}\lt\text{Median}\lt\text{Mode}$$

Q · Mean, median and mode (MSQ). Which usually hold?

A. Left-skewed: Mean > Median > Mode    B. Right-skewed: Mean < Median < Mode
C. Left-skewed: Mean < Median < Mode    D. Right-skewed: Mean > Median > Mode

Solution — step by step

One sentence solves it: the mean follows the tail. Right tail → mean dragged right (largest); left tail → mean dragged left (smallest). The mode stays at the peak, and the median sits in between.

Answer: C and D.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • "The mean follows the tail." Five words, whole topic.
  • Income and house prices → classic right skew (mean salary > median salary — now you know why news reports prefer one or the other!).
  • Alphabetical bonus: for right skew, Mean > Median > Mode is alphabetical order.
30 Q–Q plots — comparing shapes by eye
🌱 The idea in plain words

"Does my data follow a normal distribution?" A Q–Q plot answers visually. Line up your sorted data's quantiles against the theoretical distribution's quantiles and plot the pairs. If the data really follows that distribution, the pairs march along a straight line. Bends mean shape mismatch; drifting ends mean different tails; lonely points mean outliers.

Q · Exponential Q–Q plot (MSQ). An exponential Q–Q plot is made for a sample that follows the exponential distribution. Which are correct?

A. Points lie only in the first and third quadrants.
B. Points fall along the straight line through the origin with slope 1.
C. Points lie only in the first quadrant.
D. Points fall along any arbitrary straight line through the origin.

Solution — step by step
  1. Exponential values are never negative, so both coordinates of every point are $\ge0$: everything lives in the first quadrant — C true, A false (nothing can reach the third quadrant).
  2. The sample matches the same fully specified exponential, so sample quantiles ≈ theoretical quantiles: the line $y=x$, slope 1 — B true, D too vague.

Answer: B and C.

💬 Fine print worth knowing: if the sample were exponential with a different scale, the plot would still be straight but with slope ≠ 1 — straightness tests the family, slope tests the parameter.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Straight line = same shape. Slope = scale. Bends = mismatch. Stray points = outliers.
  • Non-negative distribution → Q–Q points live in the first quadrant only.
31 Central Limit Theorem — why the bell is everywhere
🌱 The idea in plain words

Here is the most magical theorem in statistics. Take any distribution — lopsided, spiky, weird. Draw $n$ independent values and average them. Repeat. The averages themselves form a distribution and, as $n$ grows, that distribution of averages becomes a normal bell curve — no matter what shape you started from!

Why? In an average, no single observation dominates; the quirks of individual values wash out, and what survives is the accumulation of many small independent nudges — which is precisely the recipe for a bell (Section 21). This is why the normal distribution keeps appearing in nature: heights, errors, totals — all are sums of many small effects.

$$\bar X\approx N\!\left(\mu,\ \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)$$ $$SE(\bar X)=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}$$
  • Centre stays at $\mu$ — averaging doesn't move the target.
  • Spread shrinks by $n$: averages are steadier than individuals. That's why bigger samples are trusted more.
  • $SE$ (standard error) = the SD of the average = $\sigma/\sqrt n$. Notice the square root: to halve your error, you need four times the data.
⚠️ What the CLT does NOT say

It does not say your data becomes normal with enough of it. Incomes stay skewed forever. Only the sample mean (or sum) becomes normal. Averages normalize; observations don't.

Q · Binomial sum via CLT. $Y=\sum_{i=1}^{300}X_i$, $X_i\sim\text{Bernoulli}(0.25)$ iid. Approximate $P(60\le Y\le90)$.

A. $\Phi(2)-\Phi(-2)$    B. $\Phi(1)-\Phi(-1)$    C. $\Phi(3)-\Phi(-3)$    D. $\Phi(90)-\Phi(60)$

Solution — step by step

Large sum of independent trials → CLT. The routine: find the mean, find the SD, convert the endpoints to z-scores.

  1. Mean: $E[Y]=np=300\times0.25=75$.
  2. Variance: $np(1-p)=300\times0.25\times0.75=56.25$, so $SD=7.5$.
  3. z-scores of the endpoints: $z_L=\dfrac{60-75}{7.5}=-2$ and $z_U=\dfrac{90-75}{7.5}=+2$.
  4. $P(60\le Y\le90)\approx\Phi(2)-\Phi(-2)$.

Answer: A. (Option D is the classic bait: plugging raw values into $\Phi$ without standardizing.)

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • "Averages become normal; data stays whatever it was."
  • SE $=\sigma/\sqrt n$ — the $\sqrt{}$ means: 4× data → only 2× precision.
  • CLT routine: mean → SD → z-scores → $\Phi$. Same four steps every time.
🔗 Connect the dots

The CLT is the engine under every hypothesis test in Sections 33–34: it's what lets us treat sample means as normal and compute p-values at all.

32 Sample proportion — polls in one formula
🌱 The idea in plain words

A poll asks 1000 people a yes/no question; $\hat p$ is the fraction of yeses. Since each answer is a Bernoulli trial, $\hat p$ is just a sample mean of 0s and 1s — so everything from the last section applies directly: it centres on the true $p$, and it steadies as $n$ grows.

$$\hat p=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i$$ $$E[\hat p]=p$$ $$\operatorname{Var}(\hat p)=\frac{p(1-p)}{n}$$

Q · Sample proportion (MSQ). A coin shows heads with probability $p$; over $n$ independent tosses define $\hat p$ as above. Which are correct?

A. $E[\hat p]=p$    B. $E[\hat p]=p/n$    C. As $n$ increases, $\operatorname{Var}(\hat p)$ decreases    D. $\operatorname{Var}(\hat p)$ doesn't depend on $n$

Solution — step by step

Straight from the formulas: the estimate is unbiased ($E[\hat p]=p$ — A true, B false), and the $n$ in the denominator of the variance means more tosses → steadier estimate (C true, D false).

Answer: A and C.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • $\hat p$ is a disguised sample mean → inherits everything from the CLT.
  • Variance $p(1-p)/n$ peaks at $p=\tfrac12$: a 50–50 question is the hardest to poll accurately.
33 Hypothesis testing — a courtroom for claims
🌱 The idea in plain words

Hypothesis testing is a courtroom. The null hypothesis $H_0$ is the defendant — "nothing special is happening" — and it's presumed innocent. Your data is the evidence. The p-value asks one precise question: "if $H_0$ really were true, how surprising would evidence this extreme be?"

Very surprising (small p-value) → the innocence story doesn't hold → reject $H_0$. Not so surprising → we fail to reject — which, like a courtroom, means "not enough evidence to convict", never "proven innocent". The threshold of surprise is $\alpha$ (usually 0.05), chosen before looking at data.

$$p\text{-value}\lt\alpha\ \Rightarrow\ \text{reject }H_0$$ $$p\text{-value}\ge\alpha\ \Rightarrow\ \text{fail to reject }H_0$$

Two ways the court can go wrong — learn them as characters:

ErrorWhat happenedProbabilityCharacter
Type IRejected a true $H_0$ (convicted the innocent)$\alpha$🚨 False alarm
Type IIFailed to reject a false $H_0$ (guilty walked free)$\beta$😴 Missed detection
$$\text{Power}=1-\beta\quad\text{(the test's ability to catch a real effect)}$$

Q · Interpret a p-value. A two-sided t-test at $\alpha=0.05$ tests $H_0:\mu=50$ vs $H_1:\mu\ne50$; the p-value is 0.03. Which statement is correct?

A. The test should be converted into a one-sided test.
B. The null hypothesis is rejected at the 5% significance level.
C. The null hypothesis is accepted because $0.03\lt0.05$.
D. A Type II error has definitely occurred.

Solution — step by step
  1. $0.03\lt0.05$: the evidence is more surprising than our threshold → reject $H_0$. That's option B.
  2. A — changing the test's direction after seeing the result is cheating (like moving the goalposts mid-game). Invalid.
  3. C — backwards twice: small p-value means reject, and we never "accept" $H_0$ anyway.
  4. D — a Type II error means failing to reject a false null; we rejected, so a Type II error is impossible here.

Answer: B.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Courtroom: $H_0$ presumed innocent; p-value = "how surprising is this evidence if innocent?"
  • "If p is low, the null must go."
  • Type I = false alarm (probability $\alpha$); Type II = missed detection (probability $\beta$); Power $=1-\beta$.
  • "Fail to reject" ≠ "accept". Courts don't declare innocence; they declare insufficient evidence.
34 Choosing the right test — z, t, chi-square, F
🌱 The idea in plain words

Choosing a test is not guesswork — it's a two-question flowchart. Question 1: what am I testing? A mean, category counts, or variances? Question 2 (means only): do I know the population SD? Known σ → z-test. Unknown σ (estimated from the sample) → t-test, because the extra uncertainty demands the fatter-tailed t (Section 23). Counts → chi-square (the home of squared errors, Section 24). Comparing two variances → F-test (literally a ratio of variances).

TestUse when…Statistic
z-testTesting a mean; population $\sigma$ known$z=\dfrac{\bar x-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt n}$
t-testTesting a mean; $\sigma$ unknown — use sample $s$; $df=n-1$$t=\dfrac{\bar x-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt n}$
Chi-squareCategorical counts: goodness-of-fit, independence$\chi^2=\sum\dfrac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i}$
F-testComparing two variances$F=\dfrac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}$
🤔 Why do z and t look identical except one letter?

Both measure the same thing: "how many standard errors is my sample mean from the claimed value?" The only difference is whether the SD in the denominator is the true $\sigma$ (z) or the estimated $s$ (t). Estimation adds wobble, wobble fattens tails, fat tails = t-distribution. One letter, one idea.

Q · Choose the F-test. Which situation most appropriately requires an F-test?

A. Testing whether a population mean equals a value when the variance is known.
B. Testing whether two population variances are equal.
C. Testing independence between categorical variables.
D. Testing a sample mean against a hypothesized value for a small sample.

Solution — step by step

Run the flowchart: A tests a mean with known σ → z-test. C is category counts → chi-square. D is a mean with a small sample (σ unknown) → t-test. B compares variances → F-test.

Answer: B.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Flowchart: mean? → σ known: z / σ unknown: t. counts? → chi-square. variances? → F.
  • Alphabet hook: F for Fraction of variances; chi-square for categories.
  • t-test df $=n-1$ — one degree paid for estimating the mean (same toll as in Section 24!).
35 Simple linear regression & OLS
🌱 The idea in plain words

You suspect study hours affect marks. Regression draws the best straight line through the cloud of (hours, marks) points. The model says: marks = a starting level, plus a fixed gain per hour, plus everything else we couldn't measure (mood, luck, sleep) bundled into an error term.

$$y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1 x_i+\epsilon_i$$
  • $x_i$ — input (study hours).   $y_i$ — observed output (marks).
  • $\beta_0$ — intercept: predicted $y$ when $x=0$, the line's starting height.
  • $\beta_1$ — slope: extra $y$ per unit of $x$. The number people actually argue about.
  • $\epsilon_i$ — error: everything the line can't explain. The "noise" from Section 9's pattern + noise split.
  • One input, one output → SISO. After fitting: $\hat y_i=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1x_i$, and the leftover $e_i=y_i-\hat y_i$ is the residual — the visible cousin of the invisible $\epsilon_i$.
🤔 What does "least squares" actually do?

Each candidate line leaves gaps (residuals) between itself and the points. OLS chooses the line minimizing the sum of squared gaps — squared for the same reasons as variance (Section 15): no cancellation, big misses punished more. Setting the derivative of that sum to zero gives the classic first-order conditions — one of which forces the residuals to sum to exactly zero, meaning the line passes through the heart of the data.

The classical OLS assumptions (the fine print under the sale): the relationship is linear in the parameters; errors average zero; errors have constant variance; observations/errors are independent; no perfect multicollinearity; and the $x$ values are measured accurately.

Q · Regression model properties (MSQ). For $y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1x_i+\epsilon_i$, which are true?

A. The regression is MISO: multi-input, single-output.
B. The regression is SISO: single-input, single-output.
C. It is assumed that the independent variable is measured without error.
D. $\partial SSE/\partial\beta_0=0$ gives $\sum_{i=1}^n\epsilon_i=0$, where $SSE=\sum_{i=1}^n\epsilon_i^2$.

Solution — step by step
  1. One $x$, one $y$ → SISO. B true, A false.
  2. The classical model treats $x$ as exact — C true.
  3. For D: $SSE=\sum(y_i-\beta_0-\beta_1x_i)^2$. Differentiate w.r.t. $\beta_0$ and set to zero: $-2\sum e_i=0$, so $\sum e_i=0$. True — the "residuals sum to zero" fact from the why-box.

Answer: B, C and D.

Q · OLS assumptions (MSQ). Which are assumptions of ordinary least squares?

A. Measurements of independent variables are accurate.
B. Errors have expected value zero.
C. The relationship between dependent and independent variables is linear.
D. Errors have constant variance.

Solution — step by step

All four appear on the classical list above. Answer: A, B, C and D.

Q · Interpreting unbiasedness. If the OLS assumptions hold, $E[\hat\beta_0]=\beta_0$ and $E[\hat\beta_1]=\beta_1$. Select the correct option.

A. The true parameter values are usually unknown because the population or data-generating process is unknown.
B. The true parameter values can always be calculated exactly from a finite sample.

Solution — step by step

"Unbiased" is a statement about repeated sampling: if you could redo the study many times, the estimates would centre on the truth. It does not mean any single sample hands you the exact truth — the truth stays unknown; we just know our aim isn't systematically off.

Answer: A.

🧠 Analogy: an unbiased archer's arrows centre on the bullseye on average — but any single arrow still misses by some amount.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Read the model aloud: "output = start + rate × input + noise".
  • OLS = "minimize squared gaps". Residuals sum to zero — the line balances the points.
  • Unbiased = correct on average across samples, not exact in one sample.
36 $R^2$ — how much does the line explain?
🌱 The idea in plain words

Before fitting any line, your best guess for every $y$ is just the average $\bar y$ — total guessing error: $SST$. After fitting the line, the remaining error is $SSE$. $R^2$ measures how much of the original error the line removed: $R^2=0.9$ means the line explains 90% of the variation that plain averaging couldn't.

$$R^2=1-\frac{SSE}{SST}$$ $$SSE=\sum(y_i-\hat y_i)^2\quad\text{(after the line)}$$ $$SST=\sum(y_i-\bar y)^2\quad\text{(before the line)}$$

Bonus connection: in simple linear regression with an intercept, $R^2$ equals the squared Pearson correlation, $r^2$. Correlation and regression are two views of the same line.

Q · Linearity vs correlation (MSQ). Statement A: $X$ and $Y$ have a linear relationship. Statement B: Pearson correlation for $X,Y$ is close to $+1$ or $-1$. Which options are correct?

A. B implies A.    B. A implies B.    C. B does not necessarily imply A.    D. A does not necessarily imply B.

Solution — step by step

Supplied answer: B and C. Reading "linear relationship" as an exact line: A ⇒ B (exact non-horizontal line forces $|r|=1$). Meanwhile $r$ close to $\pm1$ suggests but doesn't prove exact linearity — a strong-but-curved trend can also score high. Hence C.

⚠️ Exam caution: this wording is genuinely slippery — if "linear relationship" means a noisy linear trend, A no longer forces $|r|\approx1$. Decide from the paper's context which reading is intended.

Q · Strong $R^2$. Which $R^2$ value indicates a strong linear relationship?

A. 0.90    B. 0.00    C. 0.30    D. $-0.86$

Solution — step by step

0.90 = the line explains 90% of the variation — strong. 0.00 explains nothing; 0.30 is weak. And in ordinary in-sample OLS with an intercept, $R^2$ can't be negative at all, so D is out (negative $R^2$ only appears on test data or intercept-free fits — when the model does worse than guessing the average).

Answer: A.

Q · Observations and $R^2$. The higher the value of $R^2$, the more closely the observations group around:

A. Average values of the predicted variable    B. The intercept    C. The origin    D. The best-fit line

Solution — step by step

High $R^2$ = small residuals = points hugging the fitted line. Answer: D.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • $R^2$ = "fraction of the variation my line explains". 1 = perfect, 0 = no better than the average.
  • Simple regression with intercept: $R^2=r^2$ — correlation squared.
  • High $R^2$ → points hug the line.
37 Linear boundaries & feature transformation
🌱 The idea in plain words

A classifier separating two classes in 2-D draws a decision boundary. "Linear boundary" simply means: the boundary is a straight line — any straight line, written $w_1x+w_2y+b=0$. Horizontal, vertical, diagonal, through the origin or not: all lines, all linear (affine) boundaries.

And here's the trick that powers kernel methods and neural features: a curved boundary can become a straight one if you change the coordinates. Live in the right feature space and hard problems become easy.

Q · Identify linear boundaries (MSQ). Which are linear decision boundaries for binary classification in 2-D feature space $(x,y)$?

A. $y=a_0+a_1x$    B. $y=a_0$    C. $y=x$    D. $y=a_1x$

Solution — step by step

A general line, a horizontal line, the diagonal, a line through the origin — every option is a straight line. Don't overthink: special cases of lines are still lines.

Answer: A, B, C and D.

Q · Circular boundary transformation. In $(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}^2$, class 0 satisfies $x^2+y^2\le c^2$ (inside a circle) and class 1 is outside. Define $z=x^2+y^2$. Can the classes be separated by a linear decision boundary in the transformed 1-D $z$-space?

A. True    B. False

Solution — step by step

The new coordinate $z$ is "squared distance from the centre". In $z$-space, class 0 is $z\le c^2$ and class 1 is $z\gt c^2$ — separated by the single threshold $z=c^2$, which is a linear boundary in one dimension.

Answer: A.

💬 The moral: the circle was never the problem — the coordinates were. Measure the world by "distance from centre" and the curved boundary straightens out. This is the seed idea of kernel methods.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Linear boundary = any straight line (or plane, in higher dimensions).
  • Curved in the original space? Ask: "is there a feature in which it's straight?" Circle → use $z=x^2+y^2$.
38 Correlation vs causation & analytics basics
🌱 The idea in plain words

Ice-cream sales and drowning deaths rise together every year. Does ice cream cause drowning? Of course not — summer causes both. Two variables can move together because one causes the other, because the second causes the first (reverse causation), because a hidden third variable drives both (a confounder, like summer), because of how the data was selected, or by plain coincidence.

That's why the mantra exists: correlation alone does not prove causation. Establishing causation needs more — controlled experiments, or careful causal reasoning beyond the correlation itself.

Q · Smoking and lung cancer. Let $A$ = average cigarettes smoked per day and $B$ = whether a person has lung cancer. A study finds $A$ and $B$ are correlated. Select the correct option.

A. A causal relationship exists owing to the harmful effects of cigarettes, not merely because the two variables are correlated.
B. A causal relationship exists because the variables are correlated.
C. None of the above.
D. No causal relationship exists.

Solution — step by step

Read the options as reasons: B claims the correlation itself proves causation — that's exactly the fallacy. A says causation exists, but on independent biological evidence, with the correlation merely consistent with it. That's the correct scientific posture.

Answer: A.

Q · Analytics, classification and imputation (MSQ). Which statements are true?

A. Data analytics is particularly useful for high-dimensional data that cannot be completely visualized using ordinary plots.
B. In supervised classification, data points have class labels.
C. Data imputation can be regarded as a function-approximation problem.
D. The same data may be separable by a linear boundary and also by a non-linear boundary.

Solution — step by step
  1. A — true: you can't draw 100-dimensional data; analytics summarizes what eyes can't see.
  2. B — true: "supervised" literally means learning from labelled examples.
  3. C — true: filling a missing value = predicting it from the other features, i.e. approximating a function.
  4. D — true: if a straight line separates the data, a wiggly curve certainly can too.

Answer: A, B, C and D.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Ice cream & drowning — the confounder story. One story, whole concept.
  • Five reasons for correlation: cause, reverse cause, confounder, selection, coincidence.
39 Entropy & information gain — how decision trees think
🌱 The idea in plain words

Entropy measures how mixed up a group's labels are. A basket of only red balls: zero surprise, entropy 0. Half red half blue: maximum confusion, entropy 1 bit. Entropy is the "impurity meter" of a dataset.

A decision tree grows by asking questions ("is the pitch spin-friendly?") that split the data. A good question produces child groups that are purer than the parent. Information gain scores a question: impurity before − (weighted) impurity after. The tree greedily asks the highest-gain question at every step. That's the entire algorithm's heart.

$$H(C)=-\sum_{i=1}^{k}p_i\log_2 p_i$$ $$IG(C,A)=H(C)-\sum_v \frac{|C_v|}{|C|}\,H(C_v)$$
  • $p_i$ — fraction of the group in class $i$. The $\log_2$ makes the unit "bits".
  • $H=0$ — pure group (one class only). $H=1$ bit — a perfect 50–50 mix of two classes.
  • $C_v$ — the child group with attribute value $v$; each child's entropy is weighted by its share of the data $\tfrac{|C_v|}{|C|}$.
🔍 Spot it in the exam

A table of examples + "split on attribute…" + "information gain" → always the same three computations: parent entropy, each child's entropy, then subtract the weighted average. Keep $\log_2$ values for common fractions handy.

Q · Cricket matches and pitch (image-based, NAT). Ten matches between Green and Blue. Spin-friendly (S) pitches: matches 1, 2, 4, 7, 10 with winners G, B, B, G, G. Pace-friendly (F) pitches: matches 3, 5, 6, 8, 9 with winners B, G, B, B, B. Overall: Green won 4, Blue won 6. Find the information gain from splitting Winner by Pitch (2 dp).

Solution — step by step
  1. Parent impurity (4 Green, 6 Blue): $H(C)=-0.4\log_2 0.4-0.6\log_2 0.6\approx0.97095$ bits. Quite mixed — nearly a coin flip.
  2. Spin child (3 G, 2 B out of 5): $H(S)=-\tfrac35\log_2\tfrac35-\tfrac25\log_2\tfrac25\approx0.97095$. Still very mixed — spin tells us little.
  3. Pace child (1 G, 4 B out of 5): $H(F)=-\tfrac15\log_2\tfrac15-\tfrac45\log_2\tfrac45\approx0.72193$. Purer — pace pitches lean Blue.
  4. Weighted impurity after the split (each child holds 5 of the 10 matches): $0.5\times0.97095+0.5\times0.72193\approx0.84644$.
  5. Information gain: $0.97095-0.84644=0.12451$.

Answer: 0.12.

💬 What the number means: knowing the pitch type removes about 0.12 bits of our uncertainty about the winner — a modest but real clue. A tree would compare this against the gain from splitting on Format, and ask the better question first.

🧠 Tricks to remember
  • Entropy = impurity meter: 0 = pure, 1 bit = 50–50 chaos (two classes).
  • Gain = mess before − weighted mess after.
  • Weights = child sizes as fractions of the parent. Forgetting the weights is the #1 error.
  • Useful anchors: $H(0.5,0.5)=1$; $H(0.4,0.6)\approx0.971$; $H(0.2,0.8)\approx0.722$.
🔗 Connect the dots

Entropy is expectation (Section 15) applied to "surprise" $-\log_2 p$. Information gain is the decision-tree cousin of "explained variation" ($R^2$, Section 36) — both measure how much a question reduces confusion.

  • Unknown PDF endpoint → total integral equals 1.
  • CDF interval probability → upper CDF minus lower CDF.
  • Sample mean or large sum → CLT.
  • Pearson measures linear association.
  • Spearman measures monotonic rank association.
  • Zero correlation does not prove independence.
  • The mean follows the long tail in a skewed distribution.
  • Known population σ → z-test.
  • Unknown population σ → t-test.
  • Categorical counts → chi-square.
  • Compare variances → F-test.
  • Decision-tree split → parent entropy minus weighted child entropy.
  • Bayesian-network joint → multiply every node conditioned on its parents.
