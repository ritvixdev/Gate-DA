Conditional & Bayes

8 questions. Tap any option — you'll see why it's right or wrong, and the correct one lights up.

Q1. $P(A\cap B)=0.2,\ P(B)=0.5$. $P(A\mid B)=?$
That's the product $0.2\times0.5$, not the ratio.
✅ Correct — $P(A\mid B)=0.2/0.5=0.4$.
That would be a sum; use the ratio.
You divided the wrong way — it's $P(A\cap B)/P(B)$.
Q2. $A,B$ independent, $P(A)=0.3,\ P(B)=0.5$. $P(A\cap B)=?$
That's the sum, not the joint.
✅ Correct — independence ⇒ $0.3\times0.5=0.15$.
That would be mutually exclusive, not independent.
Multiply the two: $0.15$.
Q3. Independence is equivalent to…
That's mutually exclusive.
Should equal $P(A)$, not $P(B)$.
✅ Correct — knowing $B$ doesn't change $A$: $P(A\mid B)=P(A)$.
Equal probabilities don't imply independence.
Q4. Bayes' theorem says the posterior is proportional to…
It's a product, not a quotient.
✅ Correct — posterior $\propto$ likelihood × prior.
The prior matters too (base rate).
The likelihood (evidence) matters too.
Q5. Two urns, picked equally. Urn1: $P(\text{red})=0.8$; Urn2: $0.2$. Overall $P(\text{red})$?
That ignores urn 2.
✅ Correct — total probability $0.5(0.8)+0.5(0.2)=0.5$.
Can't exceed either conditional weighted average.
That's a product; use the weighted sum.
Q6. Is $P(A\mid B)$ always equal to $P(B\mid A)$?
No — confusing the two is the classic Bayes error.
✅ Correct — they're equal only when $P(A)=P(B)$; Bayes relates them otherwise.
Disjointness makes both $0$, a trivial case.
$P(A)=0$ makes the conditional undefined.
Q7. A rare disease + accurate test gives a low $P(\text{disease}\mid+)$ because…
No — even a good test is overwhelmed by false positives from the healthy majority.
Probabilities stay in $[0,1]$.
✅ Correct — most positives come from the large healthy group; the prior dominates.
Bayes is exactly what explains it.
Q8. The multiplication rule for any $A,B$ is…
That's (part of) the addition rule.
True only when independent.
✅ Correct — $P(A\cap B)=P(A\mid B)P(B)$ holds always.
That's the conditional formula rearranged wrongly.
