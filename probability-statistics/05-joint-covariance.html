Joint Distributions
& Covariance

When two random variables move together, how do we describe the pair? Joint distributions, plus covariance and correlation that measure how they relate.

2 Why this topic exists

Real data has many variables at once (height & weight, ad spend & sales). A joint distribution describes them together, and covariance / correlation tell us whether they rise and fall together — the foundation of regression, PCA, and feature relationships in ML.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

A joint distribution is a scatter cloud of $(X,Y)$ points. Marginals are the shadows cast on each axis. Positive covariance = the cloud tilts up-right (big $X$ with big $Y$); negative = tilts down-right; zero = a round blob with no tilt.

4 Basic definitions
  • Joint distribution: $p(x,y)=P(X=x,Y=y)$ (discrete) or $f(x,y)$ (continuous).
  • Marginal: the distribution of one variable alone — sum/integrate the other out.
  • Conditional distribution: the distribution of $X$ once $Y$ is fixed.
  • Independence: $X,Y$ independent if $p(x,y)=p(x)\,p(y)$ for all $x,y$.
  • Covariance: signed measure of how two variables move together (+ together, − opposite).
  • Correlation $\rho$: covariance rescaled to $[-1,1]$ — strength of a linear link.
  • Uncorrelated ≠ independent: $\rho=0$ rules out a linear link, not every dependence.
📖 Study in Depth — joint/marginal, covariance, correlation, rank correlation & regression from zero
1. Joint distribution — two variables together

When two random variables are studied as a pair, we use a joint distribution:

p(x, y) = P(X = x AND Y = y)        (discrete)
f(x, y)                            (continuous)

It answers "how likely is this combination?" — e.g. X = first die, Y = second die.

2. The joint probability table

For two fair dice every pair is equally likely:

P(X = x, Y = y) = 1/36   for each of the 36 (x,y) pairs

Lay it out as a grid: 6 rows (X) × 6 columns (Y), each cell 1/36, all cells summing to 1.

3. Marginal distribution — one variable alone

The marginal is the distribution of one variable when we ignore the other, by summing it out:

P(X = x) = Σ_y P(X = x, Y = y)      (sum across all y)
P(Y = y) = Σ_x P(X = x, Y = y)      (sum across all x)

Continuous version: f(x) = ∫ f(x,y) dy.

4. Why “marginal”? — row & column totals

In the joint table, summing a row gives that X-value's probability; summing a column gives that Y-value's. These totals are written in the margins of the table — hence the name.

marginal of X = row totals
marginal of Y = column totals
5. Two-dice marginal example

Find P(X = 3): first die is 3, second die anything — sum that row.

P(X = 3) = Σ_{y=1..6} P(X=3, Y=y) = 6 × 1/36 = 6/36 = 1/6

We "marginalized out" Y by summing over all its values.

6. Marginalization in ML & statistics

When a joint model has a variable you don't care about, sum (or integrate) it away:

P(X) = Σ_Y P(X, Y)            (discrete)
p(x) = ∫ p(x, y) dy           (continuous)

This "averaging over the unknown" powers Bayesian inference, Naive Bayes, hidden Markov models and probabilistic graphical models.

7. Independence of random variables

X and Y are independent when the joint factorizes into the marginals:

p(x, y) = p(x) · p(y)        for every x, y

Two dice are independent (each cell 1/36 = 1/6 × 1/6). Independence of variables generalizes event independence.

8. Covariance — do they move together? GATE extra

Covariance measures whether X and Y rise and fall together:

Cov(X,Y) = E[(X−E X)(Y−E Y)] = E[XY] − E[X]·E[Y]
Cov > 0 → move together · Cov < 0 → move oppositely · Cov = 0 → no linear link
Cov(X,X) = Var(X)
9. Covariance — worked GATE extra 
E[X]=2, E[Y]=3, E[XY]=8  →  Cov = 8 − 2·3 = 2 > 0  (rise together)
if instead E[XY]=6 = E[X]E[Y]  →  Cov = 0  (uncorrelated)

Scaling rule: Cov(aX, bY) = ab·Cov(X,Y).

10. Correlation ρ — covariance on a fixed scale GATE extra

Covariance's size depends on units, so we rescale it to a unitless number in [−1, 1]:

ρ = Cov(X,Y) / (σ_X · σ_Y) ,   −1 ≤ ρ ≤ 1

ρ = ±1 → perfect straight-line relationship; ρ = 0 → no linear relationship.

11. Variance of a sum 
Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) + 2·Cov(X,Y)

If X and Y are independent (or just uncorrelated), the covariance term vanishes and variances simply add.

12. Zero covariance ≠ independent GATE extra

Independence ⇒ Cov = 0. But the reverse can fail — a perfectly curved relationship can have zero covariance yet be fully dependent.

X uniform on {−2,−1,0,1,2}, Y = X²:  Cov(X,Y) = 0, but Y is determined by X

Covariance only sees the linear part of a relationship.

13. Spearman's rank correlation GATE extra

When data is ranked/ordinal, or the relationship is monotonic but not straight-line, correlate the ranks instead of the values:

ρ_s = 1 − 6·Σd² / [ n(n² − 1) ]      d = (rank of X) − (rank of Y)
ranks X:1,2,3,4,5  Y:2,1,4,3,5 → d:−1,1,−1,1,0 → Σd²=4
ρ_s = 1 − 6(4)/(5·24) = 1 − 24/120 = 0.8

Range [−1, 1]; ρ_s = 1 = perfectly increasing ranks. It catches any monotonic link, even a curved one Pearson's r would understate.

14. Regression line — the line of best fit GATE extra

Correlation measures strength; regression gives the actual line to predict Y from X:

ŷ = a + b·x      (least squares: choose a, b to minimise Σ(y − ŷ)²)

The fitted line always passes through the means point (x̄, ȳ).

15. Regression coefficient (slope & intercept) GATE extra

The slope is covariance scaled by the spread of X:

b = Cov(X,Y) / Var(X) = r · (σ_Y / σ_X)        intercept  a = ȳ − b·x̄
Cov=4, Var(X)=8 → b = 0.5 ; if x̄=10, ȳ=25 → a = 25 − 0.5(10) = 20
line:  ŷ = 20 + 0.5x

Sign of b = sign of the covariance: positive slope ⇒ Y rises with X.

16. Two regression lines & the link to r GATE extra

Predicting Y from X is not the same line as predicting X from Y — there are two regression coefficients:

b_yx = Cov/Var(X)     b_xy = Cov/Var(Y)
b_yx · b_xy = r²      (so |r| = √(b_yx · b_xy), the geometric mean of the slopes)

Both lines cross at (x̄, ȳ); they coincide only when |r| = 1. Because r² ≤ 1, the slopes can't both be large.

17. Common GATE traps
  • Marginal = conditional? No — marginal sums out the other variable; conditional fixes it.
  • Zero correlation ⇒ independent? No — only the linear link is gone.
  • Correlation can be 2? No — always in [−1, 1].
  • Var(X+Y) = VarX + VarY always? No — add 2·Cov unless uncorrelated.

Q1. Two fair dice — find P(Y = 5).

Q2. E[XY]=10, E[X]=2, E[Y]=4 — covariance?

10 − 8 = 2

Q3. σ_X=2, σ_Y=5, Cov=4 — correlation?

ρ = 4 / (2·5) = 0.4

Q4. VarX=3, VarY=5, independent — Var(X+Y)?

3 + 5 + 0 = 8

6 Main formulas

Covariance — do $X$ and $Y$ move together?

$$ \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]\,E[Y] $$

Correlation — covariance rescaled to $[-1,1]$:

$$ \rho_{XY}=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\,\sigma_Y},\qquad -1\le\rho\le 1 $$

Variance of a sum — an extra term appears when they are correlated:

$$ \operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(Y)+2\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y) $$

Regression line $\hat y=a+bx$ (least squares):

$$ b=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\operatorname{Var}(X)}=r\,\frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X},\qquad a=\bar y-b\,\bar x,\qquad b_{yx}\,b_{xy}=r^2 $$

Spearman's rank correlation (ranked / monotonic data):

$$ \rho_s=1-\frac{6\sum d^2}{n(n^2-1)},\qquad d=\text{rank}(X)-\text{rank}(Y) $$
7 Step-by-step: covariance
  1. Find $E[X]$ and $E[Y]$ from the marginals.
  2. Find $E[XY]=\sum_{x,y} x\,y\,p(x,y)$.
  3. $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]$.
  4. Divide by $\sigma_X\sigma_Y$ for correlation $\rho$.
8 Worked example
✦ Covariance sign

If $E[X]=2$, $E[Y]=3$, and $E[XY]=8$, then $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=8-2\cdot3=2\gt 0$ → they tend to rise together.

If instead $E[XY]=6=E[X]E[Y]$, covariance is $0$ (uncorrelated).

9 GATE cheat sheet
⚡ High-value facts
  • $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]$; $\operatorname{Cov}(X,X)=\operatorname{Var}(X)$.
  • Independent ⇒ $\operatorname{Cov}=0$ (and $\rho=0$). The converse is not guaranteed.
  • $\rho\in[-1,1]$; $\rho=\pm1$ means a perfect straight-line relationship.
  • $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}X+\operatorname{Var}Y+2\operatorname{Cov}$; independence drops the last term.
  • $\operatorname{Cov}(aX,bY)=ab\,\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$.
  • Marginalize by summing out the other variable: $P(X{=}x)=\sum_y P(x,y)$ (or $\int f(x,y)\,dy$).
  • Independent ⇔ the joint factorizes: $p(x,y)=p(x)\,p(y)$.
  • Regression slope $b=\operatorname{Cov}/\operatorname{Var}(X)=r\,\sigma_Y/\sigma_X$; line passes through $(\bar x,\bar y)$.
  • Two regression lines: $b_{yx}\,b_{xy}=r^2$, so $|r|=\sqrt{b_{yx}b_{xy}}$ (they coincide only when $|r|=1$).
  • Spearman $\rho_s=1-6\sum d^2/[n(n^2-1)]$ for ranks — captures any monotonic link.
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "Zero correlation ⇒ independent." Reality: independence ⇒ $\rho=0$, but $\rho=0$ can still hide a nonlinear relationship.

Trap: "Correlation can be 2." Reality: $\rho$ is always in $[-1,1]$.

Trap: "$\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}X+\operatorname{Var}Y$ always." Reality: only if uncorrelated.

Trap: "Marginal probability = conditional probability." Reality: the marginal sums out the other variable; the conditional fixes it.

Trap: "The regression of Y on X is the same line as X on Y." Reality: two different lines ($b_{yx}\ne1/b_{xy}$ unless $|r|=1$); both pass through $(\bar x,\bar y)$.

Trap: "Use Pearson's $r$ on ranked data." Reality: use Spearman's $\rho_s$ for ranks / monotonic relationships.

11 Connections with other topics
  • Random variables: covariance fixes the $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)$ formula for dependent variables.
  • Conditional probability: independence of variables generalizes event independence.
  • Linear algebra / PCA: the covariance matrix's eigenvectors are the principal components.
12 Practice problems

Q1. $E[XY]=10,\ E[X]=2,\ E[Y]=4$. Find $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$.

$10-2\cdot4=2$.

Q2. $X,Y$ independent, $\operatorname{Var}X=3,\ \operatorname{Var}Y=5$. Find $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)$.

$3+5+0=8$ (covariance is 0).

Q2b. Two fair dice. Find the marginal $P(X=3)$.

$\sum_{y=1}^{6}P(3,y)=6\times\tfrac1{36}=\tfrac16$.

Q3. Can correlation be $1.5$?

No — $\rho$ must lie in $[-1,1]$.

Q4. $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=4$, $\operatorname{Var}(X)=8$, $\bar x=10$, $\bar y=25$. Regression line of Y on X?

$b=4/8=0.5$, $a=25-0.5(10)=20$ → $\hat y=20+0.5x$.

Q5. Ranks give $\sum d^2=4$ for $n=5$. Spearman's $\rho_s$?

$1-\dfrac{6(4)}{5(24)}=1-0.2=0.8$.

Quick MCQ

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, then $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$ is…
Independence forces covariance to $0$, not $1$.
✅ Correct — independence ⇒ $E[XY]=E[X]E[Y]$ ⇒ covariance $0$.
That's $\operatorname{Cov}(X,X)$, not for independent $X,Y$.
It's well-defined and equals $0$.
