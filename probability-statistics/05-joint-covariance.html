Joint Distributions
& Covariance

When two random variables move together, how do we describe the pair? Joint distributions, plus covariance and correlation that measure how they relate.

2 Why this topic exists

Real data has many variables at once (height & weight, ad spend & sales). A joint distribution describes them together, and covariance / correlation tell us whether they rise and fall together — the foundation of regression, PCA, and feature relationships in ML.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

A joint distribution is a scatter cloud of $(X,Y)$ points. Marginals are the shadows cast on each axis. Positive covariance = the cloud tilts up-right (big $X$ with big $Y$); negative = tilts down-right; zero = a round blob with no tilt.

4 Basic definitions
  • Joint distribution: $p(x,y)=P(X=x,Y=y)$ (discrete) or $f(x,y)$ (continuous).
  • Marginal: the distribution of one variable alone, by summing/integrating out the other.
  • Independence: $X,Y$ independent if $p(x,y)=p(x)\,p(y)$ for all $x,y$.
  • Covariance: signed measure of joint variation. Correlation $\rho$: covariance rescaled to $[-1,1]$.

6 Main formulas
$$ \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]\,E[Y] $$
$$ \rho_{XY}=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\,\sigma_Y},\qquad -1\le\rho\le 1 $$
$$ \operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(Y)+2\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y) $$
7 Step-by-step: covariance
  1. Find $E[X]$ and $E[Y]$ from the marginals.
  2. Find $E[XY]=\sum_{x,y} x\,y\,p(x,y)$.
  3. $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]$.
  4. Divide by $\sigma_X\sigma_Y$ for correlation $\rho$.
8 Worked example
✦ Covariance sign

If $E[X]=2$, $E[Y]=3$, and $E[XY]=8$, then $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=8-2\cdot3=2\gt 0$ → they tend to rise together.

If instead $E[XY]=6=E[X]E[Y]$, covariance is $0$ (uncorrelated).

9 GATE cheat sheet
⚡ High-value facts
  • $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]$; $\operatorname{Cov}(X,X)=\operatorname{Var}(X)$.
  • Independent ⇒ $\operatorname{Cov}=0$ (and $\rho=0$). The converse is not guaranteed.
  • $\rho\in[-1,1]$; $\rho=\pm1$ means a perfect straight-line relationship.
  • $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}X+\operatorname{Var}Y+2\operatorname{Cov}$; independence drops the last term.
  • $\operatorname{Cov}(aX,bY)=ab\,\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$.
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "Zero correlation ⇒ independent." Reality: independence ⇒ $\rho=0$, but $\rho=0$ can still hide a nonlinear relationship.

Trap: "Correlation can be 2." Reality: $\rho$ is always in $[-1,1]$.

Trap: "$\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}X+\operatorname{Var}Y$ always." Reality: only if uncorrelated.

11 Connections with other topics
  • Random variables: covariance fixes the $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)$ formula for dependent variables.
  • Conditional probability: independence of variables generalizes event independence.
  • Linear algebra / PCA: the covariance matrix's eigenvectors are the principal components.
12 Practice problems

Q1. $E[XY]=10,\ E[X]=2,\ E[Y]=4$. Find $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$.

$10-2\cdot4=2$.

Q2. $X,Y$ independent, $\operatorname{Var}X=3,\ \operatorname{Var}Y=5$. Find $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)$.

$3+5+0=8$ (covariance is 0).

Q3. Can correlation be $1.5$?

No — $\rho$ must lie in $[-1,1]$.

Quick MCQ

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, then $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$ is…
Independence forces covariance to $0$, not $1$.
✅ Correct — independence ⇒ $E[XY]=E[X]E[Y]$ ⇒ covariance $0$.
That's $\operatorname{Cov}(X,X)$, not for independent $X,Y$.
It's well-defined and equals $0$.
13 Final memory box
🧠 Remember
  • Joint $p(x,y)$; marginals by summing out the other variable.
  • $\operatorname{Cov}=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]$; $\rho=\operatorname{Cov}/(\sigma_X\sigma_Y)\in[-1,1]$.
  • Independent ⇒ Cov $=0$; the reverse isn't guaranteed.
  • $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}X+\operatorname{Var}Y+2\operatorname{Cov}$.
