0 The complete concept map

The official syllabus moves from counting and probability to conditional probability, Bayes, random variables, distributions, moments, correlation, covariance, CLT, confidence intervals and statistical tests. The best way to remember everything is to follow that same dependency chain.

$$\text{Counting}\to\text{Events}\to\text{Probability rules}\to\text{Conditional probability}\to\text{Bayes}$$ $$\text{Random variables}\to\text{PMF/PDF/CDF}\to\text{Distributions}\to\text{Expectation and variance}$$ $$\text{Joint distributions}\to\text{Covariance}\to\text{Correlation}\to\text{Descriptive statistics}$$ $$\text{Sampling}\to\text{CLT}\to\text{Confidence intervals}\to\text{Hypothesis tests}$$

Then connect them to machine learning:

$$\text{Probability}\to\text{Bayesian networks}$$ $$\text{Entropy}\to\text{Information gain}\to\text{Decision trees}$$ $$\text{Variance and covariance}\to\text{Regression and }R^2$$
💡 What recent papers actually test

Recent GATE papers repeatedly test recognition rather than direct formula substitution: complements, conditioning reversal, shared random entries, distribution transformations, symmetry, degrees of freedom and hidden dependence.

3 Counting before probability

Probability questions often secretly begin as counting questions.

3.1 Permutation — use when order or position matters (selecting a captain and vice-captain is ordered):

$${}^nP_r=\frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$$

3.2 Combination — use when only the selected group matters (selecting three students for a team):

$$\binom{n}{r}=\frac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!}$$

3.3 Subsets — a set of $n$ elements has $2^n$ subsets; non-empty subsets: $2^n-1$.

🎯 GATE pattern · even product (appeared in the 2026 paper)

A product is odd only when every selected factor is odd. Therefore $P(\text{even product})=1-P(\text{all selected numbers are odd})$.

For $\{1,\dots,2026\}$ there are 1013 odds:

$$P=1-\frac{2^{1013}-1}{2^{2026}-1}=\frac{2^{1013}(2^{1013}-1)}{2^{2026}-1}$$

3.4 Stars and bars — for $x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_k=n$:

$$\text{Non-negative }(x_i\ge0):\ \binom{n+k-1}{k-1}$$ $$\text{Positive }(x_i\ge1):\ \binom{n-1}{k-1}$$
🎯 GATE pattern

For $n_1+n_2+n_3+n_4=20$: total non-negative solutions $\binom{23}{3}$, positive solutions $\binom{19}{3}$, so

$$P=\frac{\binom{19}{3}}{\binom{23}{3}}$$
⚠️ Trap

Do not use the positive formula directly unless every variable must be at least 1.

4 Basic probability rules

4.1 Equally likely outcomes — use only when all elementary outcomes are equally likely:

$$P(A)=\frac{\text{favourable outcomes}}{\text{total outcomes}}$$

4.2 Complement — with its most important application:

$$P(A^c)=1-P(A)$$ $$P(\text{at least one})=1-P(\text{none})$$
🎯 Example · 100 dice, at least one shows 1
$$P(\text{none shows }1)=\left(\tfrac56\right)^{100}$$ $$P(\text{at least one }1)=1-\left(\tfrac56\right)^{100}$$

4.3 Addition rule:

$$P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)$$ $$\begin{aligned}P(A\cup B\cup C)={}&P(A)+P(B)+P(C)\\[2px]&-P(A\cap B)-P(A\cap C)-P(B\cap C)\\[2px]&+P(A\cap B\cap C)\end{aligned}$$
🧠 Memory
  • Add singles, subtract pairs, add triple.

4.4 Subset and intersection identity:

$$A\cap B=A\iff A\subseteq B$$

Therefore $A\cap B\cap C=A$ implies $A\subseteq B$ and $A\subseteq C$. It does not necessarily imply $B\subseteq C$.

5 Mutually exclusive vs independent

One of GATE's favourite conceptual traps.

$$\text{Mutually exclusive: } P(A\cap B)=0$$ $$\text{Independent: } P(A\mid B)=P(A)\ \iff\ P(A\cap B)=P(A)\,P(B)$$

If $P(A)\gt0$ and $P(B)\gt0$ and $A,B$ are mutually exclusive, they cannot be independent: exclusivity gives $P(A\cap B)=0$, but independence would require $P(A)P(B)\gt0$.

🧠 Memory
  • Mutually exclusive: cannot occur together.
  • Independent: can occur together, but do not influence each other.
6 Conditional probability
$$P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}$$ $$P(A\cap B)=P(A\mid B)\,P(B)=P(B\mid A)\,P(A)$$

Interpretation: after learning that $B$ happened, restrict attention to the $B$-region.

⚠️ The reversal trap

Usually $P(A\mid B)\ne P(B\mid A)$. Example: $P(+\mid\text{disease})$ is test sensitivity, but $P(\text{disease}\mid +)$ is the probability a positive person actually has the disease — the second requires Bayes.

7 Law of total probability

When an outcome can happen through several mutually exclusive routes $B_1,\dots,B_k$ that divide the entire population:

$$P(A)=\sum_i P(A\mid B_i)\,P(B_i)$$

Think of a probability tree: route probability $=$ branch 1 $\times$ branch 2; then add all routes reaching the desired outcome.

🎯 Example · two fair coins and one double-headed coin
$$P(H)=\tfrac13\cdot\tfrac12+\tfrac13\cdot\tfrac12+\tfrac13\cdot1=\tfrac23$$
8 Bayes' theorem
$$P(B_i\mid A)=\frac{P(A\mid B_i)\,P(B_i)}{\sum_j P(A\mid B_j)\,P(B_j)}$$ $$\text{Posterior}=\frac{\text{Likelihood}\times\text{Prior}}{\text{Evidence}}$$
🔍 Recognition rule

Given $P(\text{result}\mid\text{cause})$, asked $P(\text{cause}\mid\text{result})$ → use Bayes.

🎯 Disease-test pattern

Suppose $P(+\mid D)=0.8$, $P(+\mid D^c)=0.1$, $P(D)=0.3$. First the evidence, then the posterior:

$$P(+)=0.8(0.3)+0.1(0.7)=0.31$$ $$P(D\mid +)=\frac{0.8(0.3)}{0.31}\approx0.77$$
⚠️ GATE traps
  • Using $0.8$ directly as the answer.
  • Forgetting false positives.
  • Forgetting the base rate $P(D)$.
  • Reversing $P(+\mid D)$ and $P(D\mid +)$.

Recent papers have used both medical-test and box-selection versions of Bayes.

9 Conditional independence & Bayesian networks

9.1 Conditional independence — $X\perp Y\mid Z$ means:

$$P(X,Y\mid Z)=P(X\mid Z)\,P(Y\mid Z)$$

9.2 Bayesian-network factorization — for every node, condition only on its parents:

$$P(X_1,\dots,X_n)=\prod_i P(X_i\mid\text{Parents}(X_i))$$
🎯 Example · $U\to V$, $U\to W$, $V\to Z$, $W\to Z$
$$P(U,V,W,Z)=P(U)\,P(V\mid U)\,P(W\mid U)\,P(Z\mid V,W)$$ $$P(U{=}1,V{=}1,W{=}1,Z{=}1)=0.5(0.5)(1)(0.5)=0.125$$
⚠️ Collider trap

For $V\to X\leftarrow W$: $V$ and $W$ may initially be independent, but conditioning on $X$ can make them dependent. 🧠 Conditioning on a common effect can open a dependency path.

10 Random variables

A random variable assigns a numerical value to each random outcome.

  • Discrete: countable values $0,1,2,\dots$ — number of heads, defects, wins.
  • Continuous: any value in an interval — time, height, temperature.

This distinction controls the mathematics:

$$\text{Discrete}\Rightarrow\text{sum}$$ $$\text{Continuous}\Rightarrow\text{integrate}$$
11 PMF, PDF & CDF

11.1 PMF (discrete): $p(x)=P(X{=}x)$; valid if $p(x)\ge0$ and $\sum_x p(x)=1$.

11.2 PDF (continuous) — probability is area, and the PDF height is not itself a probability:

$$P(a\le X\le b)=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$ $$P(X{=}a)=0\ \text{ for continuous }X$$

Valid if $f(x)\ge0$ and $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(x)\,dx=1$.

11.3 CDF (every random variable): $F(x)=P(X\le x)$ with properties: $0\le F(x)\le1$; non-decreasing; right-continuous; $F(-\infty)=0$, $F(\infty)=1$.

$$\text{Continuous: } P(a\le X\le b)=F(b)-F(a)$$ $$\text{Integer-valued discrete: } P(a\le X\le b)=F(b)-F(a-1)$$
🎯 From the 2026 paper

Non-decreasing behaviour, jumps and right-continuity of a discrete CDF were tested directly.

12 PDF/CDF exam tricks

12.1 Unknown support or constant — a valid PDF has total area 1. If $f(x)=\tfrac{x}{2}$ on $0\le x\le a$:

$$\int_0^a\frac{x}{2}\,dx=1\ \Rightarrow\ \frac{a^2}{4}=1\ \Rightarrow\ a=2$$

12.2 Median from a CDF — solve $F(m)=0.5$. If $F(x)=\dfrac{x-t}{4-t}$ and the median is 3:

$$\frac{3-t}{4-t}=\frac12\ \Rightarrow\ t=2$$

12.3 Squared-variable inequalities:

$$X^2\le a^2\ \iff\ -a\le X\le a$$ $$P(X^2\le a^2)=F(a)-F(-a)$$
⚠️ Common error

Calculating only $P(X\le a)$ and forgetting the lower bound $-a$.

14 Bernoulli distribution
$$P(X{=}1)=p,\quad P(X{=}0)=1-p$$ $$E[X]=p\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=p(1-p)$$

An indicator variable is a Bernoulli variable: $I_A=1$ if $A$ occurs, else $0$. Then:

$$E[I_A]=P(A)$$

This makes expectation questions much easier.

15 Binomial distribution
🧠 The BINS test
  • Binary result · Independent trials · fixed Number of trials · Same success probability.
$$P(X{=}k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$$ $$E[X]=np\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=np(1-p)$$
🎯 Equal heads and tails

In $2n$ fair tosses, equal heads and tails means exactly $n$ heads:

$$P(X{=}n)=\binom{2n}{n}\left(\tfrac12\right)^{2n}=\frac{\binom{2n}{n}}{2^{2n}}$$
⚠️ Success vs failure count

If wins have probability $p$: $X=\text{wins}\sim\text{Bin}(n,p)$ but $Y=\text{non-wins}\sim\text{Bin}(n,1-p)$. Do not use the same $p$ automatically for both variables.

16 Poisson distribution
$$P(X{=}k)=\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}$$ $$E[X]=\operatorname{Var}(X)=\lambda$$

Recognition clues: number of arrivals, calls per hour, defects per unit area, accidents per day.

17 Uniform distribution
$$f(x)=\frac{1}{b-a}\ \ (a\le x\le b)$$ $$E[X]=\frac{a+b}{2}\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=\frac{(b-a)^2}{12}$$ $$P(c\le X\le d)=\frac{d-c}{b-a}\ \text{ when }[c,d]\subseteq[a,b]$$

For two independent uniform variables, draw the joint rectangle and calculate favourable area ÷ total area.

18 Exponential distribution
$$f(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}\ (x\ge0)\qquad F(x)=1-e^{-\lambda x}$$ $$P(X\gt x)=e^{-\lambda x}$$ $$E[X]=\frac{1}{\lambda}\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=\frac{1}{\lambda^2}$$

Memoryless property:

$$P(X\gt s+t\mid X\gt s)=P(X\gt t)$$
🎯 Example

$P(X\gt5)=0.35$  ⇒  $P(X\gt10\mid X\gt5)=P(X\gt5)=0.35$.

⚠️ Parameterization trap

Some questions call $\lambda$ the rate (so $E[X]=1/\lambda$); others use $\lambda$ to denote the mean. Always read the exact definition in the question — the 2026 paper deliberately used mean notation in its exponential-memorylessness question.

19 Normal distribution
$$X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\qquad Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}$$
  • $Z=0$: exactly at the mean · $Z\gt0$: above · $Z\lt0$: below · $|Z|$: distance in standard deviations.

Transformation: $X=aZ+b$ with $Z\sim N(0,1)$ gives:

$$E[X]=b\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=a^2$$
⚠️ Sign trap

The sign of $a$ cannot be found from variance alone — both $a$ and $-a$ have the same square. Another expectation or covariance condition determines the sign.

20 Student's t-distribution
  • Symmetric around zero, heavier-tailed than the standard normal, controlled by degrees of freedom.
  • As df increase: $t_\nu\to N(0,1)$.
  • Use t for mean inference when the population standard deviation is unknown.
⚠️ Normal vs t trap

Both have $F(0)=0.5$. The t has less density near the centre and more probability in the tails. When comparing CDFs at crossing points, think in terms of accumulated area, not only PDF height.

21 Chi-square distribution
$$Z_1^2+\cdots+Z_k^2\sim\chi^2_k$$ $$E[\chi^2_k]=k\qquad \operatorname{Var}(\chi^2_k)=2k$$

Important cases: $Z^2\sim\chi^2_1$; $Z_1^2+Z_2^2\sim\chi^2_2$; and a $\chi^2_2$ is exponential with mean 2.

Sample-variance result for independent standard normals:

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i^2\sim\chi^2_n$$ $$\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i-\bar X)^2\sim\chi^2_{n-1}$$
🤔 Why $n-1$?

The deviations satisfy $\sum_i(X_i-\bar X)=0$, so only $n-1$ deviations can vary freely. Recent GATE questions explicitly tested these degree-of-freedom distinctions.

22 Expected value
$$\text{Discrete: } E[X]=\sum_x x\,P(X{=}x)$$ $$\text{Continuous: } E[X]=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}x f(x)\,dx$$ $$\text{Any function: } E[g(X)]=\sum_x g(x)P(X{=}x)\ \text{ or }\ \int g(x)f(x)\,dx$$

Linearity — independence is not required:

$$E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b$$ $$E[X+Y]=E[X]+E[Y]$$
23 Conditional expectation

$E[X\mid Y]$ is the expected value of $X$ after $Y$ is known.

Tower property — average inside each group, then average the group averages:

$$E\big[E[X\mid Y]\big]=E[X]$$

Law of total variance — total variation = average within-group variation + between-group variation:

$$\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[\operatorname{Var}(X\mid Y)]+\operatorname{Var}(E[X\mid Y])$$

This links conditional expectation, variance decomposition and regression.

24 Variance
$$\operatorname{Var}(X)=E\big[(X-E[X])^2\big]=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2$$ $$SD(X)=\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(X)}$$

Transformations — the constant $b$ does not affect spread:

$$\operatorname{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\operatorname{Var}(X)$$

Variance of a sum:

$$\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(Y)+2\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$$ $$\text{Independent: } \operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(Y)$$
🎯 Sign-flip trick (key to a 2026 variance question)

If a multiplier is always $+1$ or $-1$, squaring removes it. For Bernoulli $X$: $2X-1\in\{-1,+1\}$, so $(2X-1)^2=1$.

25 Useful variance identity
$$\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}(x_i-x_j)^2=2n\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar x)^2$$

Use this when GATE gives all pairwise squared distances but asks for variance around the mean. Remember:

$$s^2=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2$$
26 Expected waiting time

When asked "expected number of trials until a pattern appears", create states describing progress. For two consecutive successes with success probability $p$ — state 0: no current success; state 1: one success already:

$$E_0=1+pE_1+(1-p)E_0$$ $$E_1=1+p(0)+(1-p)E_0$$

Solve the simultaneous equations. For two consecutive even die results, $p=\tfrac12$ and the answer is 6.

27 Joint, marginal & conditional distributions

Joint: $P(X{=}x,Y{=}y)$ describes the two variables together.

Marginal — to keep $X$ and ignore $Y$:

$$P(X{=}x)=\sum_y P(X{=}x,Y{=}y)$$ $$f_X(x)=\int f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dy$$

Conditional:

$$P(X{=}x\mid Y{=}y)=\frac{P(X{=}x,Y{=}y)}{P(Y{=}y)}$$

Independence — for every possible pair:

$$P(X{=}x,Y{=}y)=P(X{=}x)\,P(Y{=}y)$$
28 Covariance
$$\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E\big[(X-E[X])(Y-E[Y])\big]$$ $$\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]$$
  • Positive: move together · Negative: move oppositely · Zero: no linear co-movement.
⚠️ One-way implication

Independence implies $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=0$. But $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=0\ \not\Rightarrow\ X,Y$ independent.

29 Pearson correlation
$$\rho_{XY}=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\,\sigma_Y}$$ $$r=\frac{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sqrt{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2}\ \sqrt{\sum_i(y_i-\bar y)^2}}$$ $$-1\le r\le 1$$
  • $r\approx1$: strong positive linear association · $r\approx-1$: strong negative · $r\approx0$: little linear association — it does not mean "no relationship".
🎯 Symmetry trick (appeared in GATE 2026)

If $X$ is symmetric around zero, $E[X]=0$ and every odd moment is zero: $E[X^3]=E[X^5]=\cdots=0$. If $E[Y\mid X]=X^2$, then $E[XY]=E[X\,E(Y\mid X)]=E[X^3]=0$ — so correlation can be zero even though $Y$ strongly depends on $X$.

30 Spearman correlation

Spearman correlation is Pearson correlation applied to ranks. Without tied ranks:

$$r_s=1-\frac{6\sum d_i^2}{n(n^2-1)}$$

Use it for ordinal data, rankings, monotonic-but-curved relationships, and data strongly affected by outliers.

PearsonSpearman

31 Concordant & discordant pairs

Two pairs $(x_1,y_1),(x_2,y_2)$ are concordant if both variables move in the same direction, discordant if they move oppositely:

$$\text{Concordant: } (x_1-x_2)(y_1-y_2)\gt0$$ $$\text{Discordant: } (x_1-x_2)(y_1-y_2)\lt0$$
🧠 Memory
  • Concordant = coordinates agree · Discordant = coordinates disagree.
32 Covariance matrix
$$\Sigma\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}\qquad \Sigma_{ij}=\operatorname{Cov}(X_i,X_j)\qquad \Sigma_{ii}=\operatorname{Var}(X_i)$$

The matrix is symmetric: $\Sigma_{ij}=\Sigma_{ji}$.

33 Descriptive statistics
  • Mean $\bar x=\tfrac1n\sum_i x_i$ — sensitive to extreme values.
  • Median — middle value after sorting; more resistant to outliers.
  • Mode — most frequent value.

Updating a mean after adding a value $a$ (old total is $n\bar x$):

$$\bar x_{\text{new}}=\frac{n\bar x+a}{n+1}$$ $$\text{Example: } \frac{50(40)+142}{51}=42$$
34 Population vs sample variance
$$\sigma^2=\frac{1}{N}\sum_i(x_i-\mu)^2$$ $$s^2=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2$$

The sample formula uses $n-1$ because the sample mean has already been estimated from the same observations. Shortcut: $\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2$.

35 z-score normalization
$$z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}$$ $$\text{Example: } x=106000,\ \mu=96000,\ \sigma=21000\ \Rightarrow\ z=\frac{10000}{21000}\approx0.476$$
⚠️ Trap

Minimum and maximum values may be included as distractors — the z-score needs only $x,\mu,\sigma$.

36 Skewness

The mean is pulled toward the long tail.

$$\text{Right-skewed: Mode}\lt\text{Median}\lt\text{Mean}$$ $$\text{Left-skewed: Mean}\lt\text{Median}\lt\text{Mode}$$
🧠 Memory
  • The mean follows the tail.
37 Q–Q plots

A Q–Q plot compares theoretical quantiles with observed sample quantiles.

For an exponential distribution: quantiles are non-negative, so points lie in the first quadrant; compared with the same fully specified distribution they follow approximately $y=x$.

38 Sampling distribution & standard error

For independent observations with $E[X_i]=\mu$, $\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=\sigma^2$ and sample mean $\bar X=\tfrac1n\sum_i X_i$:

$$E[\bar X]=\mu$$ $$\operatorname{Var}(\bar X)=\frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$ $$SE(\bar X)=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}$$
  • Standard deviation: spread of individual observations.
  • Standard error: spread of an estimator across samples.
  • If sample size is multiplied by 4: $SE_{\text{new}}=\dfrac{SE_{\text{old}}}{2}$.
39 Central Limit Theorem
$$\bar X\approx N\!\left(\mu,\ \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)$$ $$S_n=\sum_i X_i:\quad E[S_n]=n\mu,\quad \operatorname{Var}(S_n)=n\sigma^2$$ $$Z=\frac{S_n-n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt n}$$

Binomial CLT — for $Y\sim\text{Bin}(n,p)$: $\mu=np$, $\sigma=\sqrt{np(1-p)}$, then:

$$P(a\le Y\le b)\approx\Phi\!\left(\frac{b-np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right)-\Phi\!\left(\frac{a-np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right)$$
💡 Continuity correction

For a more accurate numerical approximation, $P(a\le Y\le b)$ becomes approximately $P(a-0.5\le N\le b+0.5)$. If the GATE options clearly use integer endpoints without correction, follow the intended option structure.

40 Sample proportion
$$\hat p=\frac1n\sum_i X_i\ \text{ for Bernoulli observations}$$ $$E[\hat p]=p\qquad \operatorname{Var}(\hat p)=\frac{p(1-p)}{n}\qquad SE(\hat p)=\sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}$$

Larger sample size means a more stable estimate.

41 Confidence intervals
$$\sigma\text{ known: }\ \bar x\pm z_{\alpha/2}\,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}$$ $$\sigma\text{ unknown: }\ \bar x\pm t_{\alpha/2,\,n-1}\,\frac{s}{\sqrt n}$$

For 95% confidence: $z_{\alpha/2}\approx1.96$.

Connection with testing: a two-sided $H_0:\mu=\mu_0$ is rejected at level $\alpha$ exactly when $\mu_0$ lies outside the corresponding $100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence interval.

42 Hypothesis-testing workflow
  • Step 1 — write hypotheses: $H_0$ = no change / no difference / current claim; $H_1$ = effect / difference.
  • Step 2 — choose significance: $\alpha$, commonly $0.05$.
  • Step 3 — calculate the test statistic: how far the sample result lies from $H_0$, in standard-error units.
  • Step 4 — use the p-value.
  • Step 5 — state the conclusion in context. Do not stop with only "reject".
$$p\lt\alpha\ \Rightarrow\ \text{reject }H_0$$ $$p\ge\alpha\ \Rightarrow\ \text{fail to reject }H_0$$

Do not normally say "accept $H_0$".

43 One-tailed vs two-tailed
  • $H_1:\mu\ne\mu_0$ — two-tailed.
  • $H_1:\mu\gt\mu_0$ — right-tailed.
  • $H_1:\mu\lt\mu_0$ — left-tailed.
⚠️ Trap

Never change a two-sided test into a one-sided test after seeing the sample result.

46 Chi-square tests
$$\chi^2=\sum_i\frac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i}$$ $$E_{ij}=\frac{(\text{row total})(\text{column total})}{\text{grand total}}$$ $$df=(r-1)(c-1)$$

$O_i$ = observed count, $E_i$ = expected count. A large $\chi^2$ means observed counts are far from expected counts.

47 F-test
$$F=\frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}\qquad H_0:\sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2$$ $$df_1=n_1-1,\qquad df_2=n_2-1$$

In standard exam calculations, the larger sample variance is often placed in the numerator so $F\ge1$.

48 The shared-random-variable trap

When two requested events overlap, do not multiply their probabilities as though they were independent. Example: "sum of row 2 equals 3" and "sum of column 3 equals 3" — the entry at $(2,3)$ belongs to both. Split into cases:

$$P=P(\text{shared}{=}1)\,P(\text{requirements}\mid 1)+P(\text{shared}{=}0)\,P(\text{requirements}\mid 0)$$ $$\text{Bernoulli(0.5) entries: } P=\tfrac12\left(\tfrac{\binom42}{2^4}\right)^{\!2}+\tfrac12\left(\tfrac{\binom43}{2^4}\right)^{\!2}\approx0.10$$

This overlap-conditioning pattern appeared in GATE 2026.

49 Entropy & information gain

These belong primarily to decision trees but are probability-based.

$$H(C)=-\sum_i p_i\log_2 p_i$$ $$H(C\mid A)=\sum_v\frac{|C_v|}{|C|}H(C_v)$$ $$IG(C,A)=H(C)-H(C\mid A)$$
  • Pure node: $H=0$ · Balanced binary classes: maximum entropy $=1$.
  • 🧠 Uncertainty before split minus uncertainty after split.
🎯 Cricket-table pattern

Overall Green 4, Blue 6: $H(C)=-0.4\log_2 0.4-0.6\log_2 0.6\approx0.971$. Spin-friendly (3 G, 2 B): $H(S)\approx0.971$. Pace-friendly (1 G, 4 B): $H(F)\approx0.722$.

$$H(C\mid \text{Pitch})=\tfrac{5}{10}(0.971)+\tfrac{5}{10}(0.722)\approx0.846$$ $$IG\approx0.971-0.846=0.12$$
50 Linear regression essentials
$$y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1x_i+\epsilon_i$$ $$\hat y_i=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1x_i\qquad e_i=y_i-\hat y_i$$ $$\text{OLS minimizes } SSE=\sum_i e_i^2$$

Slope and intercept:

$$\hat\beta_1=\frac{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum_i(x_i-\bar x)^2}$$ $$\hat\beta_0=\bar y-\hat\beta_1\bar x$$

With an intercept: $\sum_i e_i=0$ and the fitted line passes through $(\bar x,\bar y)$.

51 OLS assumptions
AssumptionMeaning
LinearityMean response is linear in parameters

Accurate predictorsClassical errors-in-variables assumption
No perfect multicollinearityPredictors are not exact linear combinations

Normal errors are needed for exact small-sample inferential results, not merely for computing the OLS coefficients.

52 $R^2$
$$R^2=1-\frac{SSE}{SST}\qquad SST=\sum_i(y_i-\bar y)^2$$ $$\text{Simple linear regression with intercept: } R^2=r^2$$

$R^2=0.90$ means the model explains 90% of the observed response variation in the fitted sample. Higher $R^2$ means points tend to lie closer to the fitted line.

⚠️ Trap

Ordinary in-sample OLS with an intercept gives $0\le R^2\le1$ — but test-set $R^2$ or models without an intercept can produce negative values.

53 Linear decision boundaries
$$w_1x+w_2y+b=0\qquad\text{equivalently } y=a_0+a_1x$$

All of these are straight-line boundaries: $y=a_0+a_1x$, $y=a_0$, $y=x$, $y=a_1x$.

Feature transformation — original circular boundary $x^2+y^2=c^2$; define $z=x^2+y^2$; then the boundary becomes:

$$z=c^2$$

which is a threshold in one-dimensional transformed space.

54 Correlation vs causation
$$\text{Correlation does not by itself prove causation}$$

Correlation may arise from: direct causation, reverse causation, a hidden confounder, selection bias, or coincidence. A causal conclusion requires additional evidence beyond the correlation coefficient.

56 Sixty-second exam decision tree
  • Counting outcomes? → permutation, combination, subsets or stars and bars.
  • "At least one"? → complement.
  • "Given"? → conditional probability.
  • Cause and result reversed? → Bayes.
  • Successes in fixed independent trials? → binomial.
  • Waiting time? → exponential; check memorylessness.
  • Large sum or sample mean? → CLT and standardize.
  • Given a PDF? → check $\int f(x)\,dx=1$.
  • Given a CDF? → $F(\text{upper})-F(\text{lower})$.
  • About spread? → variance or standard deviation.
  • Two variables moving together? → covariance / Pearson / Spearman.
  • Sample claim vs population value? → select z, t, chi-square or F.
  • Decision-tree table? → entropy before and after the split.
  • Bayesian network? → multiply every node's probability conditioned on its parents.

✦ Final recall story

Think of the entire subject as one journey:

First count possible worlds. Assign probabilities to events in those worlds. When information arrives, condition the probabilities. When the direction is reversed, use Bayes. Convert outcomes into random variables. Describe discrete variables using PMFs, continuous variables using PDFs, and both using CDFs. Summarize them using expectation and variance. Study pairs using joint distributions, covariance and correlation. Take samples, use the CLT to understand sample means, and use hypothesis tests to judge claims. Finally, use entropy for decision trees and conditional factorization for Bayesian networks.

That single sequence connects nearly every probability-and-statistics pattern seen in the recent GATE DA papers.
