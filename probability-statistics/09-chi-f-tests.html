Chi-square & F-test

Two more tests in the same framework: chi-square for counts & categories, and the F-test for comparing variances.

2 Why this topic exists

z and t handle means. But often we have category counts ("does diet relate to disease?") or need to compare spreads ("do two machines vary equally?"). Chi-square and F tests answer exactly those — and underpin ANOVA and feature-selection in ML.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

Chi-square compares the counts you observed with the counts you'd expect if nothing special were happening — big mismatch ⇒ big statistic ⇒ reject. F is a ratio of two variances: far from 1 means the spreads really differ.

4 Basic definitions

Chi-square goodness-of-fit: do observed category counts match a claimed distribution?

Chi-square test of independence: are two categorical variables related? (uses a contingency table).

F-test: compares two variances via their ratio; also the engine of ANOVA (comparing several means).

Degrees of freedom (df): control the shape of the chi-square / F distributions.

6 Main formulas
$$ \chi^2=\sum_i \frac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i} $$
$$ F=\frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}\qquad(\text{larger variance on top}) $$

Goodness-of-fit df $=k-1$ (k categories). Independence df $=(r-1)(c-1)$ for an $r\times c$ table. F has two df: $(n_1-1,\ n_2-1)$.

7 Step-by-step: chi-square
  1. State $H_0$ (fits the claimed distribution / variables independent).
  2. Compute expected counts $E_i$ under $H_0$.
  3. $\chi^2=\sum (O_i-E_i)^2/E_i$.
  4. Find df and compare to the critical $\chi^2$ (or use the p-value); reject if $\chi^2$ is too large.
8 Worked example
✦ Is a die fair?

60 rolls, expected $E_i=10$ each. Observed: $8,12,9,11,13,7$.

$\chi^2=\tfrac{(8-10)^2+(12-10)^2+(9-10)^2+(11-10)^2+(13-10)^2+(7-10)^2}{10}=\tfrac{4+4+1+1+9+9}{10}=2.8$.

df $=6-1=5$; critical $\chi^2_{0.05}=11.07$. Since $2.8\lt11.07$, the die looks fair (fail to reject).

9 GATE shortcuts
⚡ High-value facts
  • $\chi^2=\sum (O-E)^2/E$; it's always $\ge0$, right-skewed.
  • Goodness-of-fit df $=k-1$; independence df $=(r-1)(c-1)$.
  • F is a variance ratio with two df; put the larger variance on top so $F\ge1$.
  • Big $\chi^2$ or F far from 1 ⇒ reject $H_0$.
  • Chi-square & F are right-tailed tests (reject in the upper tail).
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "Chi-square uses proportions." Reality: it uses raw counts $O_i,E_i$.

Trap: "Independence df $=k-1$." Reality: it's $(r-1)(c-1)$ for a table.

Trap: "F can be negative." Reality: it's a ratio of variances, always $\ge0$.

12 Practice problems

Q1. Goodness-of-fit with 4 categories. Degrees of freedom?

$k-1=3$.

Q2. A $3\times4$ contingency table. df for the independence test?

$(3-1)(4-1)=6$.

Q3. $s_1^2=20,\ s_2^2=5$. Compute the F-statistic.

$F=20/5=4$ (larger variance on top).

13 Final memory box
🧠 Remember
  • $\chi^2=\sum (O-E)^2/E$ from counts; right-tailed.
  • df: goodness-of-fit $k-1$; independence $(r-1)(c-1)$.
  • $F=s_1^2/s_2^2$ (larger on top); compares variances / ANOVA.
