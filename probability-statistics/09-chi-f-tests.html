Chi-square & F-test

Two more tests in the same framework: chi-square for counts & categories, and the F-test for comparing variances.

2 Why this topic exists

z and t handle means. But often we have category counts ("does diet relate to disease?") or need to compare spreads ("do two machines vary equally?"). Chi-square and F tests answer exactly those — and underpin ANOVA and feature-selection in ML.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

Chi-square compares the counts you observed with the counts you'd expect if nothing special were happening — big mismatch ⇒ big statistic ⇒ reject. F is a ratio of two variances: far from 1 means the spreads really differ.

4 Basic definitions
  • Observed vs expected: counts you saw ($O$) vs counts $H_0$ predicts ($E$).
  • Chi-square goodness-of-fit: do observed category counts match a claimed distribution?
  • Contingency table: a grid of counts cross-classifying two categorical variables.
  • Chi-square test of independence: are two categorical variables related? (uses the contingency table).
  • F-test: compares two variances through their ratio.
  • ANOVA: uses the F-test to compare several group means at once.
  • Degrees of freedom (df): control the shape of the chi-square and F distributions.
📖 Study in Depth — chi-square & F distributions and their tests, from zero
1. What the chi-square distribution is

Add up squared independent standard normals and you get a chi-square variable:

χ² = Z₁² + Z₂² + … + Z_k²        (each Zᵢ ~ N(0,1), independent)

Because it is a sum of squares it is always ≥ 0. Written χ² ~ χ²(k).

2. Degrees of freedom (k)

The parameter k = how many independent squared normals were added.

Z₁² + Z₂² + Z₃²  →  k = 3 degrees of freedom

Degrees of freedom set the entire shape of the curve.

3. Mean & variance 
E(χ²) = k        Var(χ²) = 2k
k = 5 → mean 5, variance 10

Easy memory: the mean is the degrees of freedom; the variance is twice it.

4. Shape of the curve
  • starts at 0, lives only on the right (non-negative)
  • right-skewed for small k
  • becomes more bell-shaped (normal-like) as k grows
5. Goodness-of-fit test

Does observed data match an expected distribution? Compare counts:

χ² = Σ (Oᵢ − Eᵢ)² / Eᵢ          O = observed, E = expected
df = (number of categories) − 1

Big χ² ⇒ observed is far from expected ⇒ reject the assumed model (e.g. "is this die fair?").

6. Test of independence (contingency table)

Are two categorical variables related? Lay counts in an r×c table:

Eᵢⱼ = (row total × column total) / grand total
χ² = Σ (O − E)² / E
df = (r − 1)(c − 1)

Example: is product preference independent of gender? Large χ² ⇒ they are not independent.

7. Goodness-of-fit — worked (is the die fair?) 
60 rolls, observed: 8,10,9,11,12,10 ; expected E = 60/6 = 10 each
χ² = Σ(O−E)²/E = (4+0+1+1+4+0)/10 = 1.0
df = 6 − 1 = 5

χ² = 1.0 is small (well under the critical value) → fail to reject H₀: no evidence the die is unfair.

8. Test of independence — worked (gender × drink) 
Observed      Tea  Coffee  Total        Expected = row×col/grand
Male           20     30     50          Male-Tea  = 50·50/100 = 25
Female         30     20     50          (every cell works out to 25)
Total          50     50    100
χ² = Σ(O−E)²/E = 1+1+1+1 = 4
df = (R−1)(C−1) = (2−1)(2−1) = 1 ;  critical χ²₀.₀₅ = 3.841

4 > 3.841 → reject H₀: gender and drink preference are associated. (Chi-square is right-tailed — large χ² lands in the upper tail.)

9. Where chi-square is used
  • goodness-of-fit (does data follow a claimed distribution?)
  • test of independence (are two categories related?)
  • tests about a variance

Theme: chi-square is the tool for counts and categories.

10. The F-distribution

The F-distribution is the ratio of two independent chi-squares, each divided by its degrees of freedom:

F = (χ²₁ / d₁) / (χ²₂ / d₂)     two df parameters: d₁ (numerator), d₂ (denominator)
  • always ≥ 0 (a ratio of non-negative quantities)
  • right-skewed; shape depends on both df
  • since it's a ratio of variances, it's the tool for comparing two variances
11. The F-test — comparing two variances

Tests whether two populations have the same variance (H₀: σ₁² = σ₂²) using the ratio of sample variances:

F = s₁² / s₂²     (put the LARGER variance on top so F ≥ 1)
df = (n₁ − 1, n₂ − 1) ;  right-tailed test

F far above 1 ⇒ the variances differ ⇒ reject H₀. F near 1 ⇒ variances look equal.

12. F-test — worked 
sample 1: n₁=13, s₁²=45 ; sample 2: n₂=10, s₂²=20
F = 45/20 = 2.25 ,  df = (12, 9)
compare with the critical F₀.₀₅(12,9) ≈ 3.07
2.25 < 3.07 → fail to reject H₀ (no evidence variances differ)
13. ANOVA basics (the F-test for several means)

ANOVA (analysis of variance) uses an F-test to compare 3+ group means at once — by comparing two kinds of variation:

F = MSB / MSW = (between-group variance) / (within-group variance)
  • groups far apart vs tight within ⇒ large F ⇒ at least one mean differs
  • one-way ANOVA H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = … = μₖ

It avoids running many t-tests (which would inflate the error rate).

14. Which test? z · t · χ² · F

Traps: F is never negative; always put the larger variance on top; F & χ² are right-tailed.

15. Common GATE traps
  • χ² can be negative? No — it's a sum of squares, always ≥ 0.
  • df is always n − 1? No — goodness-of-fit uses categories − 1; independence uses (r−1)(c−1).
  • Mean and variance are equal? No — mean k, variance 2k.

17. Memory box & mental model
  • χ² = sum of squared standard normals; always ≥ 0.
  • E = k, Var = 2k; right-skewed, → normal as k grows.
  • Goodness-of-fit df = categories − 1; independence df = (r−1)(c−1).
  • χ² is for counts / categories; big χ² = observed far from expected.
Think: "how far are the observed counts from what we expected?" — squared, scaled by expected.

6 Main formulas
$$ \chi^2=\sum_i \frac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i} $$
$$ F=\frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}\qquad(\text{larger variance on top}) $$

Goodness-of-fit df $=k-1$ (k categories). Independence df $=(r-1)(c-1)$ for an $r\times c$ table. F has two df: $(n_1-1,\ n_2-1)$.

7 Step-by-step: chi-square
  1. State $H_0$ (fits the claimed distribution / variables independent).
  2. Compute expected counts $E_i$ under $H_0$.
  3. $\chi^2=\sum (O_i-E_i)^2/E_i$.
  4. Find df and compare to the critical $\chi^2$ (or use the p-value); reject if $\chi^2$ is too large.
8 Worked example
✦ Is a die fair?

60 rolls, expected $E_i=10$ each. Observed: $8,12,9,11,13,7$.

$\chi^2=\tfrac{(8-10)^2+(12-10)^2+(9-10)^2+(11-10)^2+(13-10)^2+(7-10)^2}{10}=\tfrac{4+4+1+1+9+9}{10}=2.8$.

df $=6-1=5$; critical $\chi^2_{0.05}=11.07$. Since $2.8\lt11.07$, the die looks fair (fail to reject).

9 GATE cheat sheet
⚡ High-value facts
  • $\chi^2=\sum (O-E)^2/E$; it's always $\ge0$, right-skewed.
  • Goodness-of-fit df $=k-1$; independence df $=(r-1)(c-1)$.
  • F is a variance ratio with two df; put the larger variance on top so $F\ge1$.
  • Big $\chi^2$ or F far from 1 ⇒ reject $H_0$.
  • Chi-square & F are right-tailed tests (reject in the upper tail).
  • As a distribution: $\chi^2=\sum Z_i^2$ (k independent standard normals), with mean $k$ and variance $2k$.
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "Chi-square uses proportions." Reality: it uses raw counts $O_i,E_i$.

Trap: "Independence df $=k-1$." Reality: it's $(r-1)(c-1)$ for a table.

Trap: "F can be negative." Reality: it's a ratio of variances, always $\ge0$.
