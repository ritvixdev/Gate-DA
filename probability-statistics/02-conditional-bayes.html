Topic 2 · GATE weight: Very High Core

Conditional Probability
& Bayes

How does knowing one fact change the probability of another? This is the engine behind Bayes' theorem — and almost every inference question in GATE DA.

2 Why this topic exists

Real decisions use partial information: a test came back positive, the first card was red. Conditional probability updates a probability once you learn something, and Bayes' theorem flips a conditional you know into the one you want — the heart of diagnosis, spam filters, and ML classifiers.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

Knowing $B$ happened shrinks the sample space down to just $B$. $P(A\mid B)$ asks: within that smaller world of $B$, what fraction is also $A$?   Bayes = walking the conditional backwards: you know $P(\text{test}\mid \text{disease})$ but want $P(\text{disease}\mid \text{test})$.

🧪 Bayes — the base-rate effect area = probability · orange TP, teal FP
4 Basic definitions
  • Joint probability $P(A\cap B)$: both $A$ and $B$ happen.
  • Conditional probability $P(A\mid B)$: chance of $A$ given $B$ already happened.
  • Independence: knowing one doesn't change the other — $P(A\mid B)=P(A)$, i.e. $P(A\cap B)=P(A)\,P(B)$.
  • Partition: events $B_1,\dots,B_n$ that are disjoint and together cover all of $S$.
  • Prior $P(B)$: belief before evidence. Posterior $P(B\mid A)$: updated belief after evidence.
  • Likelihood $P(A\mid B)$: how well a cause explains the observed evidence.
  • Total probability: combine the chance of $A$ across every part of a partition.
📖 Study in Depth — conditional probability & independence from zero
1. Does A affect B?

The central question of this topic: does one event change the chance of another?

draw 2 cards WITHOUT replacing → the first draw changes the deck → dependent
roll a die, then toss a coin     → the die can't change the coin → independent

The answer decides which multiplication rule you use.

2. Game imagination 
Two separate loot boxes (same drop rate): opening one doesn't change the
   other → INDEPENDENT.
A limited chest of 5 items, no refill: removing one changes what's left
   → DEPENDENT.

Memory: independent = probability stays the same; dependent = probability changes after the first event.

3. Conditional probability P(B|A)

P(B|A) ("probability of B given A") = the chance of B once you know A has happened.

P(B|A) = P(A ∩ B) / P(A)        (for P(A) > 0)

It re-scales probability to the smaller world where A is known true.

die: A = even {2,4,6}, B = ">4" {5,6}
P(B|A) = P({6})/P(A) = (1/6)/(3/6) = 1/3
4. The general multiplication rule

Rearranging the conditional formula gives the AND rule for any two events:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B|A) = P(B) · P(A|B)

"Probability of A and B = probability of A, times probability of B once A has happened."

5. Independent events (via conditioning)

A and B are independent when knowing A tells you nothing about B:

P(B|A) = P(B)   ⟺   P(A|B) = P(A)   ⟺   P(A ∩ B) = P(A)·P(B)

So independence is the special case where the multiplication rule simplifies — the conditional probability equals the plain probability.

6. Dependent events

A and B are dependent when A changes B's chance:

P(B|A) ≠ P(B)   →   use  P(A ∩ B) = P(A)·P(B|A)

You must use the conditional probability, not the plain one.

7. Given = filter the world (worked examples)

"Given A" means: throw away everything outside A, then count inside it.

P(king | face card): only 12 face cards matter, 4 are kings → 4/12 = 1/3
P(heart | red):      only 26 red cards matter, 13 are hearts → 13/26 = 1/2

Both match the formula P(B|A)=P(A∩B)/P(A), but the "shrink the sample space" view is faster.

8. P(B|A) vs P(A|B) — don't swap them

They condition on different events, so they usually differ (different denominators):

P(B|A) = P(A∩B)/P(A)        P(A|B) = P(A∩B)/P(B)
cards: P(ace | spade) = 1/13   but   P(spade | ace) = 1/4   → different!

Trap: "probability of A given B" is not the same as "B given A". (Swapping them wrongly is exactly what Bayes' theorem fixes.)

9. Joint probability

Joint probability is just P(A ∩ B) — the chance both happen together.

joint = AND probability
from joint to conditional:  P(B|A) = P(A∩B)/P(A)
e.g. P(A∩B)=0.2, P(A)=0.5 → P(B|A) = 0.4
10. Chain rule for several events

Extend the multiplication rule step by step:

P(A∩B∩C) = P(A)·P(B|A)·P(C|A∩B)
3 aces, no replacement:
4/52 × 3/51 × 2/50 = 1/13 × 1/17 × 1/25 = 1/5525

Each factor conditions on everything that came before.

11. Law of total probability (the sum rule)

If A₁, A₂, …, Aₖ partition the sample space (mutually exclusive & exhaustive):

P(B) = Σ P(Aᵢ)·P(B|Aᵢ)
     = P(A₁)P(B|A₁) + … + P(Aₖ)P(B|Aₖ)
Pick a bag at random, then a ball:
Bag1 {2R,1B}, Bag2 {1R,3B}, each P=1/2
P(red) = 1/2·(2/3) + 1/2·(1/4) = 1/3 + 1/8 = 11/24

This "split over cases" rule is the engine behind Bayes' theorem.

12. Bayes' theorem — reversing the condition

Total probability finds P(result). Bayes' theorem reverses it to find the cause given the result. For causes A₁,…,Aₙ that partition the sample space:

P(Aᵢ | B) = P(Aᵢ)·P(B|Aᵢ) / P(B)
          = P(Aᵢ)·P(B|Aᵢ) / Σⱼ P(Aⱼ)·P(B|Aⱼ)

The denominator is exactly the law of total probability — the total chance of the evidence over all causes.

13. Bayes example — which box? 
Box1 {3R,2B}, Box2 {1R,4B}, pick a box at random then a ball
P(R) = 1/2·3/5 + 1/2·1/5 = 2/5  (total probability)

P(Box1 | Red) = P(Box1)P(R|Box1)/P(R)
             = (1/2 · 3/5) / (2/5) = (3/10)/(2/5) = 3/4

Seeing a red ball makes Box 1 (more reds) the likelier source.

14. Bayes example — defective from which machine? 
M1 50%, M2 30%, M3 20% of output ; defect rates 2%, 3%, 5%
P(D) = .5(.02)+.3(.03)+.2(.05) = .029

P(M3 | D) = .2(.05)/.029 = .010/.029 ≈ 0.345

M3 makes only 20% of items but ~34.5% of the defects — because its defect rate is highest. That re-weighting is the power of Bayes.

15. Prior, likelihood, evidence, posterior 
P(A|B) = P(A)·P(B|A) / P(B)

P(A)    = prior      (belief before the evidence)
P(B|A)  = likelihood (how well the cause explains the evidence)
P(B)    = evidence   (total probability of the evidence)
P(A|B)  = posterior  (updated belief after the evidence)

Example: P(Box1)=prior, P(Red|Box1)=likelihood, P(Red)=evidence, P(Box1|Red)=posterior.

16. Bayes for two causes (the disease test) 
P(A|B) = P(A)P(B|A) / [ P(A)P(B|A) + P(Aᶜ)P(B|Aᶜ) ]
disease 1%, test +99% if sick, +5% false alarm:
P(D|+) = .01(.99) / [.01(.99) + .99(.05)]
       = .0099 / (.0099 + .0495) ≈ 0.167

Base-rate lesson: even a "99% accurate" test gives only ~17% chance of disease when the disease is rare — the prior matters.

17. Total probability vs Bayes — which to use?
QuestionTool
P(result), e.g. P(ball is red)Law of total probability
P(cause | result), e.g. P(Box1 | red)Bayes' theorem
cause → result  = ordinary conditional probability
result → cause  = Bayes ("given that … , find the source")
18. Bayes traps
  • P(A|B) = P(B|A)? No — Bayes is precisely what relates the two.
  • Denominator = one path? No — it's the total probability over all causes.
  • Causes needn't partition? Wrong — they must be mutually exclusive AND exhaustive.
  • Ignore the prior? No — a high likelihood with a tiny prior can still give a small posterior (base rate).
19. Bayes practice 
Box1 {4R,6B}, Box2 {7R,3B}, pick a box then a ball.
P(R) = 1/2·4/10 + 1/2·7/10 = 11/20

Given red, P(Box2 | R) = (1/2 · 7/10)/(11/20) = (7/20)/(11/20) = 7/11
20. The birthday problem

"In n people, P(at least two share a birthday)?" — compute it via the complement and the chain rule (365 days, independent):

P(all different) = (365/365)(364/365)(363/365)…((365−n+1)/365)
P(at least two same) = 1 − P(all different)
n = 3: 1 − (365·364·363)/365³

It grows fast because you compare all nC2 = n(n−1)/2 pairs — for just 23 people it's already ≈ 50%. For GATE, know the method, not the decimal.

21. Counting both orders ("in any order")

If the order isn't fixed, add up every valid order.

one king AND one queen (2 cards, no replacement):
K then Q: 4/52 × 4/51
Q then K: 4/52 × 4/51
total = 2 × (4/52 × 4/51)

Trap: computing only "king then queen" forgets the "queen then king" order.

22. Cards without replacement (dependent)

Two cards, no replacement — P(both aces):

P(1st ace) = 4/52
P(2nd ace | 1st ace) = 3/51   (deck now 51 cards, 3 aces)
P(both) = 4/52 × 3/51 = 1/13 × 1/17 = 1/221

The second factor is a conditional probability — the events are dependent.

23. With vs without replacement 
WITHOUT replacement (dependent):  4/52 × 3/51 = 1/221
WITH replacement (independent):   4/52 × 4/52 = 1/169

Memory: with replacement → independent (probabilities unchanged); without replacement → dependent (update the counts).

24. Balls without replacement

Bag: 3 red, 2 blue. Draw 2 without replacement — P(both red):

P(R₁) = 3/5 ,  P(R₂|R₁) = 2/4
P(R₁ ∩ R₂) = 3/5 × 2/4 = 3/10
25. Independent examples 
two coins:  P(HH) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4
two dice:   P(6, 6) = 1/6 × 1/6 = 1/36
die & coin: P(6 and H) = 1/6 × 1/2 = 1/12

Different experiments (or with-replacement draws) don't influence each other.

26. Spot independent vs dependent fast
SituationType
toss a coin twice / roll a die twiceindependent
roll a die and toss a coinindependent
draw cards/balls WITH replacementindependent
draw cards/balls WITHOUT replacementdependent

Ask: does the first event change the second's probability? No → independent; Yes → dependent.

27. Testing independence

The standard GATE test:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A)·P(B)  ?   equal → independent ;  not equal → dependent

(Equivalently check P(A|B) = P(A) or P(B|A) = P(B).)

28. Independence within one experiment

Two events from the SAME roll can still be independent — test with the formula:

die: A = even {2,4,6}, B = multiple of 3 {3,6}
P(A)=1/2, P(B)=1/3, A∩B={6}→1/6 ;  P(A)P(B)=1/6  → EQUAL → independent
die: A = even {2,4,6}, B = ">3" {4,5,6}
A∩B={4,6}→1/3 ;  P(A)P(B)=1/4  → NOT equal → dependent
29. Mutually exclusive vs independent 
mutually exclusive: A ∩ B = ∅ → P(A∩B) = 0   (can't co-occur)
independent:        P(A∩B) = P(A)·P(B)        (no influence)

Different ideas — one is about overlap, the other about influence.

30. They can't both hold (for positive probability)

If A, B are mutually exclusive then P(A∩B)=0. If they were also independent, P(A)P(B)=0, forcing P(A)=0 or P(B)=0.

⇒ two events with positive probability cannot be BOTH
   mutually exclusive AND independent

Intuitively: if one happening blocks the other, it certainly affects it.

31. The two contrasting examples

Mutually exclusive but NOT independent:

one die: A = "2", B = "5"  →  P(A∩B)=0 ≠ 1/36 = P(A)P(B)

Independent but NOT mutually exclusive:

two dice: A = 1st is 2, B = 2nd is 5  →  both can happen; P(A∩B)=1/36=P(A)P(B)
32. Pairwise independence

Events A, B, C are pairwise independent if every pair is independent:

P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B) ,  P(A∩C)=P(A)P(C) ,  P(B∩C)=P(B)P(C)

This alone does not guarantee all three are independent together.

33. Mutual independence

A, B, C are mutually independent only if every pair is independent and the triple factorises:

P(A ∩ B ∩ C) = P(A)·P(B)·P(C)

Mutual independence is strictly stronger than pairwise independence.

34. Pairwise ≠ mutual (classic counterexample) 
two fair coins, S = {HH, HT, TH, TT}
A = 1st Head {HH,HT} ,  B = 2nd Head {HH,TH} ,  C = coins same {HH,TT}
P(A)=P(B)=P(C)=1/2
every pair: P(·∩·) = 1/4 = (1/2)(1/2)  → pairwise independent ✓
but A∩B∩C = {HH} → 1/4  ≠  (1/2)³ = 1/8  → NOT mutually independent

A famous GATE trap: pairwise independence does not imply mutual independence.

35. Formula summary
  • Conditional: P(B|A) = P(A∩B)/P(A).
  • General AND: P(A∩B) = P(A)P(B|A) = P(B)P(A|B).
  • Independent: P(A∩B) = P(A)P(B),   P(B|A)=P(B).
  • Mutually exclusive: P(A∩B)=0 (and then P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)).
  • Mutually independent (3): all pairs independent AND P(A∩B∩C)=P(A)P(B)P(C).
36. Common GATE traps
  • Mutually exclusive = independent? No (for positive-probability events, they can't both hold).
  • Independent = mutually exclusive? No — independent events can co-occur.
  • P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B) always? No — only when independent; general is P(A)P(B|A).
  • Without replacement is independent? No — it's dependent.
  • Pairwise independence ⇒ mutual independence? No (the two-coin counterexample).
  • P(A∩B)=0 ⇒ independent? No — that's mutually exclusive (for positive probabilities).

Q1. Two cards, no replacement — P(both kings)?

4/52 × 3/51 = 1/221

Q2. Two cards, with replacement — P(both kings)?

4/52 × 4/52 = 1/169

Q3. Die: A=even, B=multiple of 3 — independent?

38. Conditional expectation E(X|A) GATE extra

Conditional expectation is the average of X after filtering to the world where A happened.

E(X|A) = Σ x · P(X=x | A)        (normal probabilities → conditional ones)
die, A = "even": values 2,4,6 each P=1/3
E(X|A) = (2+4+6)/3 = 4   (vs the unconditional E(X)=3.5)

The condition shifts the average.

39. Linearity of conditional expectation GATE extra 
E(aX + b | A) = a·E(X|A) + b
E(X + Y | A)  = E(X|A) + E(Y|A)
E(X|A)=10 → E(2X+5|A) = 25

Same linearity as ordinary expectation, applied inside the condition.

40. Tower property (law of total expectation) GATE extra

The expectation version of the law of total probability: average the conditional averages, weighted by their probabilities.

E(X) = Σ E(X|Aᵢ) · P(Aᵢ)        (A₁,…,Aₖ a partition)
claims: P(young)=0.6, E(claim|young)=5000 ; P(old)=0.4, E(claim|old)=12000
E(claim) = 0.6(5000) + 0.4(12000) = 7800
41. Conditional expectation in machine learning GATE extra

A regression model essentially predicts a conditional expectation:

E(Y | X) = expected target given the features
e.g. E(price | area=1000 sqft, city=BLR, beds=2)

So "the prediction" is the conditional average of the target given the input — a core GATE DA idea.

42. Conditional variance Var(X|A) GATE extra

The spread of X within the condition A:

Var(X|A) = E[(X − E(X|A))² | A] = E(X²|A) − [E(X|A)]²
die, A = "even": E(X|A)=4, E(X²|A)=(4+16+36)/3=56/3
Var(X|A) = 56/3 − 16 = 8/3

Note: use the conditional mean E(X|A), not E(X). Transformation: Var(aX+b|A)=a²Var(X|A).

43. Law of total variance GATE extra 
Var(X) = E[ Var(X | Group) ]  +  Var( E[X | Group] )
       = (average spread WITHIN groups) + (spread BETWEEN group means)

Total uncertainty = how much values vary inside each group, plus how much the group averages differ. It underpins ANOVA, regression and ML uncertainty — where Var(Y|X) measures how reliable a prediction is (low = confident, high = uncertain).

44. Memory box
  • Independent: P(B|A)=P(B), P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B).
  • Dependent: P(B|A)≠P(B), P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B|A).
  • Mutually exclusive: A∩B=∅, P(A∩B)=0 — not the same as independent.
  • With replacement → independent; without → dependent.
  • Pairwise independent ≠ mutually independent.
45. Mental model 
Independent      = no effect on each other
Dependent        = probability changes after the first event
Mutually exclusive = cannot happen together

For an AND question, the only decision is: independent → multiply plain probabilities; dependent → multiply using the conditional P(B|A). (Bayes' theorem, built on this conditioning idea, comes next.)

6 Main formulas
$$ P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad P(A\cap B)=P(A\mid B)\,P(B) $$

Total probability (over a partition $B_1,\dots,B_n$):

$$ P(A)=\sum_{i} P(A\mid B_i)\,P(B_i) $$

Bayes' theorem:

$$ P(B_k\mid A)=\frac{P(A\mid B_k)\,P(B_k)}{\sum_i P(A\mid B_i)\,P(B_i)} $$

Conditional expectation & the tower property (law of total expectation):

$$ E(X\mid A)=\sum_x x\,P(X=x\mid A) \qquad E(X)=\sum_i E(X\mid B_i)\,P(B_i) $$

Conditional variance & law of total variance:

$$ \mathrm{Var}(X\mid A)=E(X^2\mid A)-[E(X\mid A)]^2 \qquad \mathrm{Var}(X)=E[\mathrm{Var}(X\mid Y)]+\mathrm{Var}[E(X\mid Y)] $$
7 Step-by-step: a Bayes problem
  1. Identify the hypotheses $B_i$ (e.g. disease / no disease) and their priors $P(B_i)$.
  2. Write the likelihoods $P(A\mid B_i)$ (e.g. test accuracy).
  3. Compute the evidence $P(A)=\sum_i P(A\mid B_i)P(B_i)$ (total probability).
  4. Divide: $P(B_k\mid A)=P(A\mid B_k)P(B_k)/P(A)$.
8 Worked example
✦ Medical test

Disease prevalence $P(D)=0.01$. Test: $P(+\mid D)=0.99$, $P(+\mid D^c)=0.05$.

$P(+)=0.99(0.01)+0.05(0.99)=0.0099+0.0495=0.0594$.

$P(D\mid +)=\dfrac{0.99\times0.01}{0.0594}\approx 0.167$. Only ~17%! — rare disease + false positives.

✦ Two machines

Machines A and B make $60\%$ and $40\%$ of items; their defect rates are $2\%$ and $5\%$.

$P(\text{defective})=0.6(0.02)+0.4(0.05)=0.012+0.020=0.032$.

$P(A\mid \text{defective})=\dfrac{0.012}{0.032}=0.375$ — a defective item is most likely from A, despite A's lower defect rate (it makes more items).

9 GATE cheat sheet
⚡ High-value facts
  • Multiplication: $P(A\cap B)=P(A\mid B)P(B)=P(B\mid A)P(A)$.
  • Independent ⇒ $P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)$ and $P(A\mid B)=P(A)$.
  • Bayes in words: posterior $\propto$ likelihood $\times$ prior.
  • With a rare event, even an accurate test gives a low posterior (base-rate effect).
  • Tower property: $E(X)=\sum_i E(X\mid B_i)P(B_i)$ — average the conditional means, weighted by their probabilities.
  • Conditioning reduces variance on average: $\mathrm{Var}(X)=E[\mathrm{Var}(X\mid Y)]+\mathrm{Var}[E(X\mid Y)]$ (within + between).
  • In ML, the regression function is a conditional expectation: $\hat{y}=E(Y\mid X)$.
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "$P(A\mid B)=P(B\mid A)$." Reality: generally not equal — that's exactly what Bayes corrects.

Trap: "Independent = mutually exclusive." Reality: different! Disjoint events with positive probability are dependent.

Trap: "Ignore the prior / base rate." Reality: the prior can dominate — that's the medical-test surprise.

Trap: "$E(X\mid A)$ equals $E(X)$." Reality: only if $X$ is independent of $A$; otherwise conditioning shifts the mean (die: $E(X\mid\text{even})=4\ne3.5$).

Trap: "Total variance is just the average of conditional variances." Reality: add the between-group spread $\mathrm{Var}[E(X\mid Y)]$ too.

11 Connections with other topics
  • Intro probability: conditional probability lives inside the addition/joint rules.
  • Random variables: conditional expectation extends this idea to numbers.
  • Joint distributions: independence of random variables generalizes event independence.
  • Machine learning: Naive Bayes classifiers are Bayes' theorem applied to features.
12 Practice problems

Q1. $P(A\cap B)=0.2,\ P(B)=0.5$. Find $P(A\mid B)$.

$0.2/0.5=0.4$.

Q2. $A,B$ independent with $P(A)=0.3,\ P(B)=0.5$. Find $P(A\cap B)$.

$0.3\times0.5=0.15$.

Q3. Two urns picked equally; urn1 has $P(\text{red})=0.8$, urn2 has $0.2$. Overall $P(\text{red})$?

Total probability: $0.5(0.8)+0.5(0.2)=0.5$.

Q4. Why is $P(\text{disease}\mid+)$ low even when the test is 99% accurate?

Because the disease is rare — most positives come from the many healthy people (false positives). The prior dominates.

Q5. Fair die $X$. Find $E(X\mid X\text{ is even})$.

Even values $\{2,4,6\}$ equally likely: $(2+4+6)/3=4$.

Q6. A factory uses machine A (prob 0.6, mean output 50) or B (prob 0.4, mean 70). Expected output?

Tower property: $0.6(50)+0.4(70)=30+28=58$.

Quick MCQ

If $A$ and $B$ are independent, which is true?
That's mutually exclusive, not independent.
Independence gives $P(A\mid B)=P(A)$, not $P(B)$.
✅ Correct — independence means the joint factorizes: $P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)$.
No general relation forces the union to be 1.
13 Final memory box
🧠 Remember
  • $P(A\mid B)=P(A\cap B)/P(B)$; knowing $B$ shrinks the sample space.
  • Independent ⇒ $P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)$, $P(A\mid B)=P(A)$.
  • Total probability: $P(A)=\sum P(A\mid B_i)P(B_i)$.
  • Bayes: posterior $\propto$ likelihood $\times$ prior; $P(A\mid B)\ne P(B\mid A)$.
  • Independent $\ne$ mutually exclusive.
  • Tower: $E(X)=\sum E(X\mid B_i)P(B_i)$; total variance = within $+$ between.
  • $E(Y\mid X)$ is the regression function — the bridge to machine learning.
