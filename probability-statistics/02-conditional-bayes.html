Topic 2 · GATE weight: Very High

Conditional Probability
& Bayes

How does knowing one fact change the probability of another? This is the engine behind Bayes' theorem — and almost every inference question in GATE DA.

2 Why this topic exists

Real decisions use partial information: a test came back positive, the first card was red. Conditional probability updates a probability once you learn something, and Bayes' theorem flips a conditional you know into the one you want — the heart of diagnosis, spam filters, and ML classifiers.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

Knowing $B$ happened shrinks the sample space down to just $B$. $P(A\mid B)$ asks: within that smaller world of $B$, what fraction is also $A$?   Bayes = walking the conditional backwards: you know $P(\text{test}\mid \text{disease})$ but want $P(\text{disease}\mid \text{test})$.

4 Basic definitions
  • Joint probability $P(A\cap B)$: both $A$ and $B$ happen.
  • Conditional probability $P(A\mid B)$: probability of $A$ given $B$ already happened.
  • Independence: $A,B$ are independent if knowing one doesn't change the other: $P(A\mid B)=P(A)$, equivalently $P(A\cap B)=P(A)\,P(B)$.
  • Partition: events $B_1,\dots,B_n$ that are disjoint and cover all of $S$ (used in total probability / Bayes).
📖 Study in Depth — conditional probability & independence from zero

Two events can influence each other or not. Independent events leave each other's probability unchanged; dependent events don't. The tool that captures the link is conditional probability P(B|A), which also powers the multiplication rule and (next) Bayes' theorem. Open each card in order.

1. Does A affect B?

The central question of this topic: does one event change the chance of another?

draw 2 cards WITHOUT replacing → the first draw changes the deck → dependent
roll a die, then toss a coin     → the die can't change the coin → independent

The answer decides which multiplication rule you use.

2. Game imagination 
Two separate loot boxes (same drop rate): opening one doesn't change the
   other → INDEPENDENT.
A limited chest of 5 items, no refill: removing one changes what's left
   → DEPENDENT.

Memory: independent = probability stays the same; dependent = probability changes after the first event.

3. Conditional probability P(B|A)

P(B|A) ("probability of B given A") = the chance of B once you know A has happened.

P(B|A) = P(A ∩ B) / P(A)        (for P(A) > 0)

It re-scales probability to the smaller world where A is known true.

die: A = even {2,4,6}, B = ">4" {5,6}
P(B|A) = P({6})/P(A) = (1/6)/(3/6) = 1/3
4. The general multiplication rule

Rearranging the conditional formula gives the AND rule for any two events:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B|A) = P(B) · P(A|B)

"Probability of A and B = probability of A, times probability of B once A has happened."

5. Independent events (via conditioning)

A and B are independent when knowing A tells you nothing about B:

P(B|A) = P(B)   ⟺   P(A|B) = P(A)   ⟺   P(A ∩ B) = P(A)·P(B)

So independence is the special case where the multiplication rule simplifies — the conditional probability equals the plain probability.

6. Dependent events

A and B are dependent when A changes B's chance:

P(B|A) ≠ P(B)   →   use  P(A ∩ B) = P(A)·P(B|A)

You must use the conditional probability, not the plain one.

7. Cards without replacement (dependent)

Two cards, no replacement — P(both aces):

P(1st ace) = 4/52
P(2nd ace | 1st ace) = 3/51   (deck now 51 cards, 3 aces)
P(both) = 4/52 × 3/51 = 1/13 × 1/17 = 1/221

The second factor is a conditional probability — the events are dependent.

8. With vs without replacement 
WITHOUT replacement (dependent):  4/52 × 3/51 = 1/221
WITH replacement (independent):   4/52 × 4/52 = 1/169

Memory: with replacement → independent (probabilities unchanged); without replacement → dependent (update the counts).

9. Balls without replacement

Bag: 3 red, 2 blue. Draw 2 without replacement — P(both red):

P(R₁) = 3/5 ,  P(R₂|R₁) = 2/4
P(R₁ ∩ R₂) = 3/5 × 2/4 = 3/10
10. Independent examples 
two coins:  P(HH) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4
two dice:   P(6, 6) = 1/6 × 1/6 = 1/36
die & coin: P(6 and H) = 1/6 × 1/2 = 1/12

Different experiments (or with-replacement draws) don't influence each other.

11. Spot independent vs dependent fast
SituationType
toss a coin twice / roll a die twiceindependent
roll a die and toss a coinindependent
draw cards/balls WITH replacementindependent
draw cards/balls WITHOUT replacementdependent

Ask: does the first event change the second's probability? No → independent; Yes → dependent.

12. Testing independence

The standard GATE test:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A)·P(B)  ?   equal → independent ;  not equal → dependent

(Equivalently check P(A|B) = P(A) or P(B|A) = P(B).)

13. Independence within one experiment

Two events from the SAME roll can still be independent — test with the formula:

die: A = even {2,4,6}, B = multiple of 3 {3,6}
P(A)=1/2, P(B)=1/3, A∩B={6}→1/6 ;  P(A)P(B)=1/6  → EQUAL → independent
die: A = even {2,4,6}, B = ">3" {4,5,6}
A∩B={4,6}→1/3 ;  P(A)P(B)=1/4  → NOT equal → dependent
14. Mutually exclusive vs independent 
mutually exclusive: A ∩ B = ∅ → P(A∩B) = 0   (can't co-occur)
independent:        P(A∩B) = P(A)·P(B)        (no influence)

Different ideas — one is about overlap, the other about influence.

15. They can't both hold (for positive probability)

If A, B are mutually exclusive then P(A∩B)=0. If they were also independent, P(A)P(B)=0, forcing P(A)=0 or P(B)=0.

⇒ two events with positive probability cannot be BOTH
   mutually exclusive AND independent

Intuitively: if one happening blocks the other, it certainly affects it.

16. The two contrasting examples

Mutually exclusive but NOT independent:

one die: A = "2", B = "5"  →  P(A∩B)=0 ≠ 1/36 = P(A)P(B)

Independent but NOT mutually exclusive:

two dice: A = 1st is 2, B = 2nd is 5  →  both can happen; P(A∩B)=1/36=P(A)P(B)
17. Pairwise independence

Events A, B, C are pairwise independent if every pair is independent:

P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B) ,  P(A∩C)=P(A)P(C) ,  P(B∩C)=P(B)P(C)

This alone does not guarantee all three are independent together.

18. Mutual independence

A, B, C are mutually independent only if every pair is independent and the triple factorises:

P(A ∩ B ∩ C) = P(A)·P(B)·P(C)

Mutual independence is strictly stronger than pairwise independence.

19. Pairwise ≠ mutual (classic counterexample) 
two fair coins, S = {HH, HT, TH, TT}
A = 1st Head {HH,HT} ,  B = 2nd Head {HH,TH} ,  C = coins same {HH,TT}
P(A)=P(B)=P(C)=1/2
every pair: P(·∩·) = 1/4 = (1/2)(1/2)  → pairwise independent ✓
but A∩B∩C = {HH} → 1/4  ≠  (1/2)³ = 1/8  → NOT mutually independent

A famous GATE trap: pairwise independence does not imply mutual independence.

20. Formula summary
  • Conditional: P(B|A) = P(A∩B)/P(A).
  • General AND: P(A∩B) = P(A)P(B|A) = P(B)P(A|B).
  • Independent: P(A∩B) = P(A)P(B),   P(B|A)=P(B).
  • Mutually exclusive: P(A∩B)=0 (and then P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)).
  • Mutually independent (3): all pairs independent AND P(A∩B∩C)=P(A)P(B)P(C).
21. Common GATE traps
  • Mutually exclusive = independent? No (for positive-probability events, they can't both hold).
  • Independent = mutually exclusive? No — independent events can co-occur.
  • P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B) always? No — only when independent; general is P(A)P(B|A).
  • Without replacement is independent? No — it's dependent.
  • Pairwise independence ⇒ mutual independence? No (the two-coin counterexample).
  • P(A∩B)=0 ⇒ independent? No — that's mutually exclusive (for positive probabilities).

Q1. Two cards, no replacement — P(both kings)?

Q2. Two cards, with replacement — P(both kings)?

4/52 × 4/52 = 1/169

23. Memory box
  • Independent: P(B|A)=P(B), P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B).
  • Dependent: P(B|A)≠P(B), P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B|A).
  • Mutually exclusive: A∩B=∅, P(A∩B)=0 — not the same as independent.
  • With replacement → independent; without → dependent.
  • Pairwise independent ≠ mutually independent.
24. Mental model 
Independent      = no effect on each other
Dependent        = probability changes after the first event
Mutually exclusive = cannot happen together

For an AND question, the only decision is: independent → multiply plain probabilities; dependent → multiply using the conditional P(B|A). (Bayes' theorem, built on this conditioning idea, comes next.)

6 Main formulas
$$ P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad P(A\cap B)=P(A\mid B)\,P(B) $$

Total probability (over a partition $B_1,\dots,B_n$):

$$ P(A)=\sum_{i} P(A\mid B_i)\,P(B_i) $$

Bayes' theorem:

$$ P(B_k\mid A)=\frac{P(A\mid B_k)\,P(B_k)}{\sum_i P(A\mid B_i)\,P(B_i)} $$
7 Step-by-step: a Bayes problem
  1. Identify the hypotheses $B_i$ (e.g. disease / no disease) and their priors $P(B_i)$.
  2. Write the likelihoods $P(A\mid B_i)$ (e.g. test accuracy).
  3. Compute the evidence $P(A)=\sum_i P(A\mid B_i)P(B_i)$ (total probability).
  4. Divide: $P(B_k\mid A)=P(A\mid B_k)P(B_k)/P(A)$.
8 Worked example
✦ Medical test

Disease prevalence $P(D)=0.01$. Test: $P(+\mid D)=0.99$, $P(+\mid D^c)=0.05$.

$P(+)=0.99(0.01)+0.05(0.99)=0.0099+0.0495=0.0594$.

$P(D\mid +)=\dfrac{0.99\times0.01}{0.0594}\approx 0.167$. Only ~17%! — rare disease + false positives.

9 GATE cheat sheet
⚡ High-value facts
  • Multiplication: $P(A\cap B)=P(A\mid B)P(B)=P(B\mid A)P(A)$.
  • Independent ⇒ $P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)$ and $P(A\mid B)=P(A)$.
  • Bayes in words: posterior $\propto$ likelihood $\times$ prior.
  • With a rare event, even an accurate test gives a low posterior (base-rate effect).
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "$P(A\mid B)=P(B\mid A)$." Reality: generally not equal — that's exactly what Bayes corrects.

Trap: "Independent = mutually exclusive." Reality: different! Disjoint events with positive probability are dependent.

Trap: "Ignore the prior / base rate." Reality: the prior can dominate — that's the medical-test surprise.

11 Connections with other topics
  • Intro probability: conditional probability lives inside the addition/joint rules.
  • Random variables: conditional expectation extends this idea to numbers.
  • Joint distributions: independence of random variables generalizes event independence.
  • Machine learning: Naive Bayes classifiers are Bayes' theorem applied to features.
12 Practice problems

Q1. $P(A\cap B)=0.2,\ P(B)=0.5$. Find $P(A\mid B)$.

$0.2/0.5=0.4$.

Q2. $A,B$ independent with $P(A)=0.3,\ P(B)=0.5$. Find $P(A\cap B)$.

$0.3\times0.5=0.15$.

Q3. Two urns picked equally; urn1 has $P(\text{red})=0.8$, urn2 has $0.2$. Overall $P(\text{red})$?

Total probability: $0.5(0.8)+0.5(0.2)=0.5$.

Q4. Why is $P(\text{disease}\mid+)$ low even when the test is 99% accurate?

Because the disease is rare — most positives come from the many healthy people (false positives). The prior dominates.

Quick MCQ

If $A$ and $B$ are independent, which is true?
That's mutually exclusive, not independent.
Independence gives $P(A\mid B)=P(A)$, not $P(B)$.
✅ Correct — independence means the joint factorizes: $P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)$.
No general relation forces the union to be 1.
13 Final memory box
🧠 Remember
  • $P(A\mid B)=P(A\cap B)/P(B)$; knowing $B$ shrinks the sample space.
  • Independent ⇒ $P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)$, $P(A\mid B)=P(A)$.
  • Total probability: $P(A)=\sum P(A\mid B_i)P(B_i)$.
  • Bayes: posterior $\propto$ likelihood $\times$ prior; $P(A\mid B)\ne P(B\mid A)$.
  • Independent $\ne$ mutually exclusive.
