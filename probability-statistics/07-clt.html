Central Limit Theorem

The "magic" of statistics: average enough independent samples and the average is always bell-shaped — no matter what the original data looked like.

2 Why this topic exists

The CLT is why the Normal distribution is everywhere and why hypothesis testing works. Even if individual data is weird (a lopsided die, skewed incomes), the mean of many samples follows a Normal curve. That lets us put error bars and confidence on averages.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

Roll $n$ dice, write down the average, repeat thousands of times, and stack those averages into a histogram. Even though one die is flat (uniform), the averages pile into a bell curve centered at the true mean — and it gets narrower as $n$ grows. Watch it happen below.

Increase the sample size $n$ and the bell tightens (spread $=\sigma/\sqrt{n}$); the coral curve is the CLT's prediction.

4 Basic definitions
  • Population vs sample: the whole group vs the subset you actually measure.
  • Parameter vs statistic: a fixed population number (e.g. $\mu$) vs a quantity computed from the sample (e.g. $\bar{X}$).
  • Sampling distribution: the distribution of a statistic (like $\bar{X}$) over many samples.
  • Standard error (SE): the standard deviation of $\bar{X}$, equal to $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ — it shrinks as $n$ grows.
  • Law of large numbers: as $n$ grows, $\bar{X}$ closes in on the true mean $\mu$.
  • Central Limit Theorem: for large $n$, $\bar{X}$ is approximately Normal with mean $\mu$ and SE $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ — whatever the population's shape.
📖 Study in Depth — sampling & standard error from zero
1. Sampling

Sampling = selecting some items from a bigger group instead of measuring everyone.

To estimate the average height of all college students, measure a selected 100 rather than all 10,000.

2. Population vs sample 
Population = the COMPLETE group you want to study (all 10,000 students)
Sample     = a smaller selected part (500 of them)

We sample because measuring everyone is often expensive, slow, or impossible.

3. Game imagination

You have 10,000 loot boxes but can't open them all. Open 100 and estimate the drop rate.

10,000 boxes = population ;  100 opened = sample
estimated drop rate = statistic ;  true drop rate = parameter
4. Random sampling

Random sampling uses chance to pick, to avoid bias.

biased:  ask only your friends about exam difficulty
random:  pick students randomly from the full class list
5. Simple random sampling

Every individual has an equal chance of being selected (and every group of that size is equally likely).

Memory: simple random sampling = equal-chance selection.

6. Why random sampling matters

A biased sample gives a wrong conclusion. For average city salary: asking only IT-park workers reads too high; only students too low. Random sampling makes the sample represent the population.

7. Parameter vs statistic 
Parameter = a POPULATION value (fixed, usually unknown)
   μ = population mean, σ = population SD, p = population proportion
Statistic = a SAMPLE value (changes sample to sample)
   x̄ = sample mean, s = sample SD, p̂ = sample proportion

Memory: Parameter ↔ Population, Statistic ↔ Sample.

8. Parameter vs statistic — table
ConceptDescribesSymbol
Parameterpopulation (fixed, often unknown)μ, σ, p
Statisticsample (varies)x̄, s, p̂

We use the statistic to estimate the parameter.

9. Example 
Company has 1,00,000 users; want average daily usage.
population = all users ;  parameter = true average of all
sample = random 1,000 users ;  statistic = their average
10. Sampling error

Sampling error = difference between the sample statistic and the population parameter.

μ = 170 cm, x̄ = 168 cm  →  sampling error = x̄ − μ = −2 cm

It happens because a sample is only part of the population — even with random sampling.

11. Standard error: SE = σ/√n

Standard error measures how much a statistic varies from sample to sample. For the mean:

SE = σ / √n      (use s/√n if σ is unknown)

Bigger samples → smaller SE. Make n 4× bigger and SE halves (√4 = 2).

12. Standard-error intuition

Take many samples of size 100 and compute each mean — you get 68, 70, 69, 71, … The spread of those sample means is the standard error.

13. Standard deviation vs standard error 
Standard deviation (σ, s) = spread of individual data values
Standard error (σ/√n)     = spread of the sample MEAN

Memory: SD = data spread; SE = uncertainty of the estimate.

14. The Central Limit Theorem

Take samples of size n from any population (mean μ, SD σ). As n grows, the distribution of the sample mean becomes approximately normal:

x̄  ≈  N(μ, σ²/n)
E(x̄) = μ ,  Var(x̄) = σ²/n ,  SE = σ/√n

The population need not be normal — that is the magic. Rule of thumb: n ≥ 30 (or any n if the population is already normal).

15. Why averages turn normal (dice)

One die roll is uniform (flat), not normal. But average many rolls, repeat, and the averages pile into a bell:

one die: μ = 3.5 , σ² = 35/12
n = 100:  x̄ ≈ N(3.5, (35/12)/100)

The spread of the averages (SE) is tiny compared with single rolls, so they cluster tightly around 3.5.

16. Bigger n → tighter & more normal 
SE = σ/√n  shrinks as n grows:   n=25 → σ/5 ,  n=100 → σ/10

Quadruple the data to halve the SE. Larger samples make the sample mean both more normal and more stable (a more accurate estimate of μ).

17. Where the CLT shows up
  • exam scores — individuals skewed, but the class average is ~normal
  • manufacturing — individual weights vary; sample-mean weight is ~normal
  • polling — yes/no votes; the sample proportion is ~normal for large n
  • dice — single roll uniform, average of many rolls ~normal

Any time you average or sum many independent pieces, normality appears.

18. Confidence interval — estimate ± uncertainty

A point estimate is a single number (x̄ = 72), but the true μ may differ. A confidence interval reports a plausible range instead:

CI = Estimate ± Margin of Error
   = Estimate ± (critical value × SE)

Point estimate → one value; interval estimate → a range we are reasonably confident contains the parameter.

19. CI for the mean when σ is known 
x̄ ± Z · σ/√n          (Z = critical z-value, σ/√n = standard error)
confidence:  90% → Z=1.645 ,  95% → Z=1.96 ,  99% → Z=2.576

Higher confidence ⇒ larger Z ⇒ wider interval — more certainty costs precision.

20. Confidence interval — worked 
x̄ = 100 , σ = 15 , n = 25 , 95% confidence
SE = 15/√25 = 3
ME = 1.96 × 3 = 5.88
CI = 100 ± 5.88 = [94.12 , 105.88]
21. What “95% confidence” really means

Careful: it does not mean “there is a 95% probability the fixed true mean lies in this interval.”

It means: if we repeated the whole sampling process many times, about 95% of the intervals produced would contain μ. The procedure is 95% reliable.

22. Common GATE traps
  • Sample = population? No — sample is a selected part.
  • Statistic = parameter? No — statistic ↔ sample, parameter ↔ population.
  • SD = SE? No — SD is data spread; SE = σ/√n is estimate spread.
  • SE increases with n? No — it decreases (SE = σ/√n).
23. Practice problems

Q1. σ = 20, n = 100 — find SE.

SE = 20/√100 = 20/10 = 2

Q2. n goes 100 → 400 — what happens to SE?

√100=10, √400=20 → SE halves

Q3. True average salary of all employees is a … ?

parameter (population)

Q4. Average salary of 50 selected employees is a … ?

statistic (sample)
24. Memory box & mental model
  • Population = the full world; sample = a selected mini-world.
  • Parameter = true value of the full world (μ, σ, p); statistic = measured value from the sample (x̄, s, p̂).
  • Sampling error = how far the estimate landed; standard error σ/√n = how shaky the estimate is.
  • More data (larger n) → smaller standard error → more reliable estimate.

6 Main formula
$$ \bar{X}\ \approx\ \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mu,\ \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)\quad\text{for large }n $$
$$ Z=\frac{\bar{X}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\ \approx\ \mathcal{N}(0,1) $$

Confidence interval for the mean (σ known) — a CLT application:

$$ \bar{X}\ \pm\ Z\,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \qquad (Z_{90}=1.645,\ Z_{95}=1.96,\ Z_{99}=2.576) $$
7 Step-by-step: use the CLT
  1. Find the population mean $\mu$ and std $\sigma$.
  2. Compute the standard error of the mean: $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$.
  3. Model $\bar{X}$ as Normal$(\mu,\ \sigma^2/n)$.
  4. Standardize and use the $Z$-table for probabilities about $\bar{X}$.
8 Worked example
✦ Mean of 36 dice

One die: $\mu=3.5$, $\sigma\approx1.71$. For $n=36$, SE $=1.71/\sqrt{36}=0.285$.

So the average of 36 rolls is $\approx\mathcal{N}(3.5,\ 0.285^2)$ — tightly around 3.5, and bell-shaped.

9 GATE cheat sheet
⚡ High-value facts
  • Mean of $\bar{X}$ is $\mu$; SE is $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ — spread shrinks like $1/\sqrt{n}$.
  • To halve the SE, you need 4× the data.
  • CLT needs independent samples; "large $n$" (often $n\ge30$) makes $\bar X$ approximately Normal.
  • If the population is already Normal, $\bar X$ is Normal for any $n$.
  • Confidence interval $=$ estimate $\pm$ (critical value $\times$ SE); for the mean, $\bar X\pm Z\,\sigma/\sqrt n$.
  • $Z$ for $90/95/99\%$ $=1.645/1.96/2.576$; higher confidence $\Rightarrow$ wider interval.
  • "95% CI" = the procedure captures $\mu$ 95% of the time, not a probability about this one interval.
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "The CLT makes the data normal." Reality: it makes the sample mean normal, not the raw data.

Trap: "SE $=\sigma$." Reality: SE $=\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ — it shrinks with sample size.

Trap: "SE halves when $n$ doubles." Reality: it falls by $\sqrt{2}$; you need $4n$ to halve it.

Trap: "Higher confidence gives a narrower interval." Reality: wider — a bigger $Z$ widens it.

11 Connections with other topics
  • Distributions: the limiting shape is the Normal from Topic 4.
  • Descriptive stats: $\bar X$ and $s$ are the inputs; the CLT gives their behavior.
  • Hypothesis testing: z and t tests rely directly on the CLT.
12 Practice problems

Q1. $\sigma=10$, $n=100$. Standard error of the mean?

$10/\sqrt{100}=1$.

Q2. To halve a standard error, how much more data do you need?

4× the sample size (since SE $\propto 1/\sqrt{n}$).

Q3. Does the CLT require the population to be normal?

No — that's the whole point; it works for almost any population given large $n$.

Q4. $\bar X=100$, $\sigma=15$, $n=25$. Build the 95% confidence interval.

SE $=15/5=3$; ME $=1.96\times3=5.88$ → $[94.12,\ 105.88]$.

Q5. To get a narrower interval at the same confidence, what should you do?

Increase $n$ (shrinks SE). Raising confidence would widen it.

Quick MCQ

The standard error of the sample mean is…
That's the population std; the mean's spread is smaller.
That's the variance of $\bar X$; SE is its square root.
✅ Correct — SE $=\sigma/\sqrt{n}$.
Spread shrinks with $n$, it doesn't grow.
13 Final memory box
🧠 Remember
  • $\bar X\approx\mathcal{N}(\mu,\ \sigma^2/n)$ for large $n$, any population.
  • Standard error $=\sigma/\sqrt{n}$; shrinks like $1/\sqrt{n}$.
  • It normalizes the mean, not the raw data.
  • Already-Normal population ⇒ $\bar X$ Normal for any $n$.
  • CI for the mean: $\bar X\pm Z\,\sigma/\sqrt n$ ($Z_{95}=1.96$); higher confidence = wider, bigger $n$ = narrower.
