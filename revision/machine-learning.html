🤖 Basic Machine Learning

Closed forms, algorithms and gotchas — condensed for last-week revision.

1 · Simple linear regression
$$\hat y=\beta_0+\beta_1x \qquad \beta_1=\frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}=\frac{\sum(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2}=r\,\frac{s_y}{s_x} \qquad \beta_0=\bar y-\beta_1\bar x$$

The fitted line always passes through $(\bar x,\bar y)$. Residual $e_i=y_i-\hat y_i$; residuals sum to zero (with intercept).

$$R^2=1-\frac{\text{SSE}}{\text{SST}}=\frac{\text{SSR}}{\text{SST}}\in[0,1] \qquad \text{(simple regression: } R^2=r^2\text{)} \qquad \text{MSE}=\tfrac1n\sum e_i^2$$
2 · Multiple regression & least squares
$$\hat\beta=(X^{\mathsf T}X)^{-1}X^{\mathsf T}y=X^{+}y \qquad \hat y=X\hat\beta=Hy,\ \ H=X(X^{\mathsf T}X)^{-1}X^{\mathsf T}$$

Least squares minimizes $\|y-X\beta\|^2$ — the sum of squared residuals (vertical distances). Geometrically: $\hat y$ is the orthogonal projection of $y$ onto the column space of $X$; residual $\perp$ every column of $X$. Needs $X^{\mathsf T}X$ invertible (full column rank, no perfect multicollinearity).

3 · k-Nearest Neighbours

Algorithm: compute distances to all training points → take $k$ closest → classification: majority vote; regression: average. No training phase ("lazy learner"); prediction cost $O(nd)$ per query.

$$\text{Euclidean: }\sqrt{\textstyle\sum(x_i-y_i)^2}\qquad\text{Manhattan: }\textstyle\sum|x_i-y_i|\qquad\text{Minkowski: }\big(\textstyle\sum|x_i-y_i|^p\big)^{1/p}$$

Effect of $k$: small $k$ → flexible boundary, low bias, high variance, overfits ($k=1$: zero training error); large $k$ → smoother boundary, high bias, low variance, underfits. Use odd $k$ to avoid ties (binary). Feature scaling matters — unscaled features dominate the distance.

4 · Logistic regression
$$P(y=1\mid x)=\sigma(w^{\mathsf T}x+b)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-(w^{\mathsf T}x+b)}} \qquad \sigma(0)=0.5,\ \ \sigma(-z)=1-\sigma(z)$$
$$\text{Log-odds: }\ \ln\frac{p}{1-p}=w^{\mathsf T}x+b \qquad \text{Loss (cross-entropy): } -\tfrac1n\sum\big[y_i\ln p_i+(1-y_i)\ln(1-p_i)\big]$$

A classifier, not a regressor. Decision boundary $w^{\mathsf T}x+b=0$ is linear. No closed form — train by gradient descent (loss is convex). Squared error is not used: it's non-convex with sigmoid.

5 · k-means clustering
$$\min_{C}\ \sum_{j=1}^{k}\sum_{x\in C_j}\|x-\mu_j\|^2 \qquad\text{(WCSS / inertia)}$$

Lloyd's algorithm: init $k$ centroids → (1) assign each point to nearest centroid; (2) recompute centroids as cluster means; repeat until assignments stop changing. WCSS never increases → always converges, but only to a local optimum (run multiple inits). Sensitive to initialization, scale, and outliers; finds roughly spherical clusters; $k$ chosen by elbow method. Unsupervised — no labels.

6 · Cross-validation & bias–variance

k-fold CV: split data into $k$ folds; train on $k-1$, validate on the held-out fold; rotate so every point is validated exactly once; average the $k$ scores. LOOCV = $k=n$ (low bias, high variance, expensive). Estimates generalization error and is used to pick hyperparameters (e.g. $k$ in kNN). Never let test data leak into training (fit scalers inside the loop).

$$\text{Expected error}=\text{Bias}^2+\text{Variance}+\text{Irreducible noise}$$

Underfit: high bias — high train and validation error. Overfit: high variance — low train, high validation error. Fixes for overfit: more data, simpler model, regularization; for underfit: richer model/features.

Exam traps
  • $\sigma(0)=0.5$ — compute $z=w^{\mathsf T}x+b$ first; if $z=0$ the answer is exactly 0.5.
  • kNN with $k=1$ gives 0 training error — that's overfitting, not a good model.
  • k-means converges, but not necessarily to the global optimum.
  • Slope questions: compute $\bar x,\bar y$ first, then $S_{xy}/S_{xx}$ — arithmetic slips cost easy marks.
  • "Least squares minimizes distance to the line" — false; it minimizes vertical squared errors.
