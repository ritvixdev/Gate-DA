Optimality conditions, convexity tests, and gradient descent ŌĆö condensed for last-week revision.

1 ┬Ę Types of optimization & convexity

Classify by: constrained vs unconstrained; univariate vs multivariate; convex vs non-convex; linear vs nonlinear objective.

$$\text{Convex: } f(\theta x+(1-\theta)y)\le\theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y),\ \ \theta\in[0,1]$$

Tests: univariate $f''(x)\ge0$ everywhere; multivariate Hessian PSD everywhere. Key consequence: for convex $f$, every local minimum is global; for strictly convex $f$, the minimizer is unique.

Convex: $x^2$, $e^x$, $|x|$, $\max$, norms, $-\log x$. Concave: $\log x$, $\sqrt x$. Linear functions are both. Sum of convex functions is convex; max of convex is convex.

2 ┬Ę Univariate optimization
$$f'(x^*)=0 \text{ (necessary)} \qquad f''(x^*)>0\Rightarrow\min \qquad f''(x^*)<0\Rightarrow\max$$

If $f''(x^*)=0$: inconclusive ŌĆö check higher derivatives or sign change of $f'$ (e.g. $x^3$ has an inflection at 0, $x^4$ a min). On a closed interval also compare endpoints. Quadratic $ax^2+bx+c$ ($a>0$): minimum at $x=-\tfrac{b}{2a}$, value $c-\tfrac{b^2}{4a}$.

$$\text{Taylor: } f(x+h)\approx f(x)+f'(x)h+\tfrac12 f''(x)h^2$$
3 ┬Ę Multivariate optimization
$$\nabla f(x^*)=0 \quad\text{(stationary)} \qquad H_{ij}=\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i\partial x_j}\ \ (\text{symmetric})$$

Classify a stationary point by Hessian eigenvalues: all $>0$ (PD) ŌåÆ local min; all $<0$ (ND) ŌåÆ local max; mixed signs ŌåÆ saddle point; some zero ŌåÆ inconclusive.

$2\times2$ shortcut: $\det H>0,\ h_{11}>0$ ŌåÆ min; $\det H>0,\ h_{11}<0$ ŌåÆ max; $\det H<0$ ŌåÆ saddle.

$$\text{Quadratic } f(x)=\tfrac12x^{\mathsf T}Qx-b^{\mathsf T}x:\quad \nabla f=Qx-b,\ \ H=Q,\ \ x^*=Q^{-1}b$$
4 ┬Ę Gradient descent
$$x_{k+1}=x_k-\eta\,\nabla f(x_k)$$

Moves in steepest-descent direction $-\nabla f$. Learning rate $\eta$: too small ŌåÆ slow; too large ŌåÆ oscillation/divergence. For quadratic $f(x)=\tfrac12 x^{\mathsf T}Qx$, GD converges iff $\eta<2/\lambda_{\max}(Q)$. Univariate $f(x)=ax^2$: $x_{k+1}=(1-2a\eta)x_k$, so convergence needs $|1-2a\eta|<1 \iff 0<\eta<1/a$.

Hand-computation pattern (asked constantly): $f(x)=x^2$, $\eta=0.25$, $x_0=4$: $x_1=4-0.25(8)=2$, $x_2=2-0.25(4)=1$ ŌĆö each step halves $x$ since $x_{k+1}=(1-2\eta)x_k$.

On convex functions with a suitable $\eta$, GD reaches the global minimum; on non-convex it may stop at local minima or saddle points. Stopping: $\|\nabla f\|<\varepsilon$ or max iterations.

Exam traps
  • $\nabla f=0$ is necessary, not sufficient ŌĆö always classify with $f''$/Hessian.
  • A saddle point also has zero gradient; "mixed eigenvalue signs" is the tell.
  • Maximizing $f$ = minimizing $-f$; gradient ascent uses $+\eta\nabla f$.
  • For NAT gradient-descent questions, simulate 2ŌĆō3 steps carefully ŌĆö sign errors are the usual killer.
