Gradient Descent

When you can't solve $\nabla f=0$ by hand, just walk downhill: take small steps in the direction the slope falls fastest. This is how almost all ML models train.

2 Why this topic exists

Setting derivatives to zero works on paper, but real loss functions have millions of variables and no closed-form solution. Gradient descent finds the minimum iteratively — it's the workhorse algorithm behind training neural networks and most of modern ML.

3 Game / visual imagination
🎮 Imagine it

You're a ball on a hilly surface in fog — you can only feel the slope under your feet. Each step, move a little in the downhill direction ($-\nabla f$). The learning rate is your step size: too small and you crawl; too big and you overshoot or bounce out; just right and you settle into the valley.

Try it: step or run, change the learning rate, and switch to the Double-well to see how a bad start can leave you stuck in a local minimum.

4 Basic definitions
  • Gradient descent: repeatedly step against the gradient to reduce $f$.
  • Learning rate $\eta$: the step-size multiplier — too small is slow, too big overshoots.
  • Update step: one move $x_{k+1}=x_k-\eta\nabla f(x_k)$. Iteration / epoch: one such pass.
  • Convergence: the steps settle where $\nabla f\approx0$ (a minimum).
  • Divergence: if $\eta$ is too large, steps overshoot and $f$ grows instead of shrinking.
  • Batch vs stochastic (SGD): use all data per step, vs one point (or a mini-batch) — faster but noisier steps.
  • Momentum: add a fraction of the previous step to speed up and smooth the path.
📖 Study in Depth — gradient descent & the optimizers that train ML models, from zero
1. Optimization in ML — minimize the loss

Training a model = finding the parameters that make a loss/cost J(θ) smallest.

find θ that minimizes J(θ)   (θ = weights & biases)

For most models there's no closed form, so we improve θ iteratively — that's what gradient descent does.

2. Gradient descent — walk downhill 
θ := θ − η ∇J(θ)        η = learning rate, ∇J = gradient

The gradient points uphill (steepest increase), so we step the opposite way to go down.

slope positive → move left · slope negative → move right
3. The learning rate η 
too small → very slow, may stall
too large → overshoots, loss bounces or diverges
good       → steady, fast descent

It's the single most important optimization hyperparameter — the step size while walking down the loss hill.

4. Watch it converge — and what it trains

Apply the rule repeatedly and the point slides into the valley. Minimize f(x)=x² (so f′=2x) from x₀=10 with η=0.1:

x₁ = 10 − 0.1(20) = 8
x₂ = 8  − 0.1(16) = 6.4
x₃ = 6.4 − 0.1(12.8) = 5.12
…  → 0  (the minimum) — each step the gradient shrinks, so the steps shrink too

This exact loop — repeatedly walk downhill along −∇J — trains linear regression, logistic regression, neural networks and deep-learning models. Gradient descent is the bridge between calculus and AI.

5. Batch vs stochastic vs mini-batch 
batch GD      : use ALL data per update — stable but slow
stochastic (SGD): one sample per update — fast, noisy, can escape shallow minima
mini-batch    : a small batch (e.g. 32, 64) — the practical sweet spot
θ := θ − η ∇L_batch(θ)
6. Momentum

Momentum adds a fraction of the previous update, smoothing the path and speeding through flat/ravine regions:

v := β v + ∇J ;  θ := θ − η v

Like a ball rolling downhill — it builds speed and dampens zig-zags.

7. AdaGrad

Gives each parameter its own learning rate by dividing by the accumulated squared gradients:

big past gradients → smaller steps ; small past gradients → larger steps

Great for sparse features, but the accumulation makes the rate shrink toward 0 over time (training can stall).

8. RMSProp

Fixes AdaGrad's shrinking rate by using a moving average of squared gradients instead of a sum:

divide the gradient by a recent average of its magnitude

Keeps a healthy step size — good for non-stationary deep-learning objectives.

9. Adam — the default optimizer

Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) = momentum + RMSProp. It tracks two moving averages:

mₜ = avg of gradients          (1st moment — momentum)
vₜ = avg of squared gradients  (2nd moment — adaptive rate)
then uses bias-corrected mₜ, vₜ to update

Fast, robust, little tuning — the usual first choice in deep learning.

11. Newton's method & the Hessian

A second-order method using curvature (the Hessian H of second derivatives):

θ := θ − H⁻¹ ∇J

Can converge in far fewer steps, but computing/inverting H is expensive for large models — so first-order methods (SGD/Adam) dominate deep learning. Works well on small convex problems (e.g. logistic regression).

12. Convex vs non-convex 
convex loss (linear/logistic regression, SVM): one global minimum — GD finds it
non-convex loss (neural nets): many local minima/saddle points

SGD's noise + momentum help escape poor spots; we settle for a "good enough" minimum.

13. Gradient checking

To verify a hand-coded backprop, compare the analytical gradient with a numerical one:

∂L/∂θ ≈ [ L(θ+ε) − L(θ−ε) ] / 2ε

They should match closely. It's a debugging tool only — too slow for real training.

14. Common GATE traps
  • GD moves along the gradient? No — opposite it (to descend).
  • Bigger learning rate is always faster? No — too big diverges.
  • Adam is plain gradient descent? No — adaptive rate + moment estimates.
  • SGD uses the whole dataset per step? No — one sample / mini-batch.

6 Main formula

Update rule — step downhill, opposite the gradient:

$$ x_{k+1} = x_k - \eta\,\nabla f(x_k) $$

In one variable this is $x_{k+1}=x_k-\eta\,f'(x_k)$; repeat until the gradient is tiny (converged).

Stochastic gradient descent (SGD) — use one sample (or a mini-batch) per step:

$$ x_{k+1}=x_k-\eta\,\nabla f_i(x_k) \quad (i \text{ a random sample}) $$

Momentum — carry a velocity term to accelerate and smooth the descent:

$$ v_{k+1}=\gamma v_k+\eta\nabla f(x_k),\qquad x_{k+1}=x_k-v_{k+1} $$
7 Step-by-step method
  1. Pick a starting point $x_0$ and a learning rate $\eta$.
  2. Compute the gradient $\nabla f(x_k)$.
  3. Update: $x_{k+1}=x_k-\eta\,\nabla f(x_k)$.
  4. Repeat until $\nabla f\approx0$ (or $f$ stops decreasing).
8 Worked example
✦ Minimize $f(x)=(x-3)^2$, $\eta=0.1$, start $x_0=0$

$f'(x)=2(x-3)$. Step 1: $x_1=0-0.1\cdot(2)(0-3)=0-0.1(-6)=0.6$.

Step 2: $x_2=0.6-0.1\cdot2(0.6-3)=0.6+0.48=1.08$. Each step moves toward $x^*=3$.

✦ One gradient-descent step

Minimize $f(x)=x^2$, so $f'(x)=2x$. Start at $x_0=5$ with $\eta=0.1$.

$x_1=5-0.1(2\cdot5)=4$;   $x_2=4-0.1(8)=3.2$;   $x_3=3.2-0.1(6.4)=2.56$.

Each step multiplies $x$ by $(1-2\eta)=0.8$, so the iterates shrink geometrically toward the minimum $x^*=0$.

9 GATE cheat sheet
⚡ High-value facts
  • Update rule: $x_{k+1}=x_k-\eta\nabla f(x_k)$ — always minus the gradient.
  • Too-small $\eta$ → slow; too-large $\eta$ → overshoot / diverge.
  • On a convex function it converges to the global minimum (with a suitable $\eta$).
  • On a non-convex function it can get stuck in a local minimum (start matters).
  • At a minimum the gradient is $0$, so the update stops moving.
10 Common traps
✕ Watch out

Trap: "Move in the $+\nabla f$ direction." Reality: that's uphill; descent uses $-\nabla f$.

Trap: "Bigger learning rate is always faster." Reality: too big overshoots and can diverge.

Trap: "Gradient descent always finds the global minimum." Reality: only guaranteed for convex $f$; otherwise it may stop at a local min.

11 Connections with other topics
  • Univariate/multivariate: GD is the iterative way to reach $f'(x)=0$ / $\nabla f=0$.
  • Types of optimization: convex vs non-convex decides whether GD finds the global min.
  • Linear algebra: the gradient is a vector; the step direction is $-\nabla f$.
  • Machine learning: training = gradient descent on a loss function.
12 Practice problems

Q1. The update rule for gradient descent is?

$x_{k+1}=x_k-\eta\,\nabla f(x_k)$.

Q2. $f(x)=x^2$, $\eta=0.1$, $x_0=5$. Find $x_1$.

$f'=2x$; $x_1=5-0.1(10)=4$.

Q3. What happens if the learning rate is far too large?

The steps overshoot the minimum and the value can diverge (grow).

Q4. On a non-convex function, what can gradient descent get stuck in?

A local minimum (not necessarily the global one).

That's the uphill (ascent) direction.
